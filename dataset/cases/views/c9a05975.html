**The beginning of the storm**

When Mr. Aidan Lewis first came to the outpatient clinic on a gray Tuesday, he was already feeling more tired than usual. He is a 65‑year‑old retired electrical engineer who has lived in the rural Midwest for most of his life. The only thing he could pin his discomfort on was a vague ache in his right upper quadrant that didn’t respond to over‑the‑counter painkillers. “I’ve been feeling a little drained, and I’ve noticed my skin looks a bit yellow,” he told the nurse, noting that he had lost about 10 lb in the past three months because his appetite had dwindled. His wife had observed him getting progressively bloated and having difficulty speaking clearly, but he had considered it part of “getting older.”

He was a lifelong smoker, had a 40‑pack‑year history, and drank about two to three standard alcoholic beverages a day for the last 20 years, often as a way to settle after work. He grew up on a farm that occasionally stored grains, and his parents had reported sporadic consumption of improperly dried corn, a known source of aflatoxin B1. He was also obese (BMI 32) with a history of type 2 diabetes mellitus, hypertension, and hyperlipidemia, managed on metformin, lisinopril, and atorvastatin. His primary doctor had told him a decade ago that he was “at risk” for liver disease, but he had never followed up on the screening.

When Aidan was seen, the physical exam was notable for scleral icterus, mild ascites on abdominal palpation, and a soft, non‑tender liver edge that was just barely palpable 1 cm below the right costal margin. There were no signs of chronic heart failure, but his blood pressure was 155/88 mmHg. A quick point‑of‑care INR came back 1.4. The lab panel ordered included a complete blood count, comprehensive metabolic panel, hepatitis serology panel, ferritin, transferrin saturation, and alpha‑fetoprotein (AFP). His hepatitis B surface antigen was positive and hepatitis C antibody was positive as well. Both HBsAg and HCV‑RNA were detectable, indicating chronic infection. Serum ferritin was 480 ng/mL (normal 20–200 ng/mL for men) and transferrin saturation was 60 % (normal 15–45 %)—congruent with iron overload from an HFE gene mutation, something he had recently learned about from a genetic testing initiative at his local health system.

The key lab from that first visit was an AFP of 45 ng/mL (normal <10). That figure, combined with the imaging that would follow, began the arc of his disease.

---

### **Week 2 – “Let’s look at the liver”**

Two weeks later the primary physician had a low‑threshold for imaging. A portal‑phase abdominal ultrasound was ordered with the request: “Rule out a focal hepatic lesion.” The scan revealed a 3.5 cm hypoechoic lesion in segment 6 of the liver, with a surrounding zone of heterogeneity that was suspicious for early cirrhosis. No gallbladder stones or obvious portal hypertension signs, though there was moderate hepatic steatosis.

Given the lesion’s size, the ultrasound technician suggested a contrast‑enhanced CT to better characterize the lesion. The patient was referred to the tertiary hospital’s radiology department. He was seen by hepatology the following week; he described more pronounced lightheadedness, episodes of brief, vague black stools, and a sense that his voice had become hoarse.

On evaluation the hepatologist—Dr. Khan—confirmed cirrhosis clinically and with transient elastography showing an elastography value of 23 kPa. His Child‑Pugh score was B (3 points for bilirubin 1.8 mg/dL, 3 points for albumin 2.9 g/dL, and 3 points for INR 1.4) classifying him as a moderate‑to‑high risk for hepatic decompensation.

A percutaneous liver biopsy was performed to confirm the suspicion of HCC. The histology showed malignant hepatocytes in a trabecular pattern with fibrous septa, confirming hepatocellular carcinoma. A liver‑stiffening score of 23 kPa and the biopsy findings satisfied the 2018 AASLD guidelines for treating HCC in the setting of cirrhosis.

AFP rose to 190 ng/mL at the time of biopsy—an ominous trend but still under the 400 ng/mL threshold that sometimes prompts a more aggressive approach.

---

### **Month 1 – Treatment deliberation**

Dr. Khan convened a multidisciplinary tumor board. The patient’s comorbidities—alcoholic liver disease, chronic hepatitis B and C, metabolic syndrome, obesity, type 2 diabetes, and iron overload—placed him at high surgical risk. The board decided that a minimally invasive approach, transarterial chemoembolization (TACE), was the preferred first‑line intervention. Liver transplantation was ruled out because of his active hepatitis B and C infections and the fact he had not been on antiviral therapy for any significant period.

A plan was drafted: TACE using doxorubicin and emulsion, followed by a reassessment in six weeks for residual tumor burden. In addition, Aidan was enrolled in a low‑dose sorafenib pilot to address microvascular invasion risk, though the hepatologist cautioned that sorafenib’s hepatotoxicity and side‑effects might be intolerable here.

A dietitian was added to the team; the goal was a 10 % weight reduction, a carbohydrate‑controlled meal plan, and vitamin supplementation to address hepatic encephalopathy and liver failure risk.

---

### **Month 3 – First TACE and fallout**

The first TACE was technically successful. Post‑procedure CT showed selective embolization of the tumor’s feeding artery. Aidan reported a fleeting sense of warmth in his abdomen and a mild post‑embolization syndrome—fever, pain, nausea—but by day 4 he was back to his baseline.

However, in the ensuing weeks the patient’s ascites worsened. A repeat ultrasound showed a new moderate‑to‑large ascitic fluid collection. A diuretic regime of spironolactone 100 mg daily plus furosemide 20 mg daily was started. At the 6‑week reassessment he complained of worsening fatigue, difficulty speaking, and a “cloudy” mental state. He had a transient episode of confusion at home that required urgent evaluation for hepatic encephalopathy.

The AFP had climbed to 460 ng/mL, and the re‑imaging showed a residual 2.8 cm lesion—larger than expected, indicating tumor progression in spite of embolization. Dr. Khan convened a second tumor board: TACE only had not succeeded; systemic therapy was the next step.

Aidan was started on sorafenib 400 mg BID. Within a month he developed hand‑foot skin reactions and a mild increase in transaminases (AST 85 U/L, ALT 70 U/L). Sorafenib dose was titrated down but the liver enzymes rose to AST 120 U/L and ALT 105 U/L. The hepatologist debated holding sorafenib until a stable trough level was achieved but Aidan’s performance status was already declining. He was also placed on a low‑dose prednisone taper to blunt the inflammatory response to sorafenib, but this again complicated his diabetes control.

---

### **Month 6 – Escalation, variceal bleed, and a grim turn**

At six months, his Child‑Pugh class worsened to C (4 points for bilirubin 2.5 mg/dL, 4 points for albumin 2.5 g/dL, 5 points for INR 1.7). Portal hypertension was frank: hepatic venous pressure gradient (HVPG) measured at 18 mmHg, confirming grade 3 portal hypertension. Upper‑endoscopy uncovered large esophageal varices with red‑sign stigmata. Endoscopic variceal ligation was performed with four bands placed, but he had persistent portal hypertension, evidenced by recurrent ascites and new‐onset hepatorenal syndrome (serum creatinine rose from 0.8 to 1.5 mg/dL over three weeks).

The patient reported that he had become increasingly frustrated with his “sudden” body changes. He refused to take any more chemotherapy because it made him nauseated, and after a family discussion Dr. Khan brought in a palliative care consult. Aidan’s advance directive was consulted, and the team confirmed he was open to discussions about goals of care.

Sorafenib was discontinued. A more aggressive diuretic (adding torsemide 20 mg BID) was attempted but could not control the ascites. A peritoneovenous shunt was considered but deemed inappropriate because of his cirrhosis and poor liver synthetic function; a percutaneous paracentesis (large‑volume) relieved abdominal distension temporarily, but this repeated procedure was unsustainable.

A diagnosis of refractory portal hypertension and HCC progression set the final trajectory: a diminishing hepatic reserve, increasing tumor burden, and systemic complications from the underlying metabolic derangements and iron overload.

---

### **Month 9 – Final days and the inevitable**

Nine months after the first clinic visit, Aidan’s condition had deteriorated dramatically. His liver enzymes were now AST 210 U/L, ALT 180 U/L; bilirubin 4.3 mg/dL; albumin had fallen to 1.8 g/dL. His ascites became tense, and on a routine visit he fell and had a mild head injury. His INR had climbed to 2.5, indicating severe coagulopathy. He complained of constant abdominal pain, extreme fatigue, and a “yellow‑tinged” dreamlike state. An urgent CT scan revealed an 4.2 cm HCC with evidence of vascular invasion into the portal vein branch.

His wife and daughter were in tears as he, still lucid enough to speak, requested a calm and dignified end. The palliative team arranged hospice. The final week was spent in the home: Aidan was given only a small palliative dose of morphine for pain control and no longer tolerated oral intake. He survived just over a month from the first sign of trouble, his course reflecting a rapid escalation from subclinical hepatic dysfunction to frank hepatic decompensation, tumor progression, and multi‑system failure.

Through the six‑month arc of care, Mr. Lewis faced a complex interplay of chronic hepatitis B and C, cirrhosis, metabolic syndrome, and iron overload—all of which compounded each other and accelerated hepatocarcinogenesis. Each clinical decision, from imaging to TACE, to sorafenib, to palliative care, reflected a balance between attempting oncologic control and managing the liver’s mounting fragility. The narrative of his disease course underscores the brutal synergy of chronic liver injury, metabolic derangements, and oncogenic exposures that can push a patient toward an inexorable decline.
