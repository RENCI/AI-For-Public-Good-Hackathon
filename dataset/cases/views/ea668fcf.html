When Marjorie B. felt her right knee buckle on the third step of the stairwell at the grocery store, it was simply painful enough to warrant a pause. She was a 65‑year‑old retired schoolteacher who had long carried the cumulative weight of a 300‑lb frame, a hypertension diagnosis almost a decade earlier and two years of insulin‑managed type 2 diabetes. She also lived with a history of coronary artery disease that had required percutaneous coronary intervention four years ago. These chronic burdens had all, in one sense, shaped her daily life.

---

### Day 1 – First Red Flag

Morning, 8 a.m.: After a 30‑minute brisk walk to the market, Marjorie noticed sudden, shooting pain radiating from the medial aspect of her knee into the inner thigh. A brief period of stiffness persisted for the next half‑hour, but the pain intensified dramatically when she tried to stand up from a chair. She decided to go home, applied a cold pack, and rested the joint for a few hours. However, by late afternoon she realized that moving her knee in a straight‑line gait was no longer painless.

The next day, while preparing dinner, Marjorie's right lower leg seemed “loose” – she felt her foot slip slightly when walking. She scheduled an appointment with Dr. Patel, her primary care physician, within the week (Day 7).

---

### Week 1 – Primary Care Assessment

**Visit at 10 a.m.** Dr. Patel began with a focused history. Marjorie reported sudden onset of knee pain following routine activity, described it as a “sharp, tearing sensation,” and noted worsening pain with weight‑bearing and climbing stairs. She denied any recent fall, recent high‑impact activity, or new injuries. She did recall a sprain in her right knee during a childhood gymnastics class, and her mother had a history of osteoarthritis.

Review of systems was otherwise unremarkable aside from the usual high‑blood‑pressure headaches and a mild fatigue from her diabetes. Past surgical history was clear: angioplasty of the left anterior descending artery, no knee surgeries.

Physical exam findings: - Right knee deformity modestly flexed. - Posterior, medial, and lateral joint lines tender to palpation. - Range of motion: 0‑110° (normal 0‑140°). - Varus alignment noted, but no swelling or effusion. - No open lesions, no redness.

Dr. Patel noted that the sudden flare was evocative of an osteoarthritis (OA) flare but recognized her weight, gender, age, genetic predisposition, and prior knee injury as compounding risk factors. He also stressed the importance of minimizing joint stress to protect her cardiovascular health.

**Investigations ordered:** - Plain radiographs AP and lateral of both knees (to assess joint space narrowing, osteophytes). - Baseline labs: CBC, CMP, fasting glucose, HbA1c, lipid panel, and ESR/CRP for baseline inflammatory markers.

He prescribed acetaminophen 500 mg every 6 hours as needed for pain, and advised a topical diclofenac gel for the knee applied at bedtime. A short course of a non‑steroidal anti‑inflammatory (NSAID) was deemed risky considering her hypertension, diabetes, and history of coronary artery disease; thus, he avoided systemic NSAIDs at first.

**Management plan discussed:** - Weight loss: refer to a nutritionist specializing in diabetic weight reduction. - Physical therapy: gait training, isometric quadriceps strengthening, and gentle range‑of‑motion exercises. - Education on activity modification. - Blood pressure and glucose monitoring were reinforced, with a note to keep a joint‑pain diary.

Dr. Patel scheduled a follow‑up in two weeks, noting that the radiographs would be ready by then. She also emphasized the need for early, consistent management to avoid chronic pain and loss of function.

---

### Week 3 – Radiographs & PT Referral

Two weeks later, Marjorie returned with her radiographs. The AP view showed 3‑mm medial joint space narrowing with a few small osteophytes in the medial tibial plateau. No cystic changes.

Based on findings, Dr. Patel confirmed osteoarthritis of the right knee. He was reassured that the sudden severity of the flare was consistent with an acute exacerbation, not a new underlying pathology.

**Treatment update:** - Maintained acetaminophen regimen with caution regarding hepatotoxicity in the context of diabetes. - Started a structured home exercise plan with a physical therapist: daily gentle quad sets, heel raises, and static walking over 30 seconds with crutches for support as needed. - Reintroduced an oral NSAID after discussing aspirin therapy and anti‑platelet agents: given her cardiovascular history, Dr. Patel preferred ibuprofen 200 mg BID, but only if tolerated, noting the renal, cardiovascular, and blood pressure risks. He provided a written plan limiting NSAID use to the “least dose, least duration” principle, and he urged prompt reporting of any increased swelling or blood pressure spikes.

- Adjusted antihypertensive regimen: switched lisinopril 10 mg daily to a potassium‑sparing diuretic to mitigate NSAID-induced edema. - Rebalanced her diabetic medications: added metformin 500 mg nightly, emphasizing glycemic control to improve tissue repair.

Marjorie reported a noticeable improvement in pain: the sharp sting had mellowed into a dull ache, more evident in the afternoon. The knee seemed less “loose” when walking. She also appreciated the physical therapy guidance, feeling empowered to manage her flare at home.

---

### Month 1 – Progressive Symptom Decline

Over the next month, Marjorie adhered to the exercise regimen and maintained a modest caloric deficit of 500 kcal per day, losing about 8 pounds. Her fasting glucose dipped from 154 mg/dL to 129 mg/dL, and her HbA1c improved from 8.2% to 7.6%. Blood pressure readings in home checks hovered between 135–140/80–85 mm Hg.

**Follow‑up visit (Month 1):** - Pain was now manageable with occasional use of acetaminophen. - Range of motion increased to 0‑120°. - No swelling, no effusion. - Dr. Patel noted a mild, persistent stiffness in the morning but no exacerbation.

He adjusted her medication: discontinued ibuprofen owing to negligible benefit; prescribed a topical diclofenac gel for any flare-ups. He added a low‑dose duloxetine (30 mg nightly) as a disease‑modifying analgesic for osteoarthritis pain, citing its neuropathic component and dual benefit for depressive symptoms she occasionally reported.

Marjorie's weight continued to decline, and her cardiovascular risk profile improved. A repeat HbA1c after three months confirmed a further drop to 7.2%. Her cholesterol was also stable, reducing the need for additional statin therapy.

---

### Month 3 – Sustained Improvement and Transition to Maintenance

At the three‑month check‑in, Marjorie’s right knee was functional enough that she was climbing stairs without the crutches. The pain was described as “low‑grade ache” that flared only after prolonged standing. Dr. Patel recommended:

- Continuing home exercise and daily walking routine. - Low‑dose duloxetine tapered to 20 mg nightly if she found adequate relief without side‑effects. - Re‑evaluation of her weight‑loss plan: he suggested a continued diet adjustment, noting that an additional 60 pounds loss would further reduce OA severity. - Cardiovascular follow‑up: a cardiology consult recommended yearly echocardiogram but emphasized lifestyle modification over interventions.

The clinical picture resolved from an acute flare to a state of improving function. Marjorie’s subjective reports aligned with objective improvements: she now engaged in her granddaughter’s outdoor plays without experiencing the fear of pain that had dominated the prior weeks.
