**Clinical Narrative – Acute Gout Attack in a 58‑year‑old Male**

A 58‑year‑old African‑American man named Marcus presented with what, from the outset, was a textbook picture of an acute gout flare. Marcus was a retired factory supervisor, 6’1”, weighing 280 lb (BMI 38), who had long struggled with metabolic syndrome, hypertension, type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM) and chronic kidney disease (CKD) stage 3a (eGFR 45 mL/min/1.73 m²). He was a chronic beer‑drinker—two bottles a day in the evenings—and ate a diet heavy in red meat, organ meats, and shellfish. His medication list was dense: metformin 1000 mg BID for diabetes, losartan 50 mg daily for hypertension, hydrochlorothiazide 25 mg daily (an additional diuretic that magnified his urate retention), and a short‑term prednisone taper for his skin rash last year. Family history was significant for gout in his father.

---

### **Day 0 – The Onset**

Marcus woke at 6 am feeling “off” and noticed a sudden, throbbing pain in the big toe of his left foot, accompanied by swelling and an intense heat that made the toe feel unusually hot to the touch. He described the pain as “sharp, ripping,” with a quality that improved only when the toe was rested. The pain escalated to a 4/10 at rest and 9/10 at night, interfering with movement and sleep. The patient had no prior episodes of severe joint pain, but he had been told at a previous clinic that his uric acid was “high” and that he “should watch his food and drink.”

Marcus called his primary‑care physician the same evening. The phone consultation highlighted his high purine diet, regular alcohol use, and known CKD; the doctor advised an urgent office visit tomorrow. Marcus slept poorly, still feeling the swelling in his toe but relieved that it was not a fracture.

---

### **Day 1 – Office Visit**

Marcus arrived at the outpatient clinic at 10 am, the left foot visibly swollen and erythematous. A tender and warm metatarsophalangeal joint with minimal range of motion was noted. A quick bedside evaluation suggested an acute inflammatory process; however, the differential included septic arthritis, especially given his CKD and diabetes.

A point‑of‑care urinalysis was performed, showing sterile urine with no leukocytes. Standard labs were drawn:

- Serum uric acid: 9.8 mg/dL - Serum creatinine: 1.8 mg/dL (eGFR 45) - HbA1c: 8.2% - CRP: 18 mg/L (elevated) - WBC: 9,500/µL (normal)

The doctor suspected a gout flare, confirmed by the classic tophus‑free but painful big toe. A mild to moderate risk of renal impairment was considered, so the plan was to avoid NSAIDs that could worsen kidney function unless absolutely necessary.

**Treatment Decision** - **Colchicine:** 0.5 mg at onset, then 0.5 mg 6 h later (max 1 mg today). - **Low‑dose NSAID alternative:** Since renal function was borderline, a single 50 mg dose of ibuprofen was considered but ultimately avoided in favor of a short course of oral prednisone (10 mg once daily for 3 days to control pain rapidly). - **Steroid injection:** The physician offered intra‑articular corticosteroid injection; Marcus declined, preferring oral therapy.

**Lifestyle Counseling** - Reduce purine intake (eliminate organ meats, high‑fat dairy, shellfish). - Cut back on beer to one bottle on weekends only. - Fluid intake: at least 2.5 L/day. - Weight‑loss goal of 10% over 6 months. - Consider switching hydrochlorothiazide to a potassium‑sparing diuretic or ACE inhibitor.

Marcus left the office with a clear prescription, a dose‑structured colchicine schedule, and a short‑term prednisone taper. He was advised to return in 48 hours if pain failed to improve or worsened.

---

### **Day 3 – Follow‑up Call**

At 10 am, the clinic nurse called Marcus to check pain status. He reported that the pain had improved to 4/10, and the swelling had visibly decreased. He was taking all prescribed medications. The nurse reminded him about the upcoming in‑person appointment at the office scheduled for Day 7.

---

### **Day 7 – Clinic Revisit**

Marcus came in for a check‑in. His big toe was no longer painful; there was slight residual warmth but no swelling. Tenderness was minimal. He was symptom‑free for the last 48 h, and pain at rest was 0/10.

The clinician noted that the rapid response was consistent with the use of colchicine plus a short prednisone course. A plan was set to start chronic management to prevent future flares, taking into account his CKD:

- **Urate‑lowering therapy:** Initiate allopurinol at 50 mg daily, titrating up by 50 mg every four weeks depending on liver and kidney function. - **Kidney‑adjusted dosing of allopurinol**: Since creatinine was 1.8 mg/dL, a conservative starting dose of 50 mg was chosen. - **Discontinue hydrochlorothiazide**: Switch to amlodipine 5 mg daily for hypertension without urate‑retaining side effects. - **Continue losartan** 50 mg daily for renal protection. - **Regular monitoring**: Serum uric acid, creatinine, electrolytes every 3 months.

Marcus was also encouraged to join a low‑purine cooking class offered by the clinic and to start a basic weight‑loss exercise program (walking 30 minutes daily, monitoring glucose).

---

### **Weeks 2‑4 – Follow‑up Visits**

- **Week 2 (Day 14):** Marcus reported no pain, though he felt a bit stiff after walking. Labs: serum uric acid 6.9 mg/dL, creatinine 1.8 mg/dL. Allopurinol 100 mg daily, tolerated well. - **Week 4 (Day 28):** Serum uric acid down to 6.4 mg/dL, creatinine unchanged, HbA1c marginally improved to 7.9%. No recurrent flare. Lifestyle changes included cutting beetroot, eggs, and processed meats.

---

### **Month 3 – Long‑Term Check**

At the three‑month office visit, Marcus was weight‑stable (±0.5 kg), still taking allopurinol 150 mg daily, and reported no gout attacks. Lab results were encouraging: uric acid 5.8 mg/dL, fasting glucose 120 mg/dL, HbA1c 7.5%, eGFR 44 mL/min/1.73 m². The physician reaffirmed the importance of diet, exercise, and medication adherence. A referral to a dietitian was placed to fine‑tune his low‑purine meal plan.

---

### **Outcome**

Marcus’s acute gout attack resolved rapidly with the combination of colchicine, a short steroid course, and judicious avoidance of NSAIDs. The chronic strategy of allopurinol, diuretic switch, and lifestyle modifications stabilized his serum uric acid and prevented recurrence. Over 12 months of follow‑up, he remained flare‑free, achieved a modest weight loss, and improved glycemic control. His CKD remained stable, and he was continuing to meet the clinical targets for hypertension and metabolic syndrome. The narrative concludes with Marcus feeling confident, having turned a sudden, debilitating pain episode into an opportunity to overhaul his health habits and reduce his risk of future gout attacks.
