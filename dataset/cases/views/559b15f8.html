When Michael’s mother—who had just been told she would be undergoing her second appendectomy—told him that she was “sitting on a hot potato inside her belly” and worried about a “slow‑to‑appear stomach flare‑up,” he laughed, a little too quickly. He was 28, a software engineer, a smoker who liked his food “in the cloud” and only ate a sandwich or a pizza with a side of beer most nights. He was 28, had type 2 diabetes for a year on metformin, high blood pressure controlled with lisinopril, and a BMI of 33. On the night of that call, he felt a flicker of discomfort in his lower right abdomen, a dull ache that he shrugged off and attributed to the late‑night gaming marathon.

**Day 0 – First Flicker of Pain** Michael woke at 1 a.m. with a light nausea and a vague gnawing in what he later described as “the middle of my belly.” He had just finished a mild upper‑respiratory viral illness two weeks earlier, with a lingering low‑grade fever and sore throat that had resolved. The night before, he’d taken an over‑the‑counter antacid for an indigestion that never fully stopped. He told himself that perhaps he’d overindulged on the midnight snack—an extra cheese pizza. At 6 a.m., the pain tightened, becoming more localized just beneath his right rib cage.

**Day 1 – The Pain Migrates** By midmorning, the pain had shifted to the right lower quadrant (RLQ). It was sharp now, especially when he twisted or walked. He noticed occasional fever spikes up to 99.4°F, a cough that had turned slightly productive, and a feeling of tiredness that still lingered from the viral illness. He avoided taking NSAIDs because of his type 2 diabetes; his doctor had warned him against ibuprofen. Michael posted on a friend’s group chat, half in jest, “Do I need to worry? Or is this just another stomach bug?” The most helpful comment, from a fellow diabetic, was a cautious reminder that his diabetes could blunt his pain perception, and to seek care if symptoms persisted.

**Day 2 – Primary Care Visit** At 9 a.m., Michael walked into the outpatient clinic. He was greeted by the nurse who weighed him (195 lbs) and noted his blood pressure (142/86 mmHg). After a quick phone check with his health plan, he sat down for history. The office physician, Dr. Patel, noted the family history of appendicitis in his father, who had had a perforated appendix at 29. “That’s a significant detail,” Dr. Patel said. Michael described the RLQ pain as “burning, sharp at the start, but now pretty constant.” He mentioned a small episode of vomiting and low‑grade fever.

Differential diagnosis: viral gastroenteritis, mesenteric adenitis, ectopic pregnancy (unlikely, but not ruled out), gastric ulcer, and, of course, appendicitis. Dr. Patel ordered a basic labs panel (CBC, CMP, lipase) and a bedside ultrasound. The ultrasound was inconclusive due to Michael’s thick subcutaneous tissue, but it did show a small bowel loop with mild wall thickening. His white blood count came back at 12,500/µL with a left shift (neutrophils 85%). Blood glucose was 159 mg/dL; HbA1c had been 6.9% two months prior. The physician ordered a low‑dose CT scan of the abdomen and pelvis with contrast, citing “pain that shifted to the RLQ and leukocytosis.”

By the time the imaging results were available, it was 4 p.m. Michael’s pain had worsened, now described as “a crushing sensation” that made him “stand like he’s about to fall.” The CT scan came back at 4:45 p.m.—the appendix was dilated, 12 mm, with wall thickening, peri‑appendiceal fat stranding, and a small, non‑ruptured abscess. It was classic acute appendicitis. Dr. Patel called the surgical team.

**Day 3 – Overnight Observation & Surgical Decision** Michael was admitted to the surgical ward. Surgeons discussed a laparoscopic appendectomy. Because of his history of diabetes and hypertension, they wanted to optimize his pre‑operative glucose (target fasting <130 mg/dL) and blood pressure (<140/90 mmHg) to reduce surgical risk. Michael’s blood sugar ranged from 170 to 210 mg/dL in the first 6 hours, so the endocrinology consult was called in, and his insulin sliding scale was adjusted.

He also reported a moderate amount of cough and shortness of breath, but his oxygen saturation remained above 95% on room air. The chest X-ray showed a mild infiltrate, likely post‑viral. The pulmonologist noted that his mild smoking history (1 pack per day for 6 years) could worsen postoperative pulmonary complications but that with cautious peri‑operative pulmonary hygiene, these would be manageable.

At 11 a.m., the surgical team deemed him a good candidate for laparoscopic appendectomy. Michael's pre‑operative assessment was routine: INR 1.0, platelets 200k, normal electrolytes. After a standard bowel prep with polyethylene glycol (PPS), he was taken to the OR.

**Day 3 – Laparoscopic Appendectomy** At 1:30 p.m., the surgeon made three small incisions and inserted the camera and instruments. The appendix was inflamed but not perforated. It was successfully removed, and a 12‑x‑6 mm abscess cavity was drained and irrigated. The surgeon confirmed no other intra‑abdominal pathology. Michael’s operative time was just under 45 minutes. No drains or tubes were placed. Blood loss was minimal, and he was transferred to the post‑anesthesia care unit (PACU).

**Day 3–4 – Post‑operative Course** In the PACU, Michael slept through the first night. He tolerated a clear liquid diet at 6 a.m. the next morning. At 8 a.m., he reported some mild abdominal tenderness, particularly at the incision sites, but no guarding. His vitals were stable: BP 130/78 mmHg, HR 78, temp 99.0°F. A post‑op CBC returned with WBC 9,500/µL and neutrophils 70%. Blood glucose was 146 mg/dL, controlled on basal insulin and a sliding scale.

He was monitored on the floor for 48 hours. At 2 p.m. Day 4, he had a mild fever spike to 100.2°F and complained of mild left lower chest tightness. A chest X-ray at that time was unchanged. An early pulmonary physiotherapy session, incentive spirometry, and deep breathing exercises were instituted. The pulmonologist noted that the mild infiltrate likely represented a viral pneumonia that was resolving; no antibiotics were started as cultures remained negative.

**Day 5 – Transition to Discharge** By Day 5, Michael’s pain had subsided to a dull ache at the incision sites, controlled by acetaminophen. He tolerated a light diet—scrambled eggs, toast, and fruit. There were no signs of infection at the surgical sites; the incisions were warm but not erythematous, and the drainage output had been minimal.

He received a brief discharge teaching session, emphasizing glycemic control (carbohydrate counting, insulin adjustment), wound care, signs of infection or delayed healing, and follow‑up with his primary care provider in one week. He was instructed to return immediately if he experienced fever, worsening pain, or foul drainage.

**Day 6–10 – Home Recovery** At home, he complied with the insulin regimens and glucose monitoring. His last meal of the night was a quick pizza, but he recognized a pattern: large carbohydrate loads followed by a glucose spike; he shifted to a low‑fiber, high protein snack, and his glucose readings dropped to the mid‑80s.

He had a minor rash at his incision sites on Day 7, which resolved with over‑the‑counter antihistamine. His cough lingered slightly longer than the viral illness, but on Day 9, the cough resolved, and his chest felt normal. He started walking again on Day 8, a short 10‑minute stroll. Dr. Patel scheduled a routine follow‑up for Day 14, but Michael decided to come in on Day 11 to discuss a re-check of his blood sugar and to confirm wound healing.

**Day 11 – Follow‑Up** Michael’s incision sites were healing well; no redness or drainage. His blood sugar readings averaged 120 mg/dL over the prior week. He was congratulated by Dr. Patel on the resolution of appendicitis, advised on weight loss strategies, and referred to a nutritional counselor to reduce his risk of future gastrointestinal complications.

**Outcome** Michael’s appendicitis resolved after a timely laparoscopic appendectomy. The only lingering impact was the need to adapt his diet for better glucose control and to reduce his sedentary lifestyle. No postoperative complications occurred, and his type 2 diabetes remained stable. By the end of the second week, he was back to his 9‑to‑5 office, still a smoker, but with a newfound appreciation for monitoring his abdominal pain and seeking care promptly.

The timeline of visits—primary care on Day 2, imaging on Day 2 evening, surgery on Day 3, postoperative monitoring Days 3–4, discharge Day 5, home recovery Days 6–10, and follow‑up Day 11—provides a clear, realistic progression for an acute appendicitis case in a patient with comorbidities and risk factors.
