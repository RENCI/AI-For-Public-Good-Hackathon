**Clinical Narrative**

For Sarah Patel, a thirty‑year‑old woman who had just delivered a healthy infant six weeks earlier, the postpartum period began with a mixture of relief and residual exhaustion. Two days after delivery, she noted a faint, erythematous rash on the bridge of her nose that had appeared less than a day before. It was barely discernible, a mere pink blot, and she attributed it to the usual “strawberry” rash that can accompany a bout of viral infection. That very evening she developed a mild, low‑grade fever (37.8 °C) and a dull ache in her knees that she shrugged off as typical post‑partum soreness.

**Day 5 after Delivery (Post‑partum Day 5)** Sarah’s obstetrics office visited her for a routine check‑up. She reported “just a little soreness” and the faint rash on her cheeks. The OB nurse noted a BP of 142/92 mm Hg, a slight tachycardia (HR 92 bpm), and a normal heart and lung exam. The OB physician was relieved that Sarah’s hemoglobin was 3 weeks normal at 11.6 g/dL (down modestly from 12.3 g/dL at the time of delivery). Sarah was reassured that her mild fever was likely due to a recent upper‑respiratory infection she had caught a week earlier, and advised to rest and monitor for any worsening.

**Day 10 (Post‑partum Day 10)** Sarah’s fatigue increased. After an otherwise uneventful day of breastfeeding, she felt an unrelenting heaviness in her limbs, new arthralgias in both wrists and hips, and a diffuse erythema that had spread to her forearms. She also noticed dark‑colored spots on her inner thighs—a mild, new rash that she described as “strawberry” for the first time. Sarah visited her primary care provider (PCP), who listened to her lower respiratory tract symptoms (mild dry cough), inquired about any new infections, and noted a temperature of 38.1 °C on exam. The PCP did a quick complete blood count (CBC) and found a mildly low platelet count at 128 × 10³/µL, hemoglobin of 10.9 g/dL, and a white count of 8.9 × 10³/µL. Given her history of systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) and anti‑phospholipid syndrome (APS), the PCP ordered a basic autoimmune panel—anti‑dsDNA, complement C3/C4, and antiphospholipid antibodies.

**Day 12 (Post‑partum Day 12)** Sarah’s laboratory results returned. An elevated anti‑dsDNA at 5 IU/mL (normal <1) and markedly low C3 (48 mg/dL, normal 90–180) and C4 (12 mg/dL, normal 10–40) indicated hypocomplementemia. The antiphospholipid panel was positive for lupus anticoagulant and anticardiolipin IgG of 42 GPL units (normal < 15) with a borderline ANA titer of 1:320. Her renal function was borderline normal: creatinine 1.3 mg/dL (baseline 1.0 mg/dL), estimated GFR 73 mL/min/1.73 m². Urinalysis revealed mild proteinuria (1 g/d over 24‑hour collection) and trace blood. Sarah was now acutely symptomatic: she had a 1‑inch swelling in her left femoral lymph node, a persistent low‑grade fever, and diffuse arthralgia. Her BP had risen to 154/96 mm Hg.

Sarah was referred to the rheumatology clinic at the nearest tertiary center.

**Day 15 (Post‑partum Day 15)** – *Rheumatology Initial Evaluation* By the first rheumatology visit, Sarah’s symptoms had intensified. She complained of a “tired” feeling, a burning sensation in her wrists, and worsening rash that was no longer merely “pink” but had a subtle scaly, erythematous presentation across her cheeks and forearms. On physical exam, her joint counts were significant: a swollen tender count of 7 (wrist, knee, shoulder, hip). Her rash was described by the rheumatologist as a mild malar erythema with a faint “butter‑fluffy” component. She also had a mild edema on the lower extremities.

Her medical sheet highlighted her prior rapid steroid taper six months earlier after a flare that had required high‑dose methylprednisolone (1 g IV daily for 3 days followed by oral prednisone 30 mg, tapered off within 2 weeks). Her baseline comorbidities—CKD stage 2, hypertension (managed with amlodipine), and APS—were already on low‑dose aspirin.

The rheumatologist ordered repeat labs: CBC, CMP, anti‑dsDNA, complement, ANA, CRP, ESR, anti‑cardiolipin IgG/IgM, and lupus anticoagulant. She also ordered a renal ultrasound to evaluate for structural changes. Sarah agreed on a higher dose of prednisone (60 mg/day) and initiation of hydroxychloroquine (400 mg daily), but due to her prior rapid taper, the rheumatologist was uncertain whether a high oral dose alone would be sufficient. Nonetheless, the rheumatologist was unconvinced that Sarah was truly in a full flare, suspecting perhaps a sub‑clinical worsening or a medication side‑effect. She recommended close monitoring but no immediate biologic therapy.

**Week 3 (Post‑partum Day 21)** – *Initial Symptoms Worsening* Sarah’s back pain intensified, with a notable low‑back ache. She had become increasingly agitated, complaining of a sense of “fog” and difficulty concentrating. Her BP had spiked to 165/102 mm Hg despite being on amlodipine 5 mg and a short course of oral prednisone. An urgent CBC returned with a drop to 9.2 g/dL hemoglobin, thrombocytopenia 90 × 10³/µL (down from 128 × 10³/µL), and creatinine rising to 1.5 mg/dL. 24‑hour urine protein had climbed to 2.3 g. The ESR was 82 mm/h; CRP 18 mg/L, both markedly elevated.

She complained of a sudden throbbing headache, which did not relieve with ibuprofen. She was taken to the rheumatology clinic for an emergency reassessment.

**Day 24 (Post‑partum Day 24)** – *Urgent Evaluation* On day 24, Sarah’s symptoms had progressed to include new onset visual disturbances (blurred vision) and a mild, non‑pitting edema around her ankles (1 cm). On exam, her blood pressure was now 180/110 mm Hg; her creatinine 1.7 mg/dL. The riddle: she had no overt signs of a stroke, but she had a known APS. Her neurologist at the hospital evaluated her for potential ischemic event: CT head was negative, but MR angiography revealed a small high‑signal spot in the right parietal lobule. A Doppler ultrasound of lower extremities was positive for a non‑occlusive clot in the left common femoral vein with echogenic thrombus in the left popliteal vein but no overt deep venous thrombosis.

A renal biopsy was performed by nephrology (under conscious sedation) and revealed class III/IV proliferative lupus nephritis—endocapillary proliferation with wire‑loop lesions, mild interstitial fibrosis, and crescents in 4 of 19 glomeruli. This confirmed an active renal flare.

Given the simultaneous evidence of organ‑system involvement (vascular thrombosis, renal flare, hypertension, worsening ANA, high anti‑dsDNA, low complement), the rheumatology team concluded that Sarah was experiencing an acute, severe SLE flare in the backdrop of APS.

**Day 25–30—Intensive Care** Sarah was admitted to the rheumatology ward for high‑dose IV methylprednisolone 1 g/day for 3 days, followed by a more cautious steroid taper beginning at 60 mg/day and planned reduction to 40 mg/day after 10 days. She was started on mycophenolate mofetil (MMF) 500 mg BID as induction therapy for the proliferative lupus nephritis. Hydroxychloroquine was continued.

Because of her APS and recent thrombotic events, the hematology unit initiated full‑dose low‑molecular‑weight heparin (enoxaparin 1 mg/kg) and later transitioned her to warfarin with a target INR of 2–3, plus continued low‑dose aspirin.

During this time Sarah’s blood pressure required a triple‑agent regimen (amlodipine, lisinopril, and clonazepam for anxiety). Her creatinine rose to 1.9 mg/dL by day 5 of admission, and her urine protein to 4.2 g/24 h, indicating a rapidly progressive renal failure despite immunosuppression. Her platelet count fell to 65 × 10³/µL. A transfusion of platelets was required.

**Week 6 (Post‑partum Day 42)** Sarah’s flare persisted. The corticosteroid taper over the past month had been too brisk; her prednisone had been reduced to 15 mg by day 35, and the MMF dose remained at 500 mg BID due to her side effects (nausea, mild diarrhea). She had developed a fever up to 39 °C during a post‑op day on the 2nd week after admission. Cultures were negative, but a transesophageal echocardiogram was performed to rule out infective endocarditis; it was negative. She was empirically treated for presumed sepsis‑like syndrome with broad‑spectrum antibiotics for 7 days, but her fever resolved only after her corticosteroid dose was increased back to 20 mg daily and an additional 40 mg of methylprednisolone was given IV.

Despite aggressive therapy, her renal function remained suboptimal: creatinine 2.3 mg/dL, GFR 48 mL/min/1.73 m². The nephrologist suggested adding tacrolimus, but Sarah declined the calcineurin inhibitor due to concerns about neurotoxicity. She remained on MMF.

She also developed a new rash—more pronounced than before, with purple patches on her upper back, suggestive of cutaneous vasculitis. Cryoglobulins were negative, but she had low C4 and persisting anti‑dsDNA positivity (8 IU/mL).

**Month 3 (Post‑partum Day 90)** Sarah’s overall picture was one of persistent, severe disease activity: ongoing proteinuria (~3 g/day), hematuria, rising anti‑dsDNA titers, hypertension, reduced complement levels, and a history of thrombotic events (femoral vein thrombus, parietal brain infarct). The rheumatology team considered adding belimumab but opted for a more aggressive immunosuppressive approach: they increased MMF to 1.5 g twice daily and maintained a slow, cautious steroid taper (prednisone 20 mg/day). They continued warfarin.

Despite an attempt at an induction–maintenance strategy, Sarah’s disease did not improve. Her hemoglobin remained at ~9.5 g/dL, and her platelets hovered in the 80 × 10³/µL range. She was still dealing with fatigue, intermittent headaches, and mild confusion. The neurologist suspected a small, silent infarct burden, and a follow‑up MRI at day 90 confirmed another hyperintense focus in the left parietal white matter.

**Outcome—Worsening** By month four, Sarah’s renal function had declined to a creatinine of 3.4 mg/dL, a 200 % increase from baseline. She also developed chronic pain in her joints and new ocular manifestations (granulomatous episcleritis). Her disease activity score (SLEDAI) skyrocketed to 12 despite the high dose of corticosteroids, MMF, and low‑molecular‑weight heparin. At this point, the rheumatology group recognized that the flare was refractory: the combination of SLE, APS, and pre‑existing CKD had pushed Sarah into an irreversible disease trajectory.

She was counseled regarding the potential need for renal replacement therapy (hemodialysis) if her kidney function continued to deteriorate. She also expressed anxiety about her child’s health, her future fertility, and her overall quality of life. At the end of the fourth month, a nephrology‑oncology discussion considered the possibility of a kidney biopsy‑guided treatment change (b/c of active proliferative lesions). She remained on a high dose of prednisone 25 mg/day with MMF 1.5 g BID, warfarin, and a tight BP regimen.

For the remainder of her care, Sarah’s disease course would likely continue to worsen without a definitive remission, illustrating the peril of rapid steroid taper and the interplay of APS with SLE in a postpartum patient. The narrative captures her evolving symptoms, clinical decisions, and the gradual decompensation that ensued over three to four months.
