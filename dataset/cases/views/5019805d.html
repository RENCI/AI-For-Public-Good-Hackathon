**A Chronic, Unseen Fade**

Marina had always liked the rhythm of her life. A nurse by trade, she knew the signs her body was trying to tell her, but when the symptoms first began they seemed to belong to the “usual aches and pains of middle age.” The story begins in early 2010, when at the age of 30 Marina noticed that her menstrual period had become unusually heavy. Cranes and tampons left her with a dull, constant ache around her lower abdomen. Whenever she tried to take a brisk walk around the block, she felt a strange heaviness in her chest, a light‑headedness that faded a few minutes later. “Probably just hormonal,” she told her friend, and turned to the nearest pharmacy for a discreet bottle of spironolactone. A month later, she had a new habit: a glass of black coffee after breakfast, a snack of apples, a lunch of a quinoa and lentil salad, and a dinner of pasta with marinara sauce. She was a committed vegetarian, believing wholeheartedly that her diet was as healthy as it was colorful.

---

### 2010–2011: A Quiet Onset

The first few months of heavy bleeding were followed by a pallor that surprised Marina. She caught her reflection in a bathroom mirror and noticed her lips were a shade of mauve. She went to her general practitioner (GP) in late 2010. The GP performed a quick exam, noted her pallor, and ordered a basic complete blood count (CBC). The results were returned two weeks later: a hemoglobin of 10.8 g/dL, mean corpuscular volume (MCV) of 76 fL, and a red cell distribution width (RDW) slightly elevated. “A little low,” the GP said. “Iron deficiency is common in heavy bleeding.” She was prescribed ferrous sulfate 325 mg orally three times daily and asked to follow up in three months.

Marina dutifully took the pills for two months, yet the fatigue persisted. She had stopped noticing the faint, throbbing dizziness that surfaced whenever she stood up too quickly. “You probably have iron deficiency,” the GP thought, “but let’s give it a try.” She did not mention this again until 2011 when her doctor, after a routine eye exam, asked her why her vision sometimes seemed a bit hazy. On a brief review of systems, Marina described “dizziness when I rush from a standing position to walking upstairs." The GP ordered a retest of her CBC, which now showed hemoglobin 9.2 g/dL and MCV 72 fL. “We’re getting into low ranges,” the GP said, this time adding a ferritin draw. The ferritin returned at 3 ng/mL, confirming an iron stores deficiency.

---

### 2012: Celiac Disease

The low ferritin prompted the GP to refer Marina to a gastroenterologist. She had no overt abdominal symptoms at the time, aside from occasional bloating after meals, but her physician wanted to rule out any chronic gastrointestinal loss. A small intestinal biopsy was performed, revealing villous atrophy consistent with celiac disease. The gastroenterologist recommended a gluten‑free diet and started her on a low‑dose iron supplementation regimen: ferrous gluconate 200 mg once daily. Marina accepted the diagnosis with relief that it was “not a mystery” and a little shame that it required such a big change in her diet.

The diet change was challenging. She found it hard to replace certain staples—like a slice of cheese she always enjoyed with lunch—with gluten‑free alternatives. Instead of a whole grain of barley, she ate rice or oat‑based crackers. She often skipped the whole grain portion of her dinner, thinking “I can make up for it later.” On the other hand, she began to feel a sense of control that kept her spirits up. She also bought an over‑the‑counter supplement of vitamin B12, since she knew iron and folate were essential, but B12 was commonly deficient in vegetarians.

Despite her new diet and iron supplement, her CBC at the one‑year follow‑up showed hemoglobin 9.9 g/dL, MCV 73 fL, ferritin 4 ng/mL. Her symptoms, however, had worsened: the episodes of dizziness became more frequent, and she began noticing a “flutter” in her chest during quiet moments. Her GP, remembering her earlier history, recommended her start a low‑dose folic acid (0.8 mg daily) but did not push further investigations.

---

### 2013–2014: NSAIDs and the Gastrointestinal Cast

Marina began chronic back pain after a minor car accident in 2012. She was prescribed a course of NSAIDs (ibuprofen 600 mg twice daily) for three months and found relief for the most part. However, she began to experience occasional darkening of her stool—a subtle blackness she initially attributed to coffee. By mid‑2014, she had started to worry about “gastric bleeding.” She asked her GP if she could continue ibuprofen. The GP, wary of potential gastric erosion, advised her to switch to naproxen but insisted she continue monitoring her stool color.

The next year, her CBC was abnormal again. Her hemoglobin dropped to 8.5 g/dL and was accompanied by a thrombocytosis (platelet count 450,000 /µL) that the GP speculated could be reactive. Her GP repeated a ferritin test, which again returned below 5 ng/mL. She was told to continue her iron supplementation and take an over‑the‑counter antacid. Still, the black spots on her stool persisted. She reported occasional nausea after meals and a feeling of fullness early in the afternoon.

In 2015, after an unscheduled ER visit for syncope while going to the bathroom, a bedside hemoglobin measurement in the emergency department was 7.4 g/dL. This was a wake‑up call. A rapid test revealed positive guaiac (fecal occult blood). A colonoscopy was performed in late 2015, showing erosive gastritis with multiple small linear ulcerations in the posterior stomach wall, likely secondary to NSAID use. No obvious sources of overt bleeding were found. She received a proton pump inhibitor and was temporarily switched to a different analgesic, acetaminophen, for her back pain. Her iron supplement was increased to 325 mg ferrous sulfate once daily.

After the procedure, her iron levels improved slightly, with hemoglobin rising to 8.2 g/dL, but they never fully returned to baseline. She described feeling “sick all the time” and began experiencing shortness of breath with minimal activity. She complained of “tiredness that won’t go away” and that her skin looked “more tired.” Despite these complaints, she still felt the necessity of staying busy — she was a nurse, after all.

---

### 2016–2018: The Cumulative Weight

During 2016, Marina started complaining of a new symptom—a mild, persistent heart palpitations at rest. She felt her heart quickening and then slowing unpredictably. On a routine visit, her GP performed an ECG and noted a borderline normal sinus rhythm. His blood work was unchanged: hemoglobin 7.9 g/dL, ferritin 6 ng/mL. He told her to schedule a follow‑up in six months if symptoms worsened.

In a quiet, humid Saturday morning in 2017, Marina’s left thigh felt like a heavy stone. She had known for months that she had “tightness” after long periods of walking. She was too busy to book a visit, so she tried a self‑therapy, applying a heated compress. Yet, that evening she noticed the faint pulsing in her chest again and was forced to get an urgent CBC. The lab returned hemoglobin 7.7 g/dL, an MCV of 70 fL, elevated RDW, ferritin 5 ng/mL, and normal serum iron. Her iron supplement dosing was increased again. The GP, being thorough, added a daily dose of oral vitamin C to enhance absorption.

The next month that the same pattern of low hemoglobin and low ferritin persisted with minor variations. She tried a new brand of ferrous sulfate, feeling a stomach upset each time. She stopped taking it, rationalizing that the side effects were too bothersome. Meanwhile, the celiac diagnosis had placed her in a gluten‑free community group. She shared her anemia story, but the group’s focus was on weight loss; she felt the “anemia” was a different narrative.

In 2018, Marina’s doctor, Dr. Patel, recognized her chronic heavy periods and repeated heavy bleeding, her celiac, and her NSAID use. He noted a high suspicion of iron deficiency anemia secondary to menorrhagia, chronic GI bleeding, chronic disease (from celiac), and poor dietary intake. He offered a trial of a levonorgestrel intra‑uterine device (IUD) to reduce menstrual blood loss. Marina, however, found the idea of an IUD frightening; she had only had a contraceptive prescription in the past, not an IUD. She declined and chose instead to continue iron therapy and NSAIDs.

She was admitted to the hospital in early 2018 after fainting in the bathroom while turning around. Her hemoglobin was 6.5 g/dL. She received a transfusion of two units of packed red blood cells and was started on intravenous ferrous sulfate. A review of her medication list during that admission led the inpatient team to discontinue NSAIDs temporarily. A follow‑up CBC in the ED on day four showed hemoglobin 9.2 g/dL, ferritin 8 ng/mL—an improvement, but not adequate for her baseline.

The next month, she was discharged. The hospital notes read, “Anemia likely multifactorial; will continue iron therapy; to see GI and gynecology specialists.” On her follow‑up appointments, each specialist gave her a component of the story but no single diagnosis. The GP advised “take the iron.” The gynecologist suggested hormonal birth control as a means to reduce menstrual flow. The gastroenterologist recommended a repeat endoscopy to watch for NSAID ulcer progression, but her insurance declined coverage for a repeat procedure, citing “no new findings” from the last colonoscopy. She could not pay for an outpatient esophagogastroduodenoscopy (EGD) without insurance.

---

### 2019–2021: The Slow Drag

Marina’s 45th birthday came after a year of repeated fatigue and a persistent grayish discoloration of her hair. Her hair looked thinner, and every time she brushed through it she thought, “It must be the anemia.” She had never been called “anaemic” by anyone in her life. Her doctor at that time, a new family physician, finally documented a “diagnosis code: iron deficiency anemia” when she came in for a routine skin check. He prescribed a daily ferrous sulfate tablet, switched from the triple dose to a single daily dose because he read that multiple doses can reduce absorption.

It was March of 2020 when the pandemic forced outpatient visits into virtual. Marina’s PCP suggested she get a home finger‑stick test to see how her CBC was trending. She downloaded a free smartphone app that showed her hemoglobin at 8.1 g/dL at home, versus 7.6 g/dL in earlier labs. She tried to follow the recommendations: take her iron supplement with a glass of orange juice and avoid drinking tea on the same day. She also switched her NSAID therapy from naproxen to acetaminophen, thinking it would reduce GI bleeding.

The next few months were unremarkable. Her CBC from a local lab, performed on her request, displayed hemoglobin 7.8 g/dL, ferritin 4 ng/mL. She returned to her GP, who simply said, “You’ll get better once you get more iron into the bloodstream.” In a meeting with her gynecologist, she expressed her desire to reduce menstrual bleeding. The gynecologist suggested a copper IUD but she declined again. She had the idea that “iron deficiency is just a result of eating less, and not that there’s something else.”

By mid‑2021, her anemia had not improved, and she was experiencing new symptoms: a mild, constant headache, an occasional tinnitus, and a chronic sense of “not being able to catch my breath” on walking up a flight of stairs. Her GP prescribed a “short course of corticosteroid” for what he described as “an inflammatory disease,” expecting it might address her chronic disease anemia. Marina took the steroids for a month. The next CBC revealed hemoglobin 8.0 g/dL, ferritin 3 ng/mL. The steroids didn't bring her ferritin up; they made her feel drowsy.

She began to wonder if something was wrong but never said anything. Even though she could not find a single doctor who could put all her data together, her life continued. She did not feel the need for an official label or diagnosis. The concept of being “undiagnosed” had slipped into her routine; she kept looking to her own pulse and blood pressure, but those were unchanged. She would shrug, “That’s how people age,” told herself. The small steps she took to adjust her diet and reduce NSAID use were not enough.

---

### 2022: A Silent Continuation

When the healthcare insurance policy changed in early 2022, the cost of her iron supplements increased. Marina, being a nurse, had paid for most of it out of pocket in the past, but she realized the price had doubled. She cut back on her ferrous sulfate after the doctor told her “we need to keep the blood moving.” That month, her hemoglobin fell to 7.6 g/dL. Her wife suggested she get a new CBC in the hospital, but Marina declined, feeling the “hospital is too expensive.”

Her routine blood tests at her local laboratory over the next six months consistently produced a pattern: hemoglobin hovering between 7.5 and 8.5 g/dL, ferritin 1–6 ng/mL, serum iron low, transferrin saturation 10–12 %. Her RBC morphology consistently showed microcytosis and anisocytosis. Yet, these abnormal lab values were simply noted on the requisition form and were not entered as an explicit diagnosis in her EHR. Her last doctor wrote in the note, “Patient continues to have low iron stores; continue therapy.”

During a routine checkup with a new internal medicine specialist at the end of 2022, she was told that “the work-up looks typical.” The specialist recommended she begin an ongoing oral iron supplement regimen of 325 mg a day and “schedule a follow‑up in three months.” The patient, after careful reading of her chart, realized that iron deficiency was not yet listed as a formal diagnosis in her file; perhaps the label was not the problem. She kept her routine.

---

### 2023: A Final Piece

By mid‑2023, Marina’s fatigue had become so chronic that it started to affect her mood. She felt a sense of emptiness and thought it might be depression. Though she was seen by a psychiatrist for this, she also brought up her recurrent anemia. The psychiatrist was supportive and suggested a “full anemia work‑up” to rule out any underlying cause. She booked a lab panel that included iron studies, B12, folate, and tests for hemolysis. All came back normal except for iron parameters.

The psychiatrist prescribed a serotonin‑reuptake inhibitor, but the effect was minimal. Her doctor said, “We’re going to keep treating the anemia as it is,” and added a note in her chart that she had a “possible multifactorial anemia.” Yet no official diagnostic code was ever added. The health system used a new EHR that required explicit disease codes to trigger certain protocols. The last time a physician reviewed the chart had only scanned for the code; the code was not present. The doctor did not feel the need to add one or to push for additional testing because the patient's current symptoms had not escalated drastically.

Marina, aware of the label’s benefits (insurance coding, medication coverage, potential clinical trial inclusion), had long asked herself if she wanted the diagnosis. The answer was mixed: the diagnosis would bring an official name and a potential sense of closure, but it could also open doors to treatments that she had not yet considered, like a long‑acting iron infusion or a hormone therapy to control her heavy flow.

By the time of the narrative’s closing in mid‑2024, Marina had a formal iron deficiency anemia code filed by the new family practice in her chart. The code was present, but no follow‑up was scheduled. The story remained the same: a patient living with the steady, silent ache of a chronic, undiagnosed anemia: heavy bleeding, chronic GI bleeding, malabsorption from celiac disease, and the compounded effect of a long‑term vegetarian diet and NSAID use. The medical narrative is incomplete, but the patient and many in her health team understand the components; the single cohesive diagnosis, however, remains elusive in her daily experience.
