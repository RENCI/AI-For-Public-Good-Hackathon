**A 22‑Year‑Old Male’s Journey Through a Lateral Ankle Sprain (ATFL)**

---

### Episode Begins – Day 0

Jacob, a 22‑year‑old university sophomore, stands out on the campus quad for his love of basketball and weekend soccer games. He is a little heavier than his peers at 240 lb (≈ 108 kg) due to a history of mild obesity, but he keeps active by running on the college track and playing pick‑up sports.

On a Saturday afternoon while walking barefoot along a gravel trail behind the library, one of the stones dislodged, and his foot caught on a sudden dip. He pivoted sharply, his ankle inverted as his weight shifted, and an audible “pop” accompanied a wave of sharp pain down the lateral side of the right ankle. Instantly, Jacob felt the foot buckle, the pain intensified, and he could not put weight on it. A throbbing sensation and a feeling that the ankle was “giving way” followed.

He decided to wait a few minutes to see if the pain would subside. After 15 minutes the pain remained intense, there was visible swelling on the outer ankle, and a dull warmth spread over the lateral malleolus. His friend suggested he get medical help.

### First Medical Encounter – Day 1

Jacob goes to the university emergency department the same afternoon. The nurse observes moderate erythema and swelling at the lateral ankle joint. On physical exam, a moderate‑grade ATFL sprain (Grade II) is suspected:

- **Positive Anterior Drawer Test** – mild anterior translation of the talus with the ankle in 30° dorsiflexion and 30° inversion. - **Tinel’s sign** – tapping over the lateral malleolus elicits a tingling sensation. - **Pain** – localized to the ATFL area; no plantar or medial tenderness. The ED team performs a plain radiograph of the ankle to rule out fracture. The X‑ray is unremarkable, confirming no bony injury.

**Management Plan**

1. **Acute R.I.C.E.** – Rest, Ice (30 min every 2 hrs), Compression bandage (elastic wrap), and Elevation (leg above heart). 2. **Pain control** – Over‑the‑counter NSAID (ibuprofen 400 mg q6–8 hrs PRN). 3. **Brace** – A semi‑rigid ankle‑foot orthosis is fitted to limit inversion and support the ATFL during healing. 4. **Follow‑up** – A physiotherapy referral and an appointment with an orthopaedic clinic the next week.

### Day 3 – Home Care Progress

Jacob’s swelling has slightly subsided, but the lateral ankle still feels tender, especially when he flexes the foot. He follows the R.I.C.E. protocol diligently. Using the brace, he is able to ambulate briefly at home on crutches without pain. The pain score drops from 8/10 at diagnosis to 4/10. He begins to notice the “tightness” in the lateral foot when he does simple dorsiflexion.

### Day 5 – First Physiotherapy Assessment

Jacob meets with a licensed physiotherapist at the university sports medicine center. The PT confirms a Grade II ATFL sprain with residual ankle instability and muscle atrophy from immobility.

**Therapeutic interventions:**

- **Early mobilization** within the limits of pain (isometric strengthening of peroneal muscles, closed‑chain ankle circles). - **Gentle stretching** of the gastrocnemius‑soleus complex. - **Joint mobilization** (grade III side‑to‑side glides at the subtalar joint) to ease stiffness. - **Education** on proper use of the brace, safe walking techniques, and importance of controlled weightbearing.

Jacob is encouraged to stay with the brace and use crutches as needed, but he can now take short walks. By the end of the visit, pain has further reduced to 2/10, and he feels confident moving around the campus quad with his brace.

### Day 10 – Transition to Controlled Weightbearing

Over the week, Jacob’s swelling is essentially gone. The physiotherapist notes improved proprioception on the balance board assessment and a slight reduction in muscle bulk differences between legs. Jacob is now ready to increase activity:

- **Partial weightbearing** in the brace for 3–4 days. - Introduction of **active dorsiflexion and plantarflexion** with minimal load. - **Continued isometric peroneal strengthening** and **dynamic balance training** on unstable surfaces.

He is also given a home exercise program to reinforce the clinic visits. Jacob’s weight has gradually decreased, which helps reduce joint loading during activity.

### Day 14 – Functional Milestones

Jacob completes partial weightbearing in the brace and begins gradual return to low‑impact activities – walking upstairs and walking on uneven campus paths without pain. His ankle pain is now minimal, limited to occasional mild soreness after a long walk. He can also do the “single‑leg heel‑raise” with no significant discomfort.

The orthopaedic clinic visit on Day 14 confirms:

- No residual instability on the **Anterior Drawer Test** (within normal limits). - Adequate dorsiflexion range of motion (70°). - No tenderness on palpation. - The brace is effective in providing subjective stability.

### Day 20 – Clearance for Sport Participation

Jacob’s PT signs that he has regained proprioception and muscular strength to normal levels. He is now cleared for low‑to‑medium impact sport. Specific restrictions include:

- Avoid sudden pivot or cutting motions until he returns to full strength and stability. - Continue wearing the brace for the first 2–3 weeks post‑re‑entry into sports.

He is prescribed a gradual return-to-play protocol with a focus on neuromuscular training before full participation in competitive basketball.

### Day 28 – Return to Basketball

Jacob joins his university team for a few drills. He performs without pain, although he reports mild tightness after an extended sprint session, likely due to his weight. By the end of the first week of practice, his ankle feels normal, and no swelling is observed.

The physiotherapist reinforces calf‑strengthening exercises and addresses weight management through a nutritionist who helps him gradually reduce his body fat percentage to a healthier level. This is important to prevent recurrence and to reduce medial overload on the ankle joint.

### Day 35 – Fully Resolved

One month after the acute injury, Jacob’s ankle is pain‑free during all activities, including jogging, basketball, and barefoot walking on uneven terrain. The physiotherapist confirms:

- Normal Anterior Drawer Test. - Full range of dorsiflexion (70°) and plantarflexion (45°). - No residual tenderness or swelling. - Full muscular symmetry: peroneal and calf strength equal to the contralateral limb. - Excellent proprioceptive and dynamic balance scores.

Jacob continues with the braces only during high‑intensity sports or when playing barefoot on uneven grounds. His obesity is still a risk factor for future ankle injury, so he maintains lifestyle modifications to keep his weight under control.

Throughout this timeline, Jacob’s comorbid obesity and previous ankle sprain were acknowledged but did not alter the textbook course of a Grade II ATFL sprain. The high‑impact sports participation and barefoot walking on uneven terrain served as precipitating and predisposing factors, which the care team addressed through education and a graded rehabilitation plan. By the end of the month, the ankle has fully resolved, highlighting a successful, typical recovery for a young athletic adult.
