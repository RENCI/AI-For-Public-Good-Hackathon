By the end of a hectic Tuesday, Maya Patel was already nursing a new discomfort that she almost chalked up to a stomach bug. Maya—35, a dental hygienist in a bustling practice in Portland—had finished a long day of appointments and slipped into a low‑light lounge at a local coffee shop, where she felt an odd pressure in her lower abdomen, a twinge of burning as she went to the restroom. She chalked it to what a seasoned hygienist would label “the usual post‑workday cramps” and didn’t think much of it until the next morning.

**Day 1 (the morning after)** Maya’s bladder‑burning had escalated—each urination left a stinging bite that lingered for a minute or two. She also had a mild annoyance: a sense of pressure just above the pubic bone that seemed to worsen whenever she stood for more than a few minutes. She had had a minor urinary catheter inserted after a routine cystoscopy that she’d undergone the previous week for an unusual bladder irritation. Though the catheter was removed three days ago, a residual irritation might be lingering. She remembered that she’d slept with a spermicidal diaphragm inserted earlier that evening—an item she used infrequently but habitually with her most recent partner. Adding her recent sexual activity and spermicidal use—both well‑documented risks for UTIs—Maya quietly noted her symptoms and wondered if a day of rest would set them off.

**Day 2 (mid‑morning)** The burning was now sharp enough that Maya avoided going back to the office for a bit, and a mild fever had crept in, barely 99.6 °F. “It feels like a dull ache in the lower abdomen,” she told her mother on a brief phone call, but it was her own voice that finally sounded like “I can’t ignore this.” The next step, as she’d been advised after her last catheter event, was to seek professional care.

**Day 3 (early afternoon)** Maya walked into her primary‑care office. The nurse immediately recorded her vital signs: Blood pressure 118/70 mm Hg, pulse 84 bpm, temperature 99.4 °F. A quick digital rectal exam was declined because Maya had no rectal discomfort, and the provider noted mild suprapubic tenderness on pelvic palpation. A urinalysis dipstick in the exam room revealed positive nitrites and leukocyte esterase—classic hallmarks of a bacterial urinary tract infection. The nurse handed Maya a clean‑catch midstream urine specimen, which the lab later reported with a culture growth of *Escherichia coli* at 10^4 CFU/mL.

**Clinical Decision Point 1 – Diagnosis** Given the absence of systemic symptoms (no chills, no flank pain, no gastrointestinal distress) and no comorbidities, the office physician deemed this an uncomplicated cystitis. “We’ll start you on a short course of antibiotics and you’ll be back in a week,” she said reassuringly.

**Day 3 (same afternoon)** The physician prescribed a 7‑day course of TMP‑SMX (trimethoprim 80 mg/ sulfamethoxazole 400 mg) orally, once daily, emphasizing that her urinary catheter had already been removed and that the infection was likely bacterial. She cautioned Maya on potential side effects—rash, nausea, and a need for her to finish the full course even if symptoms improved. She also delivered advice about hydration: “At least 64 ounces of water a day will help flush the bacteria out.” Finally, she advised Maya to pause using spermicidal agents for the next few weeks, explaining how spermicides disrupt normal bacterial flora and predispose to infection.

**Day 5 (morning)** Maya woke the next morning and, for the first time since the symptoms started, the burning was minimal—one painful stream in the middle of her urination that lasted only a short time. She noted a marked improvement in the suprapubic pressure and felt “lighter.” However, she still had a mild burning on the first few minutes after she had to use the bathroom. She took her first pill of TMP‑SMX and kept a diary of her symptoms and medication adherence.

**Day 7 (the last day of antibiotics)** By the end of a 7‑day course, the burning had resolved completely, and Maya’s urination felt normal again. She reported no dizziness or rash—her only residual memory was of the “sharp sting” that had begun to fade after several days. At the end of her antibiotic course, her physician instructed her to schedule a one‑week follow‑up, just to confirm complete resolution, and advised her to avoid spermicidal agents for at least a month and to increase fluid intake.

**Day 10 (follow‑up visit)** Maya returned to her office. She was clear of all symptoms, and her pelvic exam was normal. The office physician ordered a repeat urine dipstick as a courtesy, which was negative for nitrites and leukocyte esterase. A repeat urine culture was not performed, per routine guidelines for uncomplicated, resolved cystitis. Maya left the office with a prescription for a non‑prescription analgesic in case of future dysuria and a reminder to report back immediately should symptoms recur.

**Day 15 (home)** Maya had fully resumed her normal routine. She had noticed a few nights after her catheter removal that she felt a small, sharp burning when she first tried urinating during the day of the cystoscopy, but the pain dissipated quickly within a minute and never returned. She avoided using spermicidal products for the next three weeks, and afterward continued her standard hygiene practices. She made no further visits to the office regarding urinary symptoms, indicating a completely resolved episode.

**Key Clinical Take‑aways**

- **Risk Factor Recognition**: Recent sexual activity, spermicidal agent use, and transient catheterization were appropriately noted, and the provider addressed these both diagnostically and therapeutically. - **Diagnosis and Management**: Classic dipstick positivity, culture confirmation, and a short course of TMP‑SMX—aligning with guidelines for uncomplicated cystitis. - **Follow‑up**: A brief visit to ensure resolution and to reinforce preventive measures, confirming the intended outcome of disease resolution.

Through a straightforward timeline, Maya’s uncomplicated cystitis course was effectively managed, with rapid symptom relief and no lasting sequelae.
