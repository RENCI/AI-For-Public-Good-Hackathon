John Martinez, a 35‑year‑old construction supervisor, had never had a medical problem serious enough to make him think twice about his health. He could walk a mile without a breath of air, drank a few beers on the weekend, and had a longstanding history of episodic migraines that he typically managed with over‑the‑counter analgesics. He also had been hospitalized a few times for major depressive episodes, for which he had been on a stable dose of sertraline. His father had a history of untreated seizures in his thirties, and his mother, who had been a chronic drinker during her pregnancy, had passed down a family pedigree of neurological susceptibility that John had acknowledged only in passing.

On a rainy Thursday afternoon, the day that would become the turning point of his medical story, John was attempting to lift a heavy metal beam when the load tipped. He slipped on the slick floor, slammed his forehead against a steel beam, and fell heavily onto the concrete. He heard the thud and, momentarily, the world swerved in a green haze of motion. The next thing he remembered was the white light of the emergency department, the smell of antiseptic, and the sight of a nurse holding his wrist and pulling a cuff around his arm.

**Day 0–1: ED and First Seizure**

At the ED, a cranial CT scan revealed a left frontal subdural hematoma measuring 2 mm in thickness with a small midline shift. There were no signs of skull fracture. John was admitted to the neurosurgical unit for close observation. He slept poorly that evening, waking every hour with a throbbing headache that had a migraine quality—pulsatile, accompanied by photophobia and an aura of blurred vision. Early on the next morning, he suddenly became rigid, his left arm jerking in a stereotyped clonic motion that lasted about 18 seconds before he fell to the floor. The episode was witnessed by the nurse and stopped spontaneously within a minute.

Lorazepam 1 mg IV was given, and the patient's vital signs stabilized. The neurologist, Dr. Patel, noted the resemblance of the seizure to previous focal events seen in patients with parenchymal contusion. An electroencephalogram (EEG) was scheduled for the same day. John was kept on continuous cardiac monitoring and had a lumbar puncture performed to rule out intracranial infection, which returned normal.

**Day 2–3: Early Management and Differential Warnings**

The EEG from day 2 showed frequent sharp‑wave discharges over the left temporoparietal region with evolving electrographic seizures, which suggested that the subdural hematoma was indeed the precipitating event. Based on the EEG findings and the known risk factor of a family history of epilepsy, Dr. Patel empirically started levetiracetam 500 mg twice daily. The dose was chosen to balance efficacy and side effects because John’s baseline liver function tests were normal, but his mother’s alcoholism history made liver toxicity a concern for any future antiepileptic therapies.

Meanwhile, the psychiatric team visited John, noting that the sudden stress of the accident had triggered a rebound of depressive symptoms: low mood, anhedonia, and intrusive thoughts of the fall. His sertraline dose remained unchanged for now. The migraine team was consulted, and John was given a trial of sumatriptan for acute headache, as the migraine phenotype was partially overlapping with his new post-traumatic headaches.

**Day 4–5: Second Seizure and Med Change**

Despite the levetiracetam, John had a second seizure at home on day 4. It was a generalized tonic–clonic event that lasted approximately 3 minutes. The family called emergency services, and John arrived at the ED with post‑ictal confusion. A repeat head CT showed a small increase in the size of the subdural collection, now 3 mm with a midline shift of 4 mm. Dr. Patel, after reviewing the imaging, decided to transfer John to the surgical floor for definitive management. However, an operative plan for a burr‑hole evacuation was put on hold, because the hematoma was considered to be small and the patient was otherwise clinically stable.

The neurology team, in consultation with the neurosurgeon, stepped up levetiracetam to 1000 mg twice daily and added lacosamide 100 mg once daily to broaden antiepileptic coverage. The family expressed anxiety, and Dr. Patel discussed the importance of adherence and the risk of breakthrough seizures. He also explained that the improvement of his depression and anxiety would help reduce the likelihood of seizure triggers.

**Week 2: Rehabilitation and Stabilization**

After the two seizures, John remained in the hospital for a week with serial imaging and EEG checks. A repeat CT after 48 hours demonstrated slight regression of the hematoma to 2 mm, and clinical symptoms had improved. His EEG now had only occasional sharp‑wave discharges, and there were no evolving seizures. The neurology team decided to maintain the dual antiepileptic drug regimen and monitor for side effects, such as dizziness or irritability, given John's history of depression and his own reports of feeling “foggy” in the mornings.

He began a course of physical therapy under the guidance of Dr. Gomez, a certified neuro‑rehabilitator. John was able to ambulate with a short walker, though a mild right‑side spasticity persisted that was likely secondary to the left‑frontal lobe injury. The occupational therapist noted subtle deficits in executive function—John struggled with task sequencing, which paralleled his migraine aura of “brain fog” that sometimes made him feel unable to concentrate.

**Week 3–4: Transition Home and Ongoing Improvement**

On discharge day, the treating team confirmed that John’s headache pattern had shifted toward more of a chronic tension‑type rather than a migraine aura. His levetiracetam dose was scheduled to continue at 1000 mg BID. Lacosamide remained at 100 mg QHS. A final EEG 48 hours before discharge showed no epileptiform discharges, providing reassurance of acute stability.

John’s follow‑up appointments were planned at the neurology clinic in one week, the psychiatric clinic two weeks after, and the neurosurgery outpatient clinic in six weeks for a follow‑up CT. He was advised to avoid alcohol and limit strenuous activity, to stay hydrated, and to continue taking sertraline 50 mg daily to support mood stability. His mother’s history of alcoholism was discussed with him in the context of avoiding family‑predisposed triggers—most notably, limiting exposure to potentially triggering substances and ensuring a supportive, low‑stress home environment.

**Outcome and Reflection**

Over the next month, John reported that his headaches had become less frequent and no longer accompanied the classic migraine aura. His depressive symptoms had abated, and he reported an increase in mood and a renewed interest in his hobbies. The neurologist noted that John had had no further seizures and his EEG remained normal. Because John's brain injury was subacute but he had not progressed to a permanent epilepsy syndrome, he was considered “improving” on the acuity scale. His risk for future seizures was lowered from a high suspicion (familial history and acute symptomatic event) to a moderate risk, contingent on continued AED compliance and careful monitoring. The rehabilitation teams noted improved gait speed and decreased spasticity, with an overall return to baseline functional mobility.

John remained on a low‑dose levetiracetam regimen for a further six months, with plans to trial a weaning schedule if the EEG remained stable. He and his primary care provider also instituted an updated psychiatric care plan that included mindfulness‑based cognitive therapy to address the lingering cognitive fog and a relapse prevention strategy for depression. By the end of the third month, John had returned to his construction duties with a modified shift schedule, marking his return to life with the knowledge that his new neurological status, though improved, would require vigilance.
