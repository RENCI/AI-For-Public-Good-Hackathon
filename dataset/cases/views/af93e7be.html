When Mr K. first walked into the primary‑care clinic in late October of 2023, he was a 70‑year‑old retired clerk who told the nurse that he had been dealing with an “icky” sense of having to pee all the time, especially at night. He had been going to the bathroom three to four times during daylight hours and up to six–seven times after midnight. The sensation was mild, but it was getting in the way of his sleep: “I can’t keep a full night’s rest.” He reported a new difficulty in starting his stream at the bathroom, a weak pull, and intermittent hesitation. He assured his physician that he had not had any pain or blood in the urine, that he had no flank pain, and that his appetite and weight had remained stable for the past six months.

The physical exam was essentially normal. His vital signs were a blood‑pressure reading of 150/95 mmHg, a BMI of 30.8, a heart rate of 78 beats per minute on a normal sinus rhythm, a fasting blood glucose of 145 mg/dL (HbA1c 7.3 % on a background diet and metformin 1000 mg BID), and a well‑muscled but slightly puffy abdomen. His review of systems revealed hypertension, type 2 diabetes, and a chronic “slight” kidney complaint that he described more as a “high creatinine” than a painful condition. His history was notable for heavy beer drinking in his younger years (3–4 beers a week for 45 years), a high‑fat diet with minimal fruit or vegetable intake, and a father who died of prostate cancer at age 68. He also admitted to a 20‑year history of benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH), which he had treated sporadically with over‑the‑counter medications.

In that first encounter, the office staff drew a PSA, ordered a DRE, and asked him to obtain a renal function panel. The PSA came back at 4.6 ng/mL (upper limit of normal 4.0 ng/mL). The DRE was “soft” with a slight enlargement of the transition zone. His basic metabolic panel revealed a serum creatinine of 1.8 mg/dL (eGFR 35 mL/min/1.73 m² according to CKD‑EPI), consistent with stage‑3 chronic kidney disease (CKD). Mr K. was instructed to keep hydrated, to avoid NSAIDs, and to schedule a urology consult for further workup. He was told that a PSA that high in his age group warranted further evaluation, but that the current clinical picture was not alarming enough for immediate surgery.

Within two weeks he was on the urology waiting list. That afternoon the urologist, Dr L, performed a repeat DRE, and the digital impression of the prostate was again soft but with an area of mild induration in the right transition zone. The PSA was now 4.8 ng/mL. Dr L ordered an ultrasound of the prostate and a transrectal ultrasound‑guided prostate biopsy. The ultrasound showed an 4 cm rounded gland, typical of BPH. The biopsy, performed in the clinic’s office suite, sent to pathology confirmed a 6 × 6 mm focus of cancer in the right peripheral zone. Gleason 3 + 3 (score 6) – a low‑grade, grade 1, localized prostate cancer. The pathology report noted that cancer was confined to a single core, with no evidence of extraprostatic extension, and the tumor was clinically staged as T2a–T3a depending on how you interpret imaging and biopsies.

At the next appointment (two months from the first visit) Mr K. was advised that his low-risk, low‑volume cancer meant that he could pursue *active surveillance* rather than immediate surgery. The urologist explained that because he had other significant comorbidities – hypertension, diabetes, CKD stage 3, and a history of BPH – a definitive intervention such as radical prostatectomy or radiation might be risky. The first step would be to see how his PSA behaved over the next few months with minimal intervention. Dr L recommended that the patient start tamsulosin 0.4 mg BID to relieve his lower urinary tract symptoms. Dr L also suggested a trial of finasteride 5 mg daily because it had proved to reduce prostate size in BPH and could keep the transition zone from expanding.

The patient began tamsulosin one month later and reported a modest improvement: he could start his stream more easily and his nocturia fell from six to four times per night. However, he told his nephrologist that his blood‑pressure remained 150/95 mmHg and his fasting glucose was 140 mg/dL. The nephrologist added valsartan 160 mg nightly for resistant hypertension and switched his metformin regimen to 500 mg BID to minimize nephrotoxicity. The nephrologist also advised that the patient restrict protein intake and avoid high‑salt foods, in part because the drug list, high‑fat diet, and CKD predisposed him to nephrolithiasis.

At six months from the first visit, Mr K. came into the clinic with a slightly higher PSA of 5.2 ng/mL, a tiny uptick from the baseline. He was still on tamsulosin and finasteride, but he complained that he had yet to experience a meaningful change in his urinary frequency. Dr L decided to intensify BPH relief: finasteride was increased to 5 mg daily (he had already been on that dose). There was an added discussion of the fact that PSA can be influenced by BPH and by finasteride; PSA might even be suppressed by finasteride. Dr L therefore decided to track PSA trends over the next year.

Nine months into his active surveillance, his PSA climbed to 6.8 ng/mL, and the DRE now hinted at a slightly firm zone on the left side of the prostate. The patient also noticed that he was feeling more fatigued, that his usual 5‑kilometer walk had become a 1‑kilometer brisk march, and that he had begun losing 2–3 lb over the preceding month, even though he was still eating a high‑fat, low‑vegetable diet. His HbA1c had risen to 7.9 %, and his blood pressure had spiked to 172/98 mmHg despite the addition of a diuretic. The nephrologist noted a rise in creatinine to 2.0 mg/dL, which was worrisome given his progressive diabetes and hypertension.

Mr K.’s next urology visit (12 months from the first one) found PSA at 8.4 ng/mL and a suspicious lump on the right side of the prostate that was not present two months earlier. A repeat transrectal ultrasound, now coupled with a CT of the pelvis and a bone scan, revealed a 1.2 cm hypodense lesion adjacent to the seminal vesicles, suggestive of local extension. The bone scan was equivocal for bone metastasis – there were small uptake spots at the spine, but they were non‑specific in the context of an acute fracture or osteoporosis.

Dr L now had a difficult conversation: given Mr K.’s comorbidities (severe CKD, uncontrolled diabetes, hypertension, obesity) and his new local spread, the usual definitive options – radical prostatectomy and radical radiation therapy – were high risk. The risks of a 15–20 % peri‑operative mortality for radical prostatectomy were amplified by a eGFR of 30 mL/min. Radiation, while less invasive, could cause severe radiation cystitis and rectal toxicity in a patient with a predisposed urinary tract and a high risk of GI side effects due to his diet.

A multidisciplinary discussion was convened. The oncologist proposed external beam radiation therapy (EBRT) with androgen deprivation therapy (ADT) for the next six months, but the patient was not on a hormone therapy schedule, and the nephrologist cautioned that ADT (i.e. LHRH agonists like leuprolide or antagonists like degarelix) could worsen his metabolic profile, worsen his insulin resistance, elevate his testosterone‑related cardiometabolic risk, and potentially exacerbate his CKD by causing fluid overload. After considering the options, Mr K. decided to defer definitive therapy and instead pursue a *transurethral resection of the prostate* (TURP) to relieve his urinary obstruction. The urology team explained that TURP would not impact the cancer per se but could relieve his urinary obstruction, improve his quality of life, and temporarily prevent acute urinary retention. The patient agreed, as he was reluctant to undergo a major operation in the middle of his metabolic disease.

TURP was performed during the winter of 2024, with the usual perioperative precautions: intraprostatic blood pressure control, intra‑operative magnesium and calcium supplementation, and preoperative prophylactic antibiotics (ceftriaxone) to cover his urosepsis risk. The surgery went well. He was discharged home after 36 h with catheter patency, a postoperative serum creatinine of 2.1 mg/dL, and an HbA1c of 8.0 %. However, in the two weeks after discharge, he described a new dull aching pain in his lower back, a new sensation of pressure in his pelvis, and an inability to sit for more than 10 min. He called his primary‑care doctor, who prescribed a mild opioid regimen (hydrocodone 4 mg every 6 h as needed) and arranged a follow‑up with the urology department.

On his follow‑up visit at month 15 from baseline, Mr K.’s PSA was now 12.7 ng/mL. The DRE had become markedly suspicious, with a hard mass palpable through the capsule. An MRI pelvis showed a lesion that now extended beyond the prostate capsule into adjacent neurovascular bundles. The bone scan demonstrated several foci of increased uptake at the lumbar vertebrae and the left femoral neck—early signs of bone metastasis. His serum creatinine had climbed to 2.3 mg/dL, and his HbA1c rose to 8.7 %. His blood pressure remained uncontrolled despite triple therapy (ARB, diuretic, and a calcium‑channel blocker). He complained daily of “tremendous pain” in the lower back and pelvis, and he found himself needing to sit on a wheelchair rather than moving about his house.

During a hospital admission at month 18 in late summer, Mr K. presented with a severe urinary tract infection that led to sepsis and a delirious episode. He suffered a right hip fracture that required internal fixation. During the hospital stay, his doctors noted that he had been unable to adhere to dietary recommendations, that he had been losing the last 10 lb in the last month, and that he was experiencing profound fatigue even after an IV push antibiotic. They consulted palliative care, who agreed that further oncologic intervention would likely prolong suffering rather than provide meaningful benefit. They recommended a shift to comfort‑focused care, with a focus on managing pain, maintaining continence, and providing psychosocial support to Mr K. and his family.

The narrative of Mr K.’s case is that a once‑low‑risk, low‑grade prostate cancer began under the veil of a benign prostatic hyperplasia that was not fully addressed by pharmacologic therapy. His comorbidities—hypertension, diabetes, CKD, obesity—created a fragile physiological reserve, limiting his options for definitive therapy. In addition, his risk factors (age, family history, high‑fat diet, low fruit/vegetable intake, excessive alcohol) may have contributed to rapid progression. After 18 months, he was no longer in a position for successful curative treatment; he was in the throes of progressive metastatic disease with systemic symptoms, a deteriorating kidney function, and a frail musculoskeletal system. The outcome, thus, was unequivocally “worsening.”
