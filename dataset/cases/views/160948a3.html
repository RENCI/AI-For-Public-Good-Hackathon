Narrative When 55‑year‑old Rosa Ramirez first noticed that her mornings were quieter than usual, she did not think of her thyroid ...

When 55‑year‑old Rosa Ramirez first noticed that her mornings were quieter than usual, she did not think of her thyroid at all. She was a 10‑year‑retired schoolteacher who could barely keep up with the new student‑teacher conference at the local high school. Fatigue was not uncommon; it had become a steady background hum that no amount of coffee seemed to chase away.

Around 5 a.m. in late September of that year, she noted that her hair felt thinner, her scalp itched a little, and she had lost her sharpness in the morning. “I’m just older,” she told the vending machine attendant as she poured a black coffee. “It’s normal to slow down.”

The next month, Rosa’s doctor, Dr. Patel, saw her for a routine check‑up. Rosa’s blood pressure had been creeping up to 140/88 over the last two visits, her fasting glucose was 133 mg/dL, and her lipid panel—taken after her sister's heart attack—showed LDL at 180 mg/dL with a normal HDL. Rosa’s height and weight (5’4”, 190 lbs) gave her a BMI of 32.5; her waist line measured 54 inches. “You’re a little larger than you want to be,” Dr. Patel said, “but a bit of weight loss can be achieved with your diet and a simple walking routine.” Rosa left feeling hopeful, convinced that her “normal” fatigue, hair loss, and weight gain were simply the product of a high‑stress lifestyle.

Two weeks later, she slipped on a patch of wet grass on the sidewalk and lost her sense of balance for a half‑minute. “I’m just a bit dizzy,” she murmured to her daughter, who suggested she go to the emergency department. The ER visit yielded no acute abnormality; her heart rhythm was regular, her neuro exam normal, and her blood pressure was 138/85. They sent her home with a prescription for a multivitamin and a reminder to schedule a routine blood test.

Three months into her fall routine, Rosa began losing her appetite. Meals that had always been her comfort—fried chicken, cheesy mashed potatoes, and the occasional pizza—fell flat on her plate. The slow weight gain turned into a gain. At 54, she found herself in the weight‑loss clinic for diabetes, where they noted her waist circumference had ballooned to 56 inches. Her glucose levels rose to 158 mg/dL. The clinic recommended that she eat a high‑carbohydrate low‑fat diet, add a 1500‑calorie deficit, and begin walking for 30 minutes a day. She bought a cheap fitness tracker, but it was broken after a month. Her motivation plummeted after her mother’s death the spring, and her depression set in; she began to complain of being “dragged.”

By late spring of her 55th year, Rosa’s voice had deepened, and her morning stiffness was accompanied by aching shoulder joints. She also noticed that her voice now sounded “thicker.” She told her primary‑care physician, Dr. Patel, she felt as if she were “always tired, and I just can’t get that extra push to get to the gym.” Dr. Patel ran a full CBC and metabolic panel to rule out anemia, iron deficiency, or hypothyroidism, but the lab report only showed a mild anemia (Hb 12.4 g/dL) and a normal serum TSH of 4.0 µU/mL—just at the upper limit of normal for adult women.

The physician took note of the subtle rise in TSH, but the trend was not striking. “Let’s keep an eye on this. In the meantime, focus on your weight and blood pressure,” he advised. Rosa took the advice to heart—their “eye on it” reassurance served as a gentle push.

Four months later, Rosa was evaluated in the cardiology office for “increasing fatigue and shortness of breath.” Dr. Sanchez ran an echocardiogram; it appeared that she had a mild dilation of the left ventricle with borderline ejection fraction. He suggested a stress test. The stress test was unremarkable, but Rosa’s blood pressure on the treadmill spiked to 170/100, and a small episode of atrial fibrillation was noted—consistent with her family’s history of rhythm disorders. Her hypertension was upgraded to stage 2, and she was given a second‑line agent: a low‑dose ARB. She was also referred to a dietician.

In June, a month after the cardiology office’s visit, Rosa fell again. This time, she twisted her ankle, broke the lateral bone, and was taken to the orthopedics clinic. The orthopedic surgeon, recognizing her persistent low‑grade fever, mild anemia, and unplanned weight loss, asked if she had ever gone to the doctor about a “slow‑moving “thyroid issue.” Rosa’s response was a faint nod.

Throughout that year, Rosa’s routine lab panels had been taken by Dr. Patel, but no comprehensive evaluation had been done to investigate her persistent cold intolerance or her constant, “just tired” mood. The mild rise in TSH (4.0–5.2 µU/mL) had never prompted a repeat. Her fasting glucose had improved slightly (152 mg/dL) with medication, but she had a higher LDL (210 mg/dL) and a normal triglyceride but a low HDL (35 mg/dL). The cardiology team eventually told her, after multiple visits, that there was a risk of heart failure, but they did not mention thyroid disease.

During the final months of that timeline, Rosa began to experience worsening swelling of her feet and mild edema, which she attributed to her foot injuries. She slept with a small pillow under her left knee to relieve back pain, noticing a slow but steady increase in morning stiffness. In November, after a brief visit for her diabetes medications, Rosa reported a new symptom—difficulty swallowing. The family practice physician, Dr. Patel, mentioned she could do a basic “dysphagia” assessment and ruled out mechanical obstruction. She returned home with reassurance that her “swallowing” would settle with a few adjustments in diet.

By the time winter rolled around, Rosa’s TSH had risen to 6.6 µU/mL on a repeat lab panel, now beyond normal. Yet, her primary‑care group, heavily focused on her type‑2 diabetes and hypertension, interpreted the result along with her elevated LDL and recommended a statin. No endocrine consultation was referred at that point. Her depression became more evident as she isolated herself from friends, her only comfort left being her old crossword book, which she now left open on the nightstand while she stared at her phone. The mild memory fog that had appeared initially deepened. She would often ask herself whether the “tiredness” was a normal part of getting older, or whether it was "just a part of an old man," a phrase she heard many times from her neighbors.

All of this occurred while Rosa, who had never been told about Hashimoto’s thyroiditis, was gradually slipping into a state of metabolic derangement—hypertension, hyperlipidemia, type‑2 diabetes, and depression. She experienced a mild but progressive hypothyroid picture—fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance, constipation, hair thinning, dry skin, and myalgias—yet all the symptoms were consistently misinterpreted as belonging to her pre‑existing chronic diseases.

When the family physician finally flagged the rising TSH and suggested a referral to an endocrinologist, Rosa was in the middle of a month of multiple appointments—cardiology, orthopedic, oncology for the possible lung nodules, and a sleep study for daytime somnolence. She felt overwhelmed, her depression intensified, and she missed a month’s worth of visits, including her scheduled endocrine appointment. She would eventually fall through the cracks of the health system: no one followed up on that endocrine referral, and the TSH result became a footnote in an otherwise busy chart that listed only hypertension, diabetes, hyperlipidemia, and depression.

To this day, Rosa continues to suffer from gradual, insidious decline. Her family says she “doesn’t feel great,” but no one can tell her why she feels this way—except her doctor, who keeps measuring blood pressure, glucose, and lipids, but never a free T4 or antithyroid antibody test. Her disease course remains undiagnosed, a silent, overlooked cause woven into the fabric of her other chronic illnesses.
