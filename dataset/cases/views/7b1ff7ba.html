**April – First Symptoms**

When the first vague ache started in his left upper quadrant, Michael, a 65‑year‑old retired carpenter, brushed it off as lingering costochondritis from a recent mild strain. A handful of days later, he noticed his stools had become crisper and occasionally darkened, but he still attributed it to a sudden change in his diet—an increase in fried food and a handful of fast‑food meals he had taken to his weekly poker night. His wife pointed out that he had lost about 4 lb in the past month, yet he denied any significant weight loss or fatigue. Undaunted by his history of type 2 diabetes and Crohn disease, Michael continued his usual sedentary routine of evening walks, and his primary care clinic visit in late April yielded a routine check‑up: his HbA1c was 8.7 %, fasting glucose 165 mg/dL, and blood pressure 140/88 mm Hg. A basic blood panel showed microcytic anemia (Hb 11.8 g/dL, MCV 72 fl) and a mildly elevated C‑reactive protein of 5 mg/L. His clinic physician, noting the anemia but also his Crohn flare history, prescribed a short course of prednisone and suggested a follow‑up stool occult blood test, assuming perhaps a mild ileocolonic flare or dietary iron deficiency.

**May – Symptoms Intensify, Clinic Refocus**

By mid‑May, Michael’s left upper abdominal discomfort had evolved into a dull, burning ache that radiated from the splenic flexure toward the flank. He began experiencing intermittent rectal bleeding, often bright red on toilet paper, but the volume was small. He slept poorly, feeling an urgent need to shift stools even when the bowel was empty. He told his PCP about his 40‑pack‑year smoking history and his sedentary lifestyle. The physician decided that a colonoscopy was warranted, given his anemia, unexplained bleeding, and the fact that a mass in the distal colon could explain the mixed bowel symptoms. Arranged for a second‑level imaging, the patient also underwent a basic abdominal ultrasound that incidentally revealed a 2.5‑cm hypoechoic lesion near the splenic flexure.

**End‑June – Colonoscopy, Biopsy, and Misinterpretation**

The colonoscopy, performed in late June, was surprisingly difficult. The mucosa of the distal transverse colon had a thick, ulcerated plaque, and the diverticulated mucosa near the splenic flexure was markedly inflamed. The endoscopist, wary of his Crohn disease, biopsied the lesion under the suspicion that it was an extension of ulcerative Crohn disease. Histopathology returned the first surprise: it was a moderately differentiated adenocarcinoma. A more thorough review of the biopsy slides identified invasion through the muscularis propria (T4) and nodal involvement (N2). The pathology report cited the presence of signet ring cells, raising concern for aggressive disease. Michael was referred to a surgical oncologist and a gastroenterologist for further work‑up.

**Early July – CT Staging and Metastatic Findings**

A multiphasic contrast‑enhanced CT of the chest, abdomen, and pelvis was done in early July. The scan confirmed a 5‑cm infiltrating mass at the splenic flexure, encasing the splenic vessels and suggesting local invasion (confirmatory of T4). Multiple hypodense lesions were seen in the liver—two in the right lobe measuring 3 cm and 2.5 cm, and a smaller one in the left lobe 1.2 cm in diameter. There were also enlarged mesenteric nodes, and a faint hepatic capsule retraction adjacent to the colon mass. No evidence of ascites or peritoneal carcinomatosis was noted. No distant metastases beyond the liver were seen initially. The PET‑CT that followed in mid‑July confirmed increased metabolic activity in the splenic flexure mass and in the hepatic lesions, while the rest of the body showed only benign activity. This culminated in a clinical staging of T4N2M1. The decision was made to pursue palliative intent therapy, as resection of the primary with liver metastasectomy would likely be high risk and limited benefit.

**Late July – Onco‑Medical Decision & Treatment Initiation**

The oncology team met with Michael in late July to discuss his options. He was given a choice of systemic chemotherapy with a combination of 5‑FU, leucovorin, oxaliplatin (FOLFOX) plus bevacizumab. Because of his uncontrolled hypertension and his pre‑existing type 2 diabetes, he was started on a lower dose of oxaliplatin. The physicians explained that while cure was unlikely, the goal was to control systemic disease, alleviate symptoms, and potentially prolong survival. They also warned him that bevacizumab could worsen his blood pressure and cause hepatic side effects. Michael, after a difficult conversation with his wife about the seriousness of his cancer, consented to therapy and was admitted for the first chemotherapy cycle.

**August – First Cycle, Side‑Effects, and Diabetes Compromise**

During the first cycle, Michael experienced severe neuropathy—a classic oxaliplatin side effect—along with a pronounced drop in blood sugar from baseline. Insulin requirements increased sharply. The day after the infusion, he had a faint cough and mild shortness of breath, prompting a chest X‑ray that revealed mild bibasilar interstitial infiltrates, likely drug‑induced. His blood pressure rose to 170/98 mm Hg, and the oncology pharmacy added a low‐dose ACE inhibitor. Michael’s HbA1c spiked to 10.2 % by mid‑August, leading to a referral to his endocrinology team to adjust his insulin pump. Despite the acute side‑effects, he noticed a modest reduction in his left side abdominal pain and an improvement in bowel movements, with fewer bloody stools.

**September – Progression of Disease and New Symptoms**

During the second chemotherapy cycle, on routine laboratory monitoring, his alkaline phosphatase jumped from 120 U/L to 280 U/L, and GGT rose to 160 U/L. An ultrasound of his liver showed the two largest lesions had grown to 3.5 cm and 3 cm, respectively, and a new lesion, 1.8 cm, had appeared in segment 6. The patient reported increasing fatigue, worsening appetite, and early satiety after small meals. He started noticing a dull yellow discoloration of his skin and conjunctiva. A repeat CT at the end of September confirmed marked hepatomegaly and new intrahepatic hyper-vascular nodules. There was also increased ascites—free fluid in Morison’s pouch and around the bowel loops—suggesting decompensated liver disease. His liver function panel had deteriorated: INR 1.6, bilirubin 2.5 mg/dL, albumin 2.9 g/dL.

**October – Worsening Clinical Features, Family Consultation, and Palliative Shift**

The patient’s overall performance status dropped from ECOG 1 to ECOG 3. He could no longer stand for prolonged periods and required assistance to ambulate. New symptoms included low‑grade fevers, night sweats, and intense left flank pain radiating to the shoulder blade. The oncologist convened a multidisciplinary tumor board—a gastroenterologist, medical oncologist, palliative care specialist, and a hepatobiliary surgeon. The consensus was that further systemic chemotherapy would likely yield diminishing returns while exacerbating his hepatic dysfunction and precipitating further decline. The palliative care team was engaged to manage his pain, ascites drainage, and psychosocial needs.

**November – Terminal Decline**

A therapeutic paracentesis in early November yielded 2.5 L of milky ascitic fluid, with serum‑ascites albumin gradient (SAAG) of 1.1 g/dL, indicating portal hypertension. The fluid culture was negative. Michael’s inflammatory markers remained high, and his serum bilirubin topped 4.0 mg/dL. His appetite had virtually disappeared; he was taking less than 300 kcal daily. The end of November saw a sudden exacerbation of dyspnea; a chest CT showed pulmonary edema and mild consolidation. An echocardiogram ruled out congestive heart failure, pointing instead towards hepatic‑related hepato‑cardiogenic syndrome.

**December – Final Hospitalization & End‑of‑Life Care**

In mid‑December, Michael was admitted for acute decompensation of hepatic failure, respiratory distress, and sepsis‑like presentation. Despite broad‑spectrum antibiotics, his blood cultures remained sterile. The palliative care plan was to provide comfort measures: low‑dose opioids for pain, a short course of dexamethasone to reduce hepatic inflammation and ascites, and continuous monitoring of his blood pressure and glucose. Family discussions were held to confirm Michael’s advance directives; he wished for a gentle, symptom‑focused approach. He passed away peacefully at home in early January, surrounded by family, with his pain sufficiently controlled and his remaining days spent in a state of reduced distress.
