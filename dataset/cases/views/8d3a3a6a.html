**A Narrative of a 30‑Year‑Old Woman’s Acute OCD Crisis and Subsequent Recovery**

When the call to the police came in the middle of a rainy Sunday afternoon, Maya had already been wrestling with a storm inside her own mind for the past month. The police had arrived to investigate a physical assault on her in the parking lot of a downtown bar. The assailant slipped a silver coin into her pocket—an odd, jarring detail that would unknowingly trigger a cascade of obsessive thoughts. In the week that followed, Maya was already in full spiral: she could not sleep, she could not eat, she could not let herself step outside. The stressors that fed the crisis were many: a sudden loss of her marketing job two weeks earlier, a history of alcohol use, a family pedigree of OCD and anxiety, a long‑standing diagnosis of type 2 diabetes mellitus that had been poorly controlled for years, and an unresolved sadness that had been drifting at the back of her mind since high school.

---

### Week 1 – The Trigger and Early Intrusion

**Day 1** – The assault. The silver coin left behind. Maya’s mind fixated on whether she had left it in an alley, on an elevator, on her own phone. “If it’s still there, it’s a threat,” she whispered to herself. Sleep fell away as her thoughts spiraled; she stayed awake for 24 hours, glancing at the coin in the refrigerator until it seemed to vanish. She began to feel an unrelenting fear that strangers might approach her again.

**Day 2** – Obsessions intensified. She became preoccupied with the possibility that the coin could be a weapon or a bomb. The urge to keep checking the coin, to search every nook of her apartment, grew into frantic compulsions. She woke up at 3 a.m., ran a second full circuit of the apartment, and laid the coin in a new spot, only to see it again in a different context. Panic attacks began—sudden, shaking, as if she were drowning in an invisible tide.

**Day 3** – Social anxiety flared. At her apartment complex’s community center, she noticed a group of neighbors chatting, a group that she felt she couldn’t join because “they would think I’m crazy.” She avoided the hallway entirely, staying inside for the rest of the day and night. Her blood glucose hovered at 250 mg/dL at two points when she was on the brink of a panic attack; she avoided checking sugar because checking seemed to reinforce the anxiety.

---

### Week 2 – Escalation and First Medical Contact

**Day 7** – Panic and depressive mood collided. She woke up and could not summon the energy to get her diabetic insulin or check her glucose. She made a phone call to her mother, “I’m not sleeping. I can’t breathe.” The voice on the other end of the line was distant. Her mother suggested an appointment with her primary care doctor, hoping to get something that would “make her feel better.”

**Day 9** – At the urgent‑care clinic, Maya admitted, “I keep seeing that coin. I keep feeling like I’m about to die.” The nurse took her vitals: BP 138/88, HR 98, glucose 260 mg/dL. She was given a short injection of lorazepam to calm the acute panic; the medication produced a brief relief but her intrusive thoughts persisted.

**Day 10** – Discharged with a referral to psychology and a prescription for sertraline 25 mg nightly. She also got a glucose meter and a diabetes education sheet, because her doctor noted: “If you’re not checking your blood sugar you’re missing clues to your overall mental state.” Maya left the clinic feeling a little relieved but mostly confused. She was told to call an anxiety hotline if symptoms worsened.

---

### Week 3 – First Psychiatric Consultation

**Day 14** – Maya’s first outpatient psychiatric visit. She was taken into a calm room and asked: “Tell me what’s been happening when you think about the coin?” She described a sense of impending doom, a compulsion to check and re‑check the coin, and the inability to sleep because the thought persisted. The psychiatrist noted the presence of major depressive symptoms—low mood, lack of interest in her cat, tearful at the mention of friends. The patient also confessed a pattern of nightly drinking: a bottle of wine to “sleep” followed by feelings of guilt.

The psychiatrist used a brief “Yale‑Brown Obsessive Compulsion Scale” to gauge severity (score 27, high). He started sertraline at 25 mg nightly (increase to 50 mg next week, if tolerated). A cognitive‑behavioural therapist (CBT) was scheduled for a joint session. An initial psycho‑education plan was set: sleep hygiene, routine of avoiding alcohol, and a glucose‑monitoring schedule. For the next session, Maya would practice “exposure with response prevention” (ERP) where she would deliberately not check the coin for 1–2 minutes at a time.

**Day 17** – The first exposure session. With the therapist’s guidance, Maya kept her phone out of her hand, turned off the lights, and walked through the hallway without looking at the coin for 3 minutes. The anxiety surged; her heart rate spiked. After the session, Maya reported that her heart rate was still high but she could see a slight drop after the 4th minute. She left feeling “stunned,” a typical response to initial ERP.

---

### Week 4–6 – Medication Dose Increase and Comorbidity Management

**Day 24** – Sertraline increased to 50 mg nightly (tolerated). She began noticing a small, but measurable decline in the intensity of intrusive thoughts: from a "constant chatter at 10/10" to 6/10 at night. She also slept through most of a third of an hour, often waking around 4 a.m., though she still struggled to find a new pattern.

**Day 27** – First follow‑up with her primary care provider for diabetes. She reported irregular glucose readings and missed doses of insulin. The PCP reviewed her insulin regimen and added a basal insulin adjustment. The PCP also referred her to a dietician who recommended a carbohydrate‑controlled meal plan and suggested that she limit alcohol consumption to no more than one drink per day, if at all.

**Day 35** – Maya began a weekly alcohol‑use counseling group. She confessed a pattern of “coping drinking” after the assaults and in social situations. The group leader provided a “stop‑drink” plan and offered peer support. By week 11, Maya reported having had three sobriety check‑ins during the month and a near‑complete abstinence.

---

### Week 7–10 – Progress and Relapse Attempts

**Day 42** – On a routine CBT session, the therapist noted that Maya still had the compulsion to check the coin, but now she’d check the coin no more than 3–4 times a day (instead of 15). She described a “panic threshold” lower than before. In the session, she practiced an ERP exercise where she purposely left the coin in a cupboard, turned the lights off, and walked out of her apartment while the therapist timed a 10‑minute exposure. She had heart racing but survived. By the end of the session, she claimed a sense of “power back” over the obsession.

**Day 49** – A panic attack episode after a work‑related email, where Maya felt “it’s over.” She called her therapist, and they performed a mini‑breathing exercise. Sertraline seemed to have a delayed effect on panic. She also reported an elevated glucose reading at 300 mg/dL when she had a coffee and a pastry before a meeting. Her dietician recommended a low‑glycemic index snack.

**Day 58** – A brief relapse: Maya began checking the coin 8 times during the day after seeing someone else holding a coin. She realized the pattern and wrote a note to herself: “You’re not a threat.” The note was a small but powerful tool. The therapist used a “self‑talk” technique, praising her for recognizing the compulsion.

---

### Week 11–13 – Stabilization and Consolidation

**Day 65** – Maya reports sleeping through the night with only brief awakenings at 5 a.m. She no longer feels the need to re‑check the coin. She has reduced her insomnia from 6 hours to 5.5 hours nightly. Her blood sugar has stabilized between 120–180 mg/dL in fasting. She lost 5 pounds on a balanced diet plan and reported that she no longer feels guilt over her drinking habits.

**Day 71** – A comprehensive review with her psychiatrist: sertraline 50 mg nightly plus a tapering 20 mg schedule for the next two weeks. The therapist recommends continuing ERP every other week and practicing mindfulness meditation to anchor herself during intrusive thoughts. The diabetes educator shows her a trend graph and the value of consistent glucose monitoring.

**Day 78** – Maya’s social anxiety has noticeably improved. She joins a book club without checking her phone after minutes of conversation. She has started a part‑time job at a local community center—a step forward that reduces both financial and psychological stress. She feels a sense of control that was missing after her job loss and the assault.

---

### Week 13–15 – Follow‑up and Outcome

**Day 86** – Maya meets once more with the CBT therapist. Her symptoms are reported as mild: occasional intrusive images of coins that she acknowledges, but she now refrains from any checks. Her depressive mood has also improved; she finds the cat’s purr enjoyable again. She no longer uses alcohol as a coping mechanism.

**Day 94** – A final psychiatric assessment: OCD severity score at 6/28. Mood depressed at 4/30 but with no suicidal ideation. The psychiatrist writes a note of encouragement and continues sertraline and optional short‑term CBT maintenance.

**Day 100 (Final Clinical Visit)** – Maya leaves the clinic with a plan: - Continue sertraline 50 mg nightly for at least 6 months. - Attend CBT once a month for maintenance. - Keep a daily log of glucose and mood rating. - Practice a 5‑minute breathing exercise whenever she feels intrusive anxiety.

She walks out with a light feeling, a sense that the coin is nothing more than a silver coin again, and a new confidence that her life can be lived beyond the panic, depression, and compulsions.

---

### Summary

The acute OCD crisis that began with an assault and spiraled into obsessive checks, panic attacks, and depression was addressed through a multi‑disciplinary plan. The progression from initial intrusive thoughts to full ERP and pharmacotherapy illustrates a typical but detailed therapeutic track. Maya’s comorbid diabetes required active glucose monitoring, while her substance use history was tackled through counseling and self‑help strategies. A careful schedule of medication titration, therapist‑led ERP, sleep hygiene, and social reintegration enabled a steady decline in symptom severity. By the end of 100 days, the narrative shows a clear shift from crisis to improvement, culminating in a stable, hopeful outlook.
