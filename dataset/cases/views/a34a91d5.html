On the cusp of his seventh decade, Harold Jenkins was a 68‑year‑old mechanic who had spent more of his life beneath the hood of cars than at the pharmacy. He was robust in his own way; the few inches of excess weight that clung to his hips and waist were the stubborn remnants of a lifetime of manual labor, a high‑fat diet sprinkled with the occasional bar of dark chocolate, and a family history that whispered of prostate woes. His father had been diagnosed with prostate cancer at 62 and his brother—only 55—was still in the second year of adjuvant therapy after a radical prostatectomy years ago. Harold knew the risks: African‑American ancestry, the BRCA2 mutation his geneticist had identified in 2016, metabolic syndrome undercut by poorly controlled type 2 diabetes and hypertension, and a heavy, smoked‑out diet that could only describe the slow burning of his risk factors.

The first red flag appeared three weeks before his last routine visit to Mr. Ramirez, the family physician at the downtown clinic, when Harold began to notice that the urine he exhaled from his bladder felt “thicker” and took a little longer to finish. He dismissed it as a benign consequence of an aging prostate. But when he began to experience a “sticking” sensation, a constant post‑void pressure in the lower abdomen, and a need to double‑void after his bathroom visits, he decided to make an appointment with Dr. Ramirez. The visit was brief; Mr. Ramirez noted that Harold’s blood pressure readings had been hovering in the 130s/80s over the last month and that his fasting glucose had slipped to 140 mg/dL, a hint that his diabetes was slipping out of control. Harold complained of an intermittent nocturia that had become more frequent and “unbearable.” “It’s a sign, I reckon,” Harold muttered before leaving, “they’ll have to do more than just give me a tamsulosin again.”

Two days later Harold was brought to the emergency department by his wife as a new symptom emerged—a sharp, tearing pain radiating from his perineum into his lower back while standing up from a seated position. “Feels like a ribbon cutting through my belly,” he told the nurse. He also reported noticing “dark‑brown streaks” in his urine over the past week. Physical exam revealed a firm, slightly enlarged prostate on digital rectal exam, though it was difficult to differentiate from his known benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH). The attending, Dr. Patel, ordered a PSA (Prostate‑Specific Antigen) test, which came back markedly elevated at 23 ng/mL—far higher than the age‑adjusted reference range of less than 2.5 ng/mL. A urinalysis confirmed hematuria, and a basic metabolic panel confirmed hyperglycemia and mild renal dysfunction.

The patient was scheduled for a transrectal ultrasound‑guided needle biopsy of the prostate the following week. On the day of the procedure, Harold’s breath was still heavy, but he seemed relatively stable: his blood pressure was 138/84, his pulse 106 bpm, and his oxygen saturation 97 %. During the biopsy, multiple suspicious nodules were noted in the posterior left lobe. The pathologist, after a brief consultation, reported a Gleason score of 5 + 4 = 9, suggestive of a high‑grade, aggressive tumor. Furthermore, immunohistochemical staining for BRCA2 mutation was positive, confirming a genetic underpinning to his aggressive disease.

He was admitted for definitive staging. Urgent magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) of the pelvis revealed a mass protruding from the prostate into the bladder neck and a second extension into the rectal wall—a T4 staging. The MRI also showed an enlarged, suspicious right external iliac lymph node. A staging CT scan of the chest, abdomen, and pelvis revealed no distant metastases at that point. His multidisciplinary tumor board, presided over by Dr. Lin, a radiation oncologist, and Dr. Moreno, a urologic oncologist, convened over the next day to chart a course. Harold’s comorbidities—especially his uncontrolled blood sugar, hypertension, and cardiovascular disease—warranted a careful deliberation on the feasibility of radical prostatectomy versus definitive radiotherapy.

Given the extent of local invasion, the team recommended a combined modality approach. The plan was for a radical cystoprostatectomy with en bloc resection of the bladder and rectal involvement, reconstructed with a continent ileal conduit. In addition, adjuvant external beam radiotherapy to the pelvis and androgen deprivation therapy (ADT) with leuprolide would be initiated postoperatively. Harold was counseled regarding the high morbidity associated with such an operation, including potential urinary incontinence, sexual dysfunction, and the risk of de novo decompensation of his cardiovascular status.

Harold was scheduled for surgery on October 5. Preoperative labs showed an elevated serum creatinine of 1.7 mg/dL. Dr. Patel's cardiology team conducted a quick stress test to screen for any occult coronary disease. The test came back with a non‑ischemic pattern but an abnormal diastolic function; his heart failure status was stage II. The anesthesia team adjusted medication doses accordingly, anticipating difficult fluid management because of his diabetes and pre‑existing renal impairment. On the day of surgery, his blood pressure fluctuated between 110/70 and 150/92; his hemoglobin was 13 g/dL.

The operation lasted 9 hours, with significant blood loss—over 1.5 liters, necessitating two units of packed red blood cells. Intraoperative monitoring revealed a sudden drop in blood pressure, temporarily controlled with vasopressors and crystalloid bolus. Post‑op, he was transported to the surgical ICU, where his vital signs stabilized. He was extubated on postoperative day 2. His first weeks following recovery were marked by gradual improvements in urinary control, yet he struggled with the newly reconstructed ileal conduit, requiring intermittent percutaneous catheter drainage to manage intermittent obstruction at the conduit outlet.

During his early convalescence, his diabetes remained poorly controlled, and his HbA1c was 9.8 %. The endocrinologist added a basal insulin regimen and a continuous glucose monitor. Despite this, the patient’s energy levels were low, and he expressed frustration at having to rely heavily on his wife for daily care. His cardiovascular team noted that his left ventricular ejection fraction had dropped to 45 % over a month, and he was started on low‑dose bisoprolol and spironolactone. The patient’s serum PSA on postoperative day 30 remained at 18 ng/mL, indicative of residual cancer or recurrence.

The radiation oncology team slated him for external beam radiotherapy over a 6‑week period, with a total dose of 78 Gy delivered in 1.8 Gy fractions. However, Harold’s persistent leukopenia (total WBC 3.2 K/µL) and anemia (Hgb 10.2 g/dL) made him a marginal candidate. On the 18th day of radiotherapy, he suffered a sudden onset of severe flank pain that did not improve with opioids. Urgent CT imaging revealed a ruptured left external iliac lymph node containing a sizable mass that had extended into the iliac vein, with partial occlusion of the venous drainage. This manifested as a left leg swelling and tenderness. An urgent interventional radiology procedure was performed: placement of a covered stent across the node, and a left femoral vein filter to prevent embolic events. The procedure was complicated by a minor hemoperitoneum, requiring another transfusion and further ICU monitoring.

While in ICU, Dr. Moreno visited and noted that his PSA levels had climbed to 31 ng/mL—signaling failure of local therapy and likely distant metastasis. The oncology team, realizing the window for curative intervention had closed, pivoted to a palliative management plan. Harold’s family was counseled about the high likelihood of disease progression, and the options of palliative external beam radiotherapy, chemotherapy with docetaxel, and possible enrollment in a clinical trial involving PARP inhibitors given his BRCA2 mutation were discussed.

Despite the patient’s reluctance to accept the loss of curative intent, he consented to a course of docetaxel every 3 weeks with growth factor support, along with continued ADT. He also began palliative endocrine support with a second‑generation anti‑androgen. His general condition began to worsen: a progressive fatigue that made it difficult to leave the couch, intermittent fevers that spiked to 101°F without clear source, and a new onset of neuropathic pain in the lower extremities.

His urinary conduit became intermittently obstructed, leading to several episodes of hematuria. The oncologist decided to switch him to hemodialysis for a brief period to manage fluid overload and hyperkalemia; the nephrologist noted that his serum potassium had risen to 6.3 mEq/L, a dangerous elevation in the context of his congestive heart failure.

On a bleak Sunday morning in late February, Harold awoke to a pounding migraine-like headache and the sudden loss of sensation in the medial aspect of his left leg. An urgent MRI of the brain indicated a small, ischemic infarct in the right parietal lobe. The neurology team attributed it to a hypercoagulable state precipitated by his cancer and recent vascular injury. He was started on low‑dose warfarin, but his INR oscillated widely due to his fluctuating hepatic function and poorly controlled diabetes.

By the end of March, his PSA was 75 ng/mL, and his bone scan displayed multiple hot spots along the lumbar spine, sacrum, and pelvis, indicating osseous metastasis. He was not a candidate for aggressive systemic therapy due to his declining performance status, and the palliative care team was brought in to focus on symptom control, hospice counseling, and the logistical aspects of end‑of‑life care for Harold.

Within the month that followed, Harold’s disease trajectory followed the expected pattern of an advanced, highly aggressive T4, N+ prostate cancer that had invaded the bladder and rectum, caused regional lymph node involvement, and subsequently disseminated to the bone and brain. His comorbidities—chiefly uncontrolled diabetes, hypertension, obesity, and cardiovascular disease—had compounded the toxicities of his treatments, while his limited social support and the stress of his family's involvement contributed to his deteriorating mood. In this acute case, each intervention was executed with the best clinical judgment given the constraints of the disease’s aggressiveness and the patient’s overall frailty, yet the inevitable outcome remained a progressive decline until eventual demise.
