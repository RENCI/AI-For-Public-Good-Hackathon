Mr. Rajesh Patel, a 57‑year‑old man with an established history of chronic kidney disease (CKD) stage 2, uncontrolled hypertension, type 2 diabetes mellitus, congestive heart failure (NYHA III), and liver cirrhosis Child‑Pugh B, presented to the emergency department (ED) late on a Thursday evening with a 12‑hour history of worsening right upper quadrant abdominal pain, nausea, and a fever that had started the day before. He had undergone an ERCP three weeks earlier for suspected biliary obstruction, during which iodinated contrast was administered to delineate the common bile duct. He had also been taking a complex medication list for his comorbidities: metformin 500 mg BID, lisinopril 20 mg daily, furosemide 20 mg BID, spironolactone 25 mg daily, warfarin, insulin glargine (dose titrated prior to admission), and a low‑dose prednisone taper that he had recently stopped to treat a pulmonary embolism.

In the ED, Mr Patel’s vital signs were notable for a blood pressure of 78/52 mmHg and a heart rate of 118 bpm. His respiratory rate was 22/min, and his oxygen saturation was 95 % on room air. His skin was cool and diaphoretic, and his sclerae were icteric. The physical examination revealed tender hepatomegaly, mild tenderness in the epigastric region, and mild lower extremity pitting edema. His abdomen was mildly distended, with positive shifting dullness suggesting ascites. Laboratory studies showed leukocytosis (WBC 18,200 /µL), hypochromic microcytic anemia (Hb 10.5 g/dL), thrombocytopenia (platelets 105,000 /µL), bilirubin 2.8 mg/dL, alkaline phosphatase 580 U/L, AST 87 U/L, ALT 62 U/L, and a baseline creatinine of 1.25 mg/dL (estimated GFR ≈ 58 mL/min/1.73 m²). Serum electrolytes were within normal limits, and his initial lactate was 3.2 mmol/L.

The triage team quickly suspected septic cholangitis superimposed on septic shock. A 12‑lead ECG revealed sinus tachycardia without ischemic changes. Intravenous broad‑spectrum antibiotics were initiated with piperacillin‑tazobactam 4.5 g IV q6h, and empiric vancomycin 1 g IV q12h was added pending culture results. A central venous catheter was placed in the right internal jugular vein for vasopressor support, and norepinephrine was started at 0.05 µg/kg/min to maintain MAP > 65 mmHg. Simultaneously, a 0.9 % saline bolus of 30 mL/kg was administered over the first hour of resuscitation.

Over the next six hours, Mr Patel remained hypotensive, requiring a continuous infusion of norepinephrine titrated up to 0.2 µg/kg/min. Due to his elevated baseline creatinine and the additional nephrotoxic insults (contrast medium, vancomycin, and diuretics that had been held in the ED), the medical team discontinued lisinopril, furosemide, and spironolactone. Metformin was held because of the theoretical risk of lactic acidosis in the setting of diminished renal clearance. The warfarin was held pending INR monitoring, and his insulin regimen was switched to a continuous IV infusion on day 1 in response to his hyperglycemic stress response.

By the end of day 1, the patient’s lactate had trended down to 2.4 mmol/L, but his urine output remained modest at ~1.1 mL/kg/h. His serum creatinine had climbed to 1.55 mg/dL, with a urine sediment showing bland cells and occasional epithelial casts. The clinical picture suggested early AKI precipitated by prerenal factors (hypotension and hypovolemia) and potential intrinsic injury from both contrast exposure and nephrotoxic antibiotics.

On day 2, the patient experienced persistent oliguria with an average output of 0.6 mL/kg/h, despite adequate central venous pressure readings of 8‑10 cm H₂O when on low‑dose vasopressor support. The renal service was consulted. With a baseline CKD stage 2 and a rising creatinine (2.02 mg/dL), the nephrologist confirmed the onset of KDIGO stage 2 AKI. The nephrologist recommended a restrictive fluid strategy given the patient’s congestive heart failure and cirrhosis with ascites; thus, a 1 L bolus of 0.9 % saline was administered over 4 hours, followed by strict fluid balance monitoring. The vasopressor infusion was slowly tapered as MAP approached 70 mmHg.

Blood cultures grew Klebsiella pneumoniae sensitive to piperacillin‑tazobactam; thus, vancomycin was discontinued after 48 hours, and the antibiotic regimen was de-escalated to piperacillin‑tazobactam alone. The patient’s diuretic therapy was resumed on day 3 as diuretic‑induced natriuresis improved the ascitic fluid parameters and helped preserve intravascular volume, with careful monitoring of electrolytes.

At the mid‑night of day 3, a repeat creatinine measurement was 2.3 mg/dL, and the patient’s urine output had dropped further to 0.5 mL/kg/h. His potassium rose to 5.8 mmol/L, and blood gas showed non‑anion gap metabolic acidosis (pH 7.32, bicarbonate 14 mmol/L). The nephrologist, after reviewing the patient’s complex comorbidities and risk profile, advised initiating continuous renal replacement therapy (CRRT) to manage electrolyte derangements, volume overload, and worsening renal function. A continuous venovenous hemodiafiltration circuit was set up in the ICU. Despite CRRT, the patient’s urine remained scarcely detectable, and his creatinine peaked at 3.0 mg/dL on day 5.

The worsening renal failure coincided with an escalation of hepatic dysfunction. The Child‑Pugh score rose to 10 (B) due to worsening encephalopathy, thrombocytopenia (plts 75,000/µL), and ascitic fluid analysis showing a SAAG of 1.3 g/dL but a low total protein, confirming portal hypertension. The ICU team, in concert with hepatology, began empiric lactulose for hepatic encephalopathy and considered transjugular intrahepatic portosystemic shunt (TIPS) placement, but the patient’s deteriorating renal function precluded the intervention at that time.

On day 6, Mr Patel’s clinical status deteriorated. He became tachypneic with a respiratory rate of 28/min, and his oxygen saturation dropped to 88 % on a non‑rebreather mask. He developed new left lower lobe infiltrates on chest X‑ray, suggesting a nosocomial pulmonary infection. Vancomycin, though previously discontinued, was re‑initiated, and broadening of coverage to piperacillin‑tazobactam/vancomycin and aztreonam was considered. However, the nephrologist cautioned against further nephrotoxic agents given the patient’s stage 3 AKI (creatinine > 4 mg/dL).

The family was informed of the progressive decline. A decision for comfort care and DNR status was made. The CRRT circuit was disconnected, and the patient was provided with palliative support. Mr. Patel passed away peacefully 48 hours later.

This narrative outlines a pathophysiologically complex progression of acute kidney injury in a patient with multiple chronic illnesses, highlighting how overlapping risk factors—septic shock, hypotension, contrast exposure, and polypharmacy—interacted subtly and progressively to culminate in a fatal multiorgan failure syndrome.
