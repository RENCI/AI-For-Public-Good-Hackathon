### A Tale of One Man’s Breathing Battle

**A Quiet Beginning**

On a damp August morning, Harold, a 68‑year‑old former steel‑sheeting foreman, woke with a new, sharp cough that had lingered for just over a day. The sputum, once straw‑colored and minimal, was now purplish‑brown and much thicker. He felt “tightness” in his chest and a rapid, wheezing breath that seemed louder to him than to anyone else. By evening, his breathing had become noticeably more labored, and he began to notice a faint, constant dizziness that made standing up a challenge.

He called his wife, Margaret, who urged him to rest and keep his inhalers. The inhalers—an albuterol metered‑dose inhaler and a budesonide/formoterol combination—had given him reliable relief on previous flare‑ups, but the new episode felt different. Harold’s hands shook slightly as he filled his inhaler; the shaking was not new—years of rheumatoid arthritis and his ongoing depression had made these tremors a familiar, if unwelcome, companion.

**First Visit (Day 2)**

By the next morning, with breathlessness at its apex, Harold walked into the emergency department (ED). He had a fever of 38.3 °C (100.9 °F), heart rate 108 bpm with an irregular rhythm, and a blood pressure of 140/78 mmHg. Oxygen saturation was 88 % on room air and improved to 94 % after a 2 L/min nasal cannula. A chest x‑ray showed a hyperinflated lung field with a subtle increase in infiltrate in the right lower lobe—suggestive of an emerging infection superimposed on COPD. The ECG showed left ventricular hypertrophy and occasional premature atrial complexes, raising suspicion for his known coronary artery disease (CAD). An arterial blood gas (ABG) performed while he was on 2 L/min revealed a PaO₂ of 52 mmHg and a pH of 7.24, indicative of acute respiratory acidosis.

Given the clinical picture, the attending physician called in the pulmonology team. A sputum culture was sent, and an empirical antibiotic—amoxicillin‑clavulanate—was started to cover likely bacterial agents. Since Harold’s diabetic (type 2) history had taught us to anticipate glucose spikes during steroid therapy, the endocrinologist was consulted, and a goal of 140–180 mg/dL for glucose control was set.

**Hospitalization (Days 3–5)**

By day 3, Harold was admitted to the general medicine floor. His chest pain had worsened slightly—radiating to his left arm, prompting a troponin I that was mildly elevated (0.08 ng/mL, normal < 0.04). An echo revealed preserved ejection fraction at 55 % with moderate diastolic dysfunction; the cardiologist added a low dose of carvedilol for heart failure (HF) management, recognizing the interplay between COPD exacerbations and fluid overload.

A daily nebulizer regimen with ipratropium bromide and supplemental albuterol was prescribed. The dose of oral prednisone, a common steroid for COPD exacerbations, was initiated at 30 mg/day due to the heightened concern for bacterial infection and impending worsening of airflow obstruction. The rheumatologist noted the increased steroid exposure, warning Harold of potential bone demineralization given his osteoporosis history. A daily dose of vitamin D and calcium was prescribed, along with a reminder to take his bisphosphonate as prescribed.

On day 4, the sputum culture returned positive for *Streptococcus pneumoniae*. The antibiotic was switched to ceftriaxone 1 g IV once daily for three more days, aligning with the positive culture. Harold’s diabetes management was adjusted: his oral hypoglycemics were held, and insulin was started per sliding scale protocols, especially with the known steroid‑induced hyperglycemia.

**Clinical Decision Points**

- **Steroid Management:** The endocrinologist and cardiologist collaborated to monitor Harold’s glucose nightly and adjust prednisone taper to a 10 mg daily by day 5, avoiding abrupt discontinuation that could precipitate a rebound exacerbation. - **Fluid Status & HF:** On day 3, a 2 L IV saline bolus was administered, but the HF team cautioned about fluid overload. Harold’s daily weight and jugular venous pressure were monitored, and diuretics (furosemide 40 mg BID) were initiated on day 4 to manage a mild peripheral edema that had appeared.

- **Mental Health Consideration:** The psychiatric liaison recognized that the stress of hospitalization and the persistent cough could aggravate Harold’s depression. A brief psychotherapeutic intervention was offered, and his selective serotonin reuptake inhibitor (SSRIs) dose was maintained.

**Improvement and Discharge (Day 6–7)**

By day 6, Harold's fever had resolved, oxygen saturation rested at 96 % with a nasal cannula set at 1 L/min, and his cough decreased dramatically—only a few clear sputum expectorations now. His chest pain had subsided, and his pulse rate was settling into the 90s.

The multidisciplinary team decided to discharge him on:

- Oral amoxicillin‑clavulanate 875/125 mg BID for a total of 7 days. - Oral prednisone taper: 20 mg/day (Day 7), 15 mg/day (Day 10), 10 mg/day (Day 13), 5 mg/day (Day 16), and off by Day 19. - Oral budesonide/formoterol inhaler twice daily, as per pre‑exacerbation regimen. - Oral carvedilol 12.5 mg BID. - Furosemide 40 mg BID. - Insulin sliding scale for the next 48 hours post‑discharge, then conversion to basal‑bolus regimen. - Calcium 600 mg and vitamin D 800 IU daily; bisphosphonate to be taken as previously scheduled. - Antidepressant maintained on sertraline 50 mg daily.

**Follow‑up Plan**

On day 7, a telehealth follow‑up was scheduled with pulmonary medicine to check inhaler technique and assess symptom control. Margaret was instructed on inhaler coordination steps, ensuring that even with Harold's tremors, the inhalation remained effective. The endocrinologist set a lab draw date for fasting blood glucose and HbA1c in 6 weeks. The cardiologist scheduled an echocardiogram in 6 months to monitor HF progression.

**Outcome**

Three weeks later, Harold reported that his breathing had returned to his baseline “windy‑day” level, and he no longer felt the heaviness in his chest. He was able to walk to the local grocery store without needing to pause for breath—a personal triumph for someone who had never been able to keep up with a young family in the past. Margaret noted that he seemed less irritable, and the weight of depression had lightened, possibly due to the stabilization of his health.

Despite the complex web of comorbidities—CAD, HF, diabetes, anemia, osteoporosis, depression, and rheumatoid arthritis—the acute exacerbation of COPD was managed successfully through a coordinated, multidisciplinary approach. Harold's continued adherence to inhaler therapy, careful monitoring of glycemic control, and management of medications across specialties marked a turning point, moving his trajectory from a potential decline to the hopeful horizon of recovery.
