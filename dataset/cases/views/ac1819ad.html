John M. was seventy years old when he first went to see his primary‑care physician, Mr. Lopez, with what seemed at first a simple set of urinary symptoms. “Mr. M. I can’t make it to the bathroom before you’re done, and I have to get up a few times in the night,” he complained over the phone in late March. His family history was a worry: his father had died of prostate cancer at 68, and his brother had been diagnosed at 59. John’s own medical record already marked him as high‑risk: a chronic smoker, a waist‑line well over 40 inches, a daily intake of processed meats and a pizza‑heavy diet, and very few servings of vegetables or fruit.

### Initial Encounter (March 2025)

Mr. Lopez noted that John’s frequent nighttime voids (“nocturia”) were now occurring six–seven times per night, and he reported a dribbling of urine after he finished emptying. “You sound a little like BPH, but we should check a PSA in case something’s wrong.” He sent for a basic metabolic panel, a lipid panel, and a PSA test. John’s fasting glucose was 165 mg/dL, confirming poorly controlled type 2 diabetes (HbA1c 9.2 %). Blood pressure read 165/90 mmHg, highlighting his hypertension, and his BMI was 33 kg/m²—a clear case of obesity.

The PSA came back 4.1 ng/mL (borderline high for a 70‑year‑old). Mr. Lopez scheduled an appointment with Dr. Patel, a urologist, to review the results.

### Urologist’s Visit (April 2025)

In the office, Dr. Patel performed a digital rectal exam (DRE) that found a slightly enlarged, indurated prostate, more typical of benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH) but not entirely reassuring. “We’ll do an ultrasound‑guided prostate biopsy to rule out cancer,” she said.

Before the biopsy, John was started on a 5‑alpha‑reductase inhibitor (dutasteride) to relieve BPH symptoms and a low‑dose α‑blocker (tamsulosin) to reduce urinary obstruction. A short course of oral antibiotics (ciprofloxacin 500 mg BID) was prescribed due to mild prostatitis symptoms he had reported—dysuria and perineal pressure—that resolved after that week.

### Biopsy Results (May 2025)

The pathology returned “adenocarcinoma of the prostate, Gleason 7 (3+4), with two cores containing cancer. No invasion beyond the prostate capsule.” The PSA had risen to 6.2 ng/mL. Dr. Patel recommended a staging imaging study—an MRI of the pelvis and a bone scan—to identify local extension and distant disease.

John’s comorbidities complicated matters. His diabetes made him susceptible to infections, his hypertension required careful monitoring of medication interactions, and his obesity posed a risk for perioperative complications.

### Imaging (June 2025)

MRI showed a 2.5 cm solid lesion in the left peripheral zone extending into the base of the prostate but no evident extraprostatic extension. The bone scan was negative for metastases. With these findings, the multidisciplinary team (Dr. Patel, oncologist Dr. Li, and radiologist Dr. Simmons) determined that John had a locally confined prostate adenocarcinoma with a moderate risk profile (Gleason 7, PSA > 4, < 10 ng/mL, T2).

Given John’s significant urinary symptoms from BPH, Dr. Patel elected to proceed with a transurethral resection of the prostate (TURP) in early July, hoping to both relieve obstruction and obtain a larger tissue sample for staging. The procedure was complicated by a postoperative urinary leak that required a second procedure weeks later. His recovery was slow, partly due to his diabetes and obesity, which delayed wound healing.

### Follow‑Up (August–September 2025)

After the TURP, John complained of persistent pelvic pain and a feeling of heaviness in the groin that was not relieved by pain medications. His PSA rose to 8.5 ng/mL within a month, and MRI now showed perineural invasion and a slight extraprostatic extension toward the seminal vesicles. The team discussed definitive treatment options: radical prostatectomy, external beam radiotherapy (EBRT), or hormone therapy.

John’s history of hypertension and risk of cardiovascular events made the surgical team cautious. Dr. Patel suggested he undergo EBRT with short‑course androgen deprivation therapy (ADT) to minimize surgical complications. John, however, was reluctant to start testosterone‑blocking drugs due to concerns about bone density and cardiovascular risk. After a thorough discussion, he consented to a 6‑month course of leuprolide, understanding that this would reduce PSA aggressively and help control symptom spread.

### Hormone Therapy (October 2025 – March 2026)

During the first six months of leuprolide therapy, John’s PSA fell to 2.2 ng/mL. He reported that his urinary frequency had decreased significantly; the nocturia episodes were now limited to 2–3 times per night. However, the androgen suppression caused a significant weight gain (5–7 kg) and a rise in blood pressure to 180/95 mmHg. His glycemic control worsened, with fasting glucose hovering around 200 mg/dL and HbA1c rising to 9.8 %. Dr. Patel and his PCP adjusted his antihypertensive regimen—adding an ACE inhibitor—and his endocrinologist added a GLP‑1 receptor agonist to help with weight and glucose.

Meanwhile, John developed dull, aching pain in his lower back and left hip. X-rays revealed osteoblastic lesions in the lumbar vertebrae and left femoral neck. A repeat bone scan confirmed metastasis to the bone. The multidisciplinary team held a tumor board meeting in April 2026 and concluded that John’s disease had progressed beyond the prostate, despite hormone therapy.

### Deterioration (May–June 2026)

John’s pain intensified, requiring chronic opioid therapy. His PSA had climbed back to 13 ng/mL. He experienced fatigue, weight loss of 8 kg (~10%) over two months, and intermittent worsening of urinary obstruction. He was eventually admitted to the hospital for an acute exacerbation of urinary retention, requiring intermittent catheterization. During a hospital stay, a urinary tract infection (UTI) was identified; treatment involved broad‑spectrum antibiotics due to his diabetes.

The oncologist proposed adding docetaxel chemotherapy to his regimen. John’s overall performance status had dropped to ECOG 2; his cardiologist cautioned against any treatment that could further strain his heart. An echocardiogram was normal, but his blood pressure remained poorly controlled. The team ultimately decided against systemic chemotherapy, citing diminishing quality of life and the risk of exacerbating his comorbidities.

### Palliative Phase (July 2026 and beyond)

By mid‑2026, John’s disease had become widespread and painful. He and his family agreed on a palliative care approach. Pain was managed with a multimodal regimen—including opioids, nerve blocks, and bisphosphonates. His PSA remained elevated at 18 ng/mL, and repeat imaging showed further progression to the ribs and pelvis.

The medical team continued to adjust his anti‑hypertensives, optimized his diabetes management, and coordinated regular physical therapy to maintain mobility. He also received counseling services to cope with the psychological impact of the disease trajectory.

### Reflection

John’s case illustrates a classic, yet accelerated, progression of prostate adenocarcinoma in the context of common comorbidities. The presence of BPH, prostatitis, hypertension, type 2 diabetes, and obesity compounded each other: BPH initially masked early cancer symptoms; prostatitis and infections necessitated recurrent antibiotic use; the metabolic syndrome contributed to poorer wound healing and increased systemic inflammation, likely accelerating tumor growth.

From a primary‑care visit for nocturia in March 2025 to uncontrolled, metastatic disease in July 2026, less than 14 months had passed. Despite aggressive hormone therapy and radiologic interventions, the tumor continued to evolve, ultimately requiring a shift from curative intent to palliative care. His outcome—worsening and ultimately undiagnosed as a curable disease—highlights the importance of early, coordinated care and continuous monitoring in high‑risk populations.
