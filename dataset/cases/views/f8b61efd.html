**The Rapid Descent of Mr. James H. – A Journey through Stage IV Melanoma and Chronic Illness**

---

#### A Rough Beginning (Week 0–1)

James “Jim” H., a 58‑year‑old office coordinator with fair, freckled skin, walked into his primary‑care office with a dull, persistent ache in the upper right side of his abdomen. “It’s been there for about a month,” he said, holding his belly. “I’ve been feeling tired all the time, and I’ve lost about 10 pounds, even though I’m eating as usual.” Jim also complained of a low‑grade fever and a vague sense of fatigue that was keeping him from his morning walk despite a short inhaler rescue. His medical history was long and complicated: type 2 diabetes poorly controlled on metformin and glargine, hypertension on lisinopril, obesity (BMI 33), chronic liver disease (cirrhosis secondary to chronic alcohol use), and chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) from a 40‑pack‑year smoking history. In 2015, he received a kidney transplant following end‑stage renal disease; he is maintained on tacrolimus and mycophenolate.

Jim’s family had a history of melanoma in his maternal aunt, who had died at 62 after a delayed diagnosis. The family had never performed a comprehensive skin check, and Jim himself had never had a dermatologist’s exam. He recalled a dark, evolving mole on the upper back that had grown gradually over the past fifteen years.

During the visit, Jim’s blood pressure was 148/84 mm Hg, heart rate 92 bpm, and oxygen saturation 95 % on room air. Lab work showed:

- ALT 112 U/L, AST 98 U/L, ALP 210 U/L, total bilirubin 3.2 mg/dL - INR 1.5, serum albumin 2.8 g/dL - Glucose 178 mg/dL, HbA1c 8.2 % - Normal CBC, mild leukocytosis 13,000/mm³

Given the abnormal liver panel and the presence of an abdominal mass sign on exam, the PCP ordered an abdominal ultrasound. The ultrasound showed multiple hypoechoic lesions in the liver, ranging from 2 cm to 4 cm. The radiologist also noted mild ascites. Jim was referred urgently to hepatology.

---

#### The Diagnosis (Week 2–3)

In a hepatology clinic two days later, Chris T, a hepatologist, reviewed the ultrasound. “We’re looking at a picture that fits metastatic disease,” he said, pointing to the nodular shapes on the screen. An abdominal CT scan confirmed numerous hepatic lesions, some with centrally necrotic cores. No significant portal hypertension was observed, but the cirrhotic background made the liver appear nodular. The CT was then sent to pathology for biopsy.

A week later, a laparoscopic liver biopsy was performed. Histology revealed malignant melanoma cells with characteristic dendritic dendrons. Immunohistochemistry was positive for S100, HMB‑45, and SOX10. A genetic panel identified a BRAF V600E mutation.

In the same week, Jim was referred to dermatology. A skin examination uncovered a 1.8 cm, black, irregularly bordered nodule on the right upper back, with satellite lesions and ulceration. A punch biopsy of the back lesion revealed classic nodular melanoma, Clark level IV, Breslow depth 3.2 mm, high mitotic index, and ulceration. The pathology report confirmed that the liver lesions were metastases from this primary melanoma.

By the end of the third week, Jim had been formally staged: T3b N0 M1c (liver metastases only). In a multidisciplinary tumor board, oncologists, a melanoma surgical specialist, and the transplant nephrologist convened to decide a course of action. With his active immunosuppression and cirrhosis, the discussion hinged on balancing therapeutic potency against hepatic toxicity and the risk of transplant rejection.

---

#### A Cautious Beginning (Month 1–2)

On the first day of oncology care, Dr. Patel explained, “Given the BRAF mutation, we can use a combination of dabrafenib and trametinib, but we must watch for hepatic failure. Also, because you’re on tacrolimus, we cannot give a checkpoint inhibitor like nivolumab; that could trigger rejection of your kidney.” The nephrologist confirmed that tacrolimus trough levels must stay below 7 ng/mL to avoid nephrotoxicity and that the patient is at high risk for rejection if immunotherapy is started.

Jim’s therapy was initiated at a dose ½ the standard for patients with normal liver function: dabrafenib 50 mg BID and trametinib 0.5 mg QD. The transplant team had to adjust tacrolimus from 5 mg BID to 2.5 mg BID and schedule extra trough monitors.

During the first two weeks of treatment, Jim tolerated the regimen with mild fatigue, a transient fever, and a low‑grade maculopapular rash that resolved with topical steroids. His liver enzymes rose modestly: ALT 170 U/L, AST 140 U/L, but remained under 3× the upper limit. His nephrologist noted a slight rise in creatinine to 1.4 mg/dL. The transplant team held tacrolimus for 48 h and then resumed a lower dose, keeping troughs at 4–5 ng/mL.

In week 7 of therapy, Jim presented to the emergency department with worsening abdominal pain, confusion, and a tremor of the upper right hand. He was diaphoretic, with a blood pressure of 112/68 mm Hg. Labs revealed:

- ALT 310 U/L, AST 250 U/L, total bilirubin 5.5 mg/dL, INR 2.3 - Serum albumin 2.4 g/dL - Creatinine 1.5 mg/dL - No leukocytosis

The picture was one of acute-on-chronic liver failure: increased transaminases, rising bilirubin, and coagulopathy. Jim was admitted to the hepatology service and started on lactulose for encephalopathy and spironolactone 100 mg/amiloride 50 mg for ascites. Meanwhile, the oncology team paused dabrafenib and trametinib to allow liver recovery.

---

#### The Tipping Point (Month 3–4)

After a week of supportive care, Jim’s liver function began to stabilize. ALT and AST decreased to 210 U/L, but bilirubin remained 4.8 mg/dL. His encephalopathy improved with lactulose titration. At the end of the first month on holding therapy, the oncology team re‑initiated dabrafenib, now at 37.5 mg BID, and trametinib at 0.375 mg QD. The nephrologist carefully increased tacrolimus back to 3 mg BID, with trough targets of 4–5 ng/mL.

Three weeks after resumption, a follow‑up CT scan of the abdomen revealed that one of the larger liver lesions had enlarged from 4.2 cm to 5.1 cm, while the others were relatively stable in size. A skin exam this week found no new lesions. There were no new bone or brain metastases. The response was indeterminate: partial at best but clearly not a sustained clinical benefit.

Jim’s diabetes remained hard to control, with fasting glucose fluctuations to 220 mg/dL and post‑prandial spikes above 300 mg/dL. He was given a higher dose of insulin glargine and added basal‑bolus therapy. Unfortunately, his COPD flare at that time forced a re‑introduction of oral steroids (prednisone 30 mg daily) for two weeks, which further complicated glycemic control and raised his blood pressure to 160/95 mm Hg.

The transplant nephrologist was concerned about tacrolimus levels spiking to 9 ng/mL during the steroid flare; they reduced the dose to 1.5 mg BID to avoid rejection.

In late month 4, Jim was admitted once more for a new episode of confusion and vomiting. Labs indicated worsening liver function (ALT 420 U/L, AST 350 U/L, bilirubin 6.7 mg/dL, INR 3.2), rising creatinine 2.0 mg/dL, and mild hyponatremia. He was treated in the ICU with continuous lactulose infusion, diuretics for refractory ascites, and empiric antibiotics for suspected spontaneous bacterial peritonitis (ascitic fluid cultures negative but WBC high). The nephrology team switched tacrolimus to cyclosporine 150 mg BID for the short term, hoping to reduce its hepatic metabolism burden.

---

#### The Last Weeks (Month 5–6)

After four days of ICU care, Jim was transferred to a step‑down unit where outpatient oncologic therapy was re‑established. The oncology team decided to switch to a single‑agent immunotherapy—pembrolizumab—despite the transplant risk, as the patient’s prognosis had become dire and the potential for systemic benefit outweighed the risk of rejection. The transplant nephrologist placed a high dose of steroids (prednisone 60 mg daily) to mitigate rejection risk concurrently. In the meantime, a full-dose cyclosporine was maintained.

The first infusion of pembrolizumab was well tolerated; however, within a week, blood counts showed a drop in platelets to 75,000, and ALT climbed to 580 U/L. The immunotherapy was held, and the patient was put on a low‑dose steroid taper. Unfortunately, the liver enzymes kept climbing despite repeated interventions; at the end of month 6, his total bilirubin had reached 10.2 mg/dL, INR 4.1, sodium 130 mmol/L, creatinine 3.0 mg/dL. He developed generalized edema, refractory ascites, and grade 3 hepatic encephalopathy.

Given the terminal trajectory, Jim’s family met with the oncology, hepatology, and transplant teams to discuss hospice care. The palliative care service took over, and pain control was maintained with a fentanyl patch and oral oxycodone. Jim spent the last weeks of life at home, resting on a couch while his wife and son sat nearby. He passed peacefully at 02:12 AM after a brief period of confusion and a final, brief breath, with the hospice nurse standing beside him and his family holding his hand.

---

#### Retrospective Summary

When the trajectory of Mr. James’s disease is traced chronologically, the interplay of comorbidities, risk factors, and therapeutic constraints becomes stark. The combination of immunosuppression for transplant, underlying cirrhosis, COPD exacerbations, and poorly controlled diabetes each compounded the aggressiveness and treatment toxicity of metastatic melanoma. The early decision to use targeted therapy mitigated the risk of transplant rejection but was limited by hepatic metabolism, while the eventual switch to immunotherapy—though potentially more effective against melanoma—triggered significant hepatotoxicity and kidney strain.

At every clinical decision point, the multidisciplinary team weighed the benefits of systemic therapy against the imminent risk of organ failure. The chronic nature of Jim’s cirrhosis and COPD, coupled with his immunosuppression, prevented the use of the most effective melanoma treatments (checkpoint inhibitors) until the disease was refractory to all other options, at which point the treatment plan was altered in a last‑minute attempt to achieve a clinical response. Unfortunately, the cumulative burden of disease and therapy led to rapid hepatic decompensation and, ultimately, a terminal decline that could not be reversed.

What remained, however, was a patient’s determination to endure each week, a family’s steadfast support, and a team’s compassionate care that honored Jim’s dignity even as his body failed in a predictable, inexorable decline. The timeline and clinical decisions recorded here can serve as a guide for physicians navigating the complex balance between aggressive treatment of metastatic melanoma and the realities imposed by comorbid chronic illnesses.
