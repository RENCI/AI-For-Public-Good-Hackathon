**A Narrative of Maria C.’s Major Depressive Episode and Progress Toward Recovery**

**April 1 – “The First Night of Waking”**

Maria, a 38‑year‑old woman, came to the urban primary‑care clinic feeling *“constantly drained”* and *“worse than ever”*. She reported that for the last month her appetite had shrunk to a couple of spoonfuls of oatmeal each morning, and she had lost 12 kg (26 lb) in that same period. Her sleep had become a frantic scramble: she could only fall asleep after a full hour of tossing, and when she did, she woke every two to three hours with a racing heart.

She also described a “thunder in her chest” that began as an odd feeling on weekends then progressed into panic attacks—short‑duration, with sweaty palms and an intense fear of fainting—in the middle of the day. When she tried to talk to anyone about her worries, she felt people would judge her; this, she admitted, had made her isolate in the last month.

Maria’s chart was dense with chronic medical problems: a 10‑year history of hypertension (currently on lisinopril 20 mg), type 2 diabetes (HbA1c 8.5 % two months ago), obesity (BMI 38), chronic lower back pain radiating to the right leg, and a long‑standing diagnosis of Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD), Social Anxiety Disorder, and Post‑Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD) stemming from a car accident she had lived with for five years. She was unemployed two months prior (job loss due to company downsizing) and had been drinking light beer one or two times a week—a pattern she reported had “just gotten out of hand.” A family history of depression and bipolar disorder in her parents further clouded her mind with dread.

**April 3 – “First Psychiatric Evaluation”**

After a brief physical exam and routine labs—CBC, CMP, fasting glucose, lipid panel—her primary‑care physician, Dr. Patel, referred Maria to a community mental‑health clinic. A psychiatrist, Dr. Nguyen, met her on April 5. She described Maria’s symptoms within DSM‑5 criteria for a Major Depressive Episode: three or more symptoms (depressed mood, anhedonia, poor concentration, feelings of worthlessness, sleep disturbance, appetite change) lasting more than two weeks, with psychosocial impairment (loss of job, strained relationships). An elevated PHQ‑9 score of 22 (severe depression) and GAD‑7 of 17 (severe anxiety) confirmed the clinical picture.

**April 6 – “Treatment Plan—Medications & Therapy”**

Dr. Nguyen initiated a dual‑track treatment plan:

1. **Medication:** Started Sertraline 25 mg daily (a “proven” SSRI with anxiolytic properties), noting that the patient’s chronic pain, hypertension, and diabetes made duloxetine a potential future alternative because of its dual SNRI effect on pain.

2. **Therapeutic Modalities:** - Cognitive‑Behavioral Therapy (CBT) for depression and anxiety, 12 weekly 60‑minute sessions, starting April 12. - Trauma‑Focused CBT (specific to PTSD) for the car‑accident trauma, concurrently with general CBT, with an additional 8 sessions slated for June. - Substance‑Use Counseling (motivational interviewing), one 45‑minute session initially, then weekly group sessions at the community center.

3. **Lifestyle & Social Support:** - Referral to a dietitian for diabetes‑friendly weight‑loss plan. - Enrollment with a local social worker to explore unemployment benefits, small‑business aid, and financial counseling. - Introduction to a chronic pain clinic on April 20 for physical therapy and pain management review.

**April 12–25 – “First Weeks of Medication”**

Maria started sertraline on the evening of April 6. Within the first week she noticed a slight, but welcome, reduction in the intensity of her panic attacks – they became brief, lasting only 10–12 minutes instead of an hour. By the second week, she reported sleeping through the night about half of the time and feeling “a little less numb” when she went to the gym for her usual low‑impact cardio. However, her weight remained stable at 122 kg, and she still avoided conversations.

**May 5 – “Clinical Decision Point–Sertraline Titration”**

At her first follow‑up visit, Dr. Nguyen noted that Maria’s PHQ‑9 had dropped to 18 and GAD‑7 to 13. She still reported a loss of interest in almost all activities, appetite decreased further (now minimal snacking), and she denied suicidal ideation. Given partial response, sertraline was increased to 50 mg nightly. A follow‑up appointment was set for May 12.

**May 12 – “Escalation of Therapy & Substance Use Assessment”**

Dr. Nguyen praised Maria’s engagement and highlighted her improvement in pain management (she was tolerating a new physical therapy regimen focused on lumbar strengthening). She noted Maria’s substance use: the patient had admitted to 2‑3 beers on weekdays and 4 on weekends, but the frequency had decreased from “nearly daily” to “once every other day.” In the interim, Maria had started a short‑term “detox” workshop (24 hours of detox and an after‑care intake) that had helped her become acutely aware of withdrawal symptoms and craving patterns. Dr. Nguyen decided to continue with the 50 mg dosage and maintain the same therapy structure.

**June 1 – “Duloxetine Trial and Pain Control”**

In June, Maria’s chronic back pain had become worse after a recent lifting incident at a volunteer event. The pain clinic reviewed her plan: while sertraline was working for mood, it wasn’t addressing neuropathic pain. Dr. Nguyen offered a switch to duloxetine 30 mg nightly—a medication that could address both depression and nerve pain—and informed Maria of potential side effects (nausea, dry mouth) and need for monitoring of blood pressure and glucose levels.

Maria consented. The week after the switch, her PHQ‑9 dropped to 13, and her back pain rating decreased from 8/10 to 5/10. Her anxiety symptoms lessened; GAD‑7 fell to 9.

**June 15 – “Stabilization & Lifestyle Gains”**

During her physical therapy appointment, Maria reported that her energy levels had improved sufficiently that she could hike a 3‑mile trail for the first time in months. She had regained a small amount of appetite and was eating a balanced diet; her dietitian noted a 4 lb weight loss in four weeks (116 kg). Her HbA1c had improved to 7.8 %—a measurable benefit from better control of blood glucose via diet and exercise. Her blood pressure was now a stable 132 / 80 under duloxetine and lisinopril therapy.

**July 1 – “CBT Success & Social Anxiety Breakthrough”**

By the seventh CBT session, Maria had successfully identified one negative core belief—“I am useless and will never find work”—and was working to challenge it. She had also begun to “invite” a coworker (at the community center she volunteered) to a coffee break, despite the social anxiety fear. She reported feeling proud and her PHQ‑9 fell to 9. She also noted the “panic spikes” were less frequent (1–2 per week instead of daily).

**August 3 – “Reaching the ‘Improving’ Milestone”**

Maria’s PHQ‑9 was now 6 (minimal depression) and GAD‑7 was 5 (mild anxiety). She had returned to part‑time work at a local nonprofit, earning a modest stipend, which reduced her financial strain. Her social worker helped her apply for a small‑business loan to start a freelance web‑design service based on her graphic‑design portfolio—a project that had been a dream for years. She had ceased all alcohol consumption during the past 10 weeks, verified by a negative urine screen during a scheduled group counseling session.

**August 20 – “Final Clinical Decision‐Point: Medication Tapering”**

Given the sustained improvement, Dr. Nguyen and Maria discussed tapering duloxetine. Maria agreed to maintain her dose for two more months to ensure stability, with an additional check of her pain scales and blood sugar. Dr. Nguyen emphasized continued engagement in therapy even after medication taper, but Maria felt confident and was planning a modest “maintenance” CBT schedule of one bi‑monthly check‑in.

**September 15 – “Course of Recovery”**

One month after the planned duloxetine taper, Maria reported feeling “more like myself than before.” Her PHQ‑9 was 2 (no clinically significant depression), and she had maintained her weight and blood sugar within target ranges. Her anxiety, while still present, had become manageable—she could attend social gatherings with brief, pre‑planned self‑regulation techniques taught in CBT. Her chronic back pain was now only mild, and she had begun taking a 30‑minute walk in the park every morning.

Her journey, while marked by severe early symptoms and a constellation of comorbid conditions, had progressed through a structured, multi‑modal intervention. Family health history, financial hardship, and hormonal shifts had initially amplified her risk, but a coordinated approach that addressed medication, psychotherapy, substance use, chronic pain, and psychosocial factors fostered a trajectory toward recovery. The story left an outline for a future where Maria could continue to thrive, with periodic clinical checkpoints to forestall relapse and maintain her hard‑earned resilience.
