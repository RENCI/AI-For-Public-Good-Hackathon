When the last summer of his third decade of life hit, the 35‑year‑old man—who most afternoons spent hauling heavy boxes in a downtown warehouse—felt a vague, uncomfortable cramp in his lower abdomen that he chalked up to “overworking” and the city’s endless traffic. He had been battling ulcerative colitis (UC) for eight years, a history that began when a rash of bloody, frothy stools had abruptly cut back a routine that once seemed trivial. The diagnosis had come with a set of pills—mesalamine and a short course of steroids—followed by a plateau of quiescent periods punctuated by “flare‑ups” that he endured by doing what he thought best: skip doses whenever he was on the go, let his stress at work dictate his diet, and, when necessary, take a handful of over‑the‑counter NSAIDs for the lingering back pain that never quite budged.

The flare that began with the abdominal cramp was no ordinary flareup. In the ensuing days he reported a new pattern of bowel movements that was frequent yet unpredictable. At first, he told his friends that perhaps he had caught a cold: the stools were mixed green and brown, with a faint tingling in the rectal area that he mistook for a "strep throat" that never came. The first time he went to the office of his PCP, a family nurse practitioner, the symptoms were mild. His vital signs looked normal. A complete blood count returned with a hemoglobin of 10.9 g/dL, a microcytic picture that his doctor had long associated with his known iron deficiency anemia secondary to chronic UC. The patient was already on oral iron and monthly B12 injections, a combination that kept him from the full-blown pallor that would otherwise have followed.

“What’s the plan? Keep doing your meds, eat iron‑rich foods? Anything else?” the practitioner asked.

The young man laughed, rolled his eyes, and said, “I don’t have the time or the money for these appointments. I’m good.”

He left the office with a prescription for an extended‑release mesalamine but was, again, unlikely to fill it. The week progressed, and he found himself having two to three bloody stools each day. The pain that had started as a cramp migrated to a deeper level of the abdomen, a dull ache that was sometimes sharp when he forced himself up from an overturned chair. He did what most people do in such circumstances: he tried to “drink more water,” eat “well,” and, when his job’s hours became too demanding, took diclofenac from a friend who had chronic back pain.

Three weeks after the initial cramp, he found himself in a hurry to the emergency department after a sudden, 12‑hour bout of dehydration, low‑grade fevers (not more than 101 °F), and a sudden spike in the frequency of stools—up to 12 times a day—with thick, bright‑red clots that made his clothes feel sticky. The nursing staff noted his blood pressure 95/57 mmHg, pulse 110 bpm, respiratory rate 22, and a low central venous pressure.

The ED physicians ran a full panel: CBC, CMP, CRP, ESR, coagulation studies, and a stool series for C. difficile toxin and bacterial pathogens. The results were telling. Hemoglobin was 9.8 g/dL, WBC count 18.6 K/µL with a shift to the left, CRP 38 mg/L, ESR 94 mm/hr. The stool toxins came back negative, the cultures were bland. The picture fit a severe flare of ulcerative colitis, with a hint of a superimposed infection—a possibility that was being considered as the treatment plan unfolded.

The attending intensivist, noticing the patient’s comorbidities, specifically the documented primary sclerosing cholangitis (PSC) with a mild rise in alkaline phosphatase a couple of months earlier (now 280 U/L, 1.5 times the upper limit of normal), wanted to double‑check the biliary tree. At the same time, the patient’s known calcium oxalate kidney stones had recurred in the summer of the previous year; the patient had endured a painful flank ache that had lingered for a week, leading him to a urologist who discovered a 7 mm proximal ureteric stone on an ultrasound. In the ED, they decided to monitor his urine output, give him high‑dose IV fluids, and start him on a cautious hydration plan with a clear fluid input. The decision for a CT abdomen/pelvis without contrast was urgent, as the attending physician suspected worsening colitis with a possible perforation.

The CT report described diffuse colonic wall thickening (12 mm of the sigmoid, 10 mm the descending) with a pericolic fat stranding consistent with severe inflammatory changes, but nothing beyond that; no free air, no abscess formation. The kidneys were normal aside from the known stone in the right proximal ureter, causing mild hydroureter but no obstruction to the point of an emergency intervention.

The ED team, aware of the patient’s high thrombo‑embolic risk due to systemic inflammation (C‑reactive protein so high that it could tip the balance towards a hypercoagulable state) and his recent prolonged sedentary shift, decided on a prophylactic low‑molecular‑weight heparin protocol pending discharge.

The next morning, after a slow improvement in hydration, the patient was admitted to the Gastroenterology service. In the hospital, his flares were escalated with a course of intravenous methylprednisolone 125 mg every 8 hours, a fluid resuscitation at 1.5 × 10^9 mL, and a high‑dose oral mesalamine 4.8 g daily. The blood work showed a gradual downtrend in CRP but the hemoglobin kept dipping, falling to 8.3 g/dL by day four, requiring two packed red blood cell units. He also required a fresh frozen plasma infusion because his coagulation profile slowly worsened (INR 1.4, fibrinogen 80 mg/dL).

At day five, a flexible sigmoidoscopy was performed. The endoscopic impression was an ulcerated, friable mucosa throughout the sigmoid and mid‑colon. Biopsies confirmed active colitis with crypt abscess formation and occasional pseudomembranes; no viral cytopathic changes were seen. The pathologist noted the presence of iron‑laden macrophages reflecting his chronic iron deficiency. No dysplasia was detected, but because of his PSC status and pancolitis, a colonoscopy had already been scheduled for every 6 months as part of surveillance, an appointment he had missed twice over the past two years.

The day after the endoscopy, the patient complained of worsening abdominal pain during the night; his abdomen became rigid, rebound tenderness was present, and the temperature spiked to 103 °F. A repeat CT scan was ordered—this time with contrast. It now revealed a small area of gas within the portal venous system, a harbinger of portal vein thrombosis, and a faint collection of free fluid in the right paracolic gutter. No perforation was seen yet. The patient was immediately switched to intravenous antibiotics: vancomycin to cover gram‑positive organisms, cefepime for anaerobes and gram‑negative coverage, and metronidazole was dropped.

He remained ICU‑level for monitoring, but a few days later, on day eight, the patient’s pain localized to the flank and radiated up into the back. The pain was sharp, worsened when he breathed, and did not correlate with constipation or bowel habits. An ultrasound of the abdomen showed the stone had migrated into the left kidney's lower pole, causing hydronephrosis. A urology consult was called; the patient was placed on a percutaneous nephrostomy tube, which relieved the pain and stabilized the urinary output. A 24‑hour urine collection later revealed a high urinary oxalate level (12 mg/kg in a day), a pattern he had had before, indicating the chronic tendency to form calcium oxalate stones.

The story takes a darker turn on day nine: a chest X‑ray taken for a persistent cough revealed a subtle wedge‑shaped opacity in the left lower lobe. A CT pulmonary angiogram was performed; it confirmed a large saddle pulmonary embolism. By this point the patient had already had a deep vein thrombosis in the left popliteal artery a month prior, diagnosed when he had a sudden onset of calf pain during a day shift. That DVT had been treated with a 6‑month course of rivaroxaban, which he had stopped a week before because of the insurance co‑payment after his last prescription.

He was placed on a high‑dose unfractionated heparin drip, and his anticoagulation management was shifted to a combination of therapeutic LMWH and then back to rivaroxaban after stabilization. The ICU team closely watched his anticoagulation levels and his overall hemodynamic status, which had been precarious due to systemic inflammation and repeated fluctuations in blood pressure.

At day ten, a sudden worsening of tachycardia, a rise of his lactate to 3.6 mmol/L, and a new drop in SpO2 from 98% to 85% in the presence of a flat left chest wall brought about emergency surgical consultation. The surgeons felt a perforation was highly probable: the peritoneal signs he had in the middle of that day indicated sudden peritoneal contamination. He was taken to the operating room, where an emergency right colectomy was performed. A perforated ulcer measuring 4 cm was found in the proximal ascending colon, with a focal abscess and purulent fluid seen throughout the right paracolic gutter. The surgeons resected the proximal colon, closed the distal ileum with a stapled side‑to‑side anastomosis, and left the perforation in the stump for drainage (a Hartmann’s procedure).

Post‑operative management was a marathon. He was admitted to the ICU on ventilatory support for a complicated respiratory effort that had already been aggravated by a large pulmonary embolism and a massive fluid shift. They were able to extubate after 48 hours, but he needed a non‑invasive ventilator for a short period because of residual respiratory compromise. Blood cultures grew E. coli, for which they added piperacillin‑tazobactam. He required a second blood transfusion on day 12 to maintain his hemoglobin above 9 g/dL. His WBC count peaked at 25 K/µL, and his CRP spiked again to 60 mg/L at this time.

His metabolic panel, however, showed a rising bilirubin up to 2.3 mg/dL and a plateau in alkaline phosphatase at 420 U/L—an ominous sign in someone with PSC. The attending gastroenterologist was already preparing a plan for an induction of infliximab—a biologic agent that requires a maintenance dose every two weeks—and discussed the possibility of a prophylactic mesenchymal stem cell infusion, a therapy that was still experimental in UC but had shown promise. The patient, exhausted and physically reduced, was hesitant but ultimately agreed to a trial after a discussion with his insurance.

A week after the surgery—day 17—he began the first infusion of infliximab 5 mg/kg. The next dose was scheduled after two weeks. He received iron infusions and started a high‑protein diet with vitamin B12 supplementation because his serum B12 remained low at 180 pg/mL.

Despite the aggressive management, the patient was plagued by a “new” rash: a diffuse, itchy purpuric eruption that appeared on his lower limbs on day 21. He was sent to dermatology as a potential drug reaction to either the biologic or the prophylactic anticoagulation. The skin biopsy confirmed leukocytoclastic vasculitis—an autoimmune phenomenon possibly triggered by the inflammatory cascade of UC and the potent immunosuppressive effect of infliximab.

The infection was being managed; however, another complication loomed. The patient’s creatinine climbed to 2.5 mg/dL, probably related to the nephrotoxic potential of the high‑dose antibiotics paired with a recent nephrostomy placement. The renal team decided to dial off certain antibiotics and switch to a more favorable regimen to minimize further renal insults. Meanwhile, his potassium began to rise; he was on a low‑potassium diet, but the kidneys weren’t keeping up.

Because of the PSC and the extensive pancolitis, the gastroenterology team re‑ordered a colonoscopy at day 27 to assess for dysplasia or neoplastic lesions. It was conducted with a 15 mm polypectomy for an ileal stricture visible on a flexible sigmoidoscopy. The pathology returned with high‑grade dysplasia in the polyp. The team consulted a surgical oncologist, who suggested a subtotal colectomy to reduce long‑term cancer risk. The patient’s condition—now complicated by ongoing sepsis from the perforation recovery, a significant bleed risk with anticoagulation, and the new high‑grade dysplasia—did not lend itself to a smooth surgery, but the decision to proceed was made in late December after a family meeting.

On day 29, after a 2‑hour wait for the patient's platelet count to stabilize (it had dipped to 62 × 10^9/L), the patient was taken back to surgery. This time, the operation was a subtotal colectomy with a loop ileostomy. The procedure took seven hours, with significant blood loss (~1200 mL). After revascularization of the remaining ileum, the surgery concluded.

The postoperative period was rough. His leukocyte count spiked again to 30 K/µL, his CRP at 70 mg/L, and he developed a fever that resolved on the third postoperative day with vancomycin. The abdominal cavity was irrigated with warm saline, but some residual abscesses in the right upper quadrant persisted and were drained via a percutaneous approach.

On day 35, the patient’s renal function improved slightly after switching to a low‑dose furosemide regimen. He was weaned off the ventilator, and the nephrostomy tube was removed two days later as imaging showed no hydronephrosis. The skin rash resolved spontaneously over the next week.

The story didn't end there. In early February, after five months of total parenteral nutrition to mitigate malabsorption caused by extensive resections, the patient was finally able to tolerate a bland diet and a progressive oral schedule. His weight had been decreasing for the past several months, from 82 kg to 56 kg, but over the last five weeks, he started gaining back about 2 kg.

A follow‑up colonoscopy on day 110 found a large, irregular polyp at the ileocolic anastomosis. The pathology was consistent with a colorectal adenocarcinoma, moderately differentiated, stage pT3N1M0. The surgical oncologist recommended a low‑dose chemotherapeutic regimen in addition to a planned extended ileocolic resection in six months. The patient, exhausted, agreed. His condition had dramatically worsened due to repeated flares, septic complications, and now cancer. The narrative, however, does not end with an abrupt tragedy. The last entry—date 2026‑03‑25—shows our patient at home, in hospice care, reflecting on how he has, through the last several years, endured a cascade of medical crises. The story ends with a note about his decision to focus on quality of life, emphasizing that the disease course had been relentless, with each flare eclipsing the last, leaving a chronicle of relentless worsening that, despite aggressive interventions, remained ultimately unresolved.
