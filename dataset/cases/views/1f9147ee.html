**John’s Story – A Hard‐Case Peptic Ulcer Bleed**

---

**Thursday, 8:00 AM – The first whisper of trouble**

John M., a 58‑year‑old engineer, had been feeling “a bit off” that morning. After his usual bowl of oatmeal, a quiet coffee, and a slice of toast, a sour, dull ache settled in the upper left quadrant of his abdomen. He told his wife it was probably the reflux that kept him up on his PPI, and he took the extra dose he’d been using the weekend before to ease his back pain. Two hours later, he vomited a couple of small, wet‑looking waves; the first was a faint brownish tint, the next a bit darker. He felt a sudden, weak pulse and a brief, dizzy spell while reaching for a jar of pickles. His wife called the nurse she works with at the local urgent‑care clinic.

The nurse, a seasoned RN, noted that John’s blood pressure measured 132/84 mm Hg and his pulse was at 82 bpm. John denied any recent trauma or alcohol binge. The nurse asked about his medications, revealing a complex list: 81 mg aspirin daily for his ischemic heart disease, warfarin 2 mg daily for chronic atrial fibrillation, omeprazole 20 mg daily for GERD, and a handful of ibuprofen tablets taken three to four times a week for chronic lower back pain. He also gave the nurse a pack‑in‑hand smoking habit (½ pack per day for 30 years) and “a couple of beers after dinner” to “keep the nerves quiet.” The nurse noted a faint sign of nausea, and she recommended a home stay‑in and a call to his primary care physician (PCP) for a possible review.

**Friday, 10:30 AM – Overlooking the storm**

John’s PCP, Dr. Patel, saw him in the office. John complained of a persistent “burn” in the upper abdomen and occasional mild vomiting. Dr. Patel, noting the patient’s INR was 1.6 in his last pharmacy check, suggested continuing the warfarin and aspirin until the next INR reading, and added a stool guaiac test. John, reassured by the normal‑appearing exam and the PCP’s reassurance, left feeling that this was just another case of GERD flare‑up.

**Friday night – The first sign of a deeper crisis**

At 10 PM the patient’s wife found two black, mucus‑laden stools in the toilet — a sudden, alarming change. She placed the bucket on the phone and called 911. A rapid response had arrived within ten minutes. The paramedics documented a blood pressure of 110/54 mm Hg, pulse 108 bpm, and capillary refill one second. They noted a distended, tender abdomen and his conjunctivae appeared pale. The patient’s oxygen saturation was 94 % on room air.

An IV of normal saline was started while the EMTs collected a sample of the bowel peristalsis for labs. The rapid INR test returned 2.0. Hemoglobin was 13.2 g/dL, but the patient’s baseline was usually 11.8 g/dL, so the drop seemed minimal. Nevertheless, he felt “heavy” in the chest and noted a fleeting, unsteady feeling.

**Saturday, 7:30 AM – Emergency department assessment**

John arrived in the ED with a pulse of 116 bpm, BP 98/60 mm Hg, and an unsteady gait. There was a faint, tar‑black discoloration of the stool on the bed sheets. On examination, he demonstrated minimal abdominal tenderness, and his epigastric region seemed soft on palpation. No peritoneal signs.

Labs: Hemoglobin 11.6 g/dL (down 1.6 g/dL from baseline), INR 2.1, aPTT 28 sec, platelets 188 k/µL, electrolytes: Na 140 mEq/L, K 4.2, BUN 32 mg/dL, creatinine 2.1 mg/dL (CKD stage 3B baseline 1.8). A complete metabolic panel highlighted mild metabolic acidosis (pH 7.32, lactate 2.7 mmol/L). Coagulation was consistent with warfarin therapy; the INR had risen. The doctor decided to reverse: vitamin K 5 mg IV, along with a fresh frozen plasma load (4 units) to bring INR <1.5.

He was placed on a 48‑hour telemetry monitor due to concern for hemodynamic instability and for cardiac arrhythmia risk from warfarin and anemia. A bedside ultrasound of the abdomen showed no free fluid; the gallbladder, pancreas, and kidneys were normal. A nasogastric tube was inserted for decompression, and the suction returned 0.5 L of dark, coffee‑ground‑like fluid, consistent with upper GI bleeding.

**Saturday night – Endoscopic intervention**

A gastroenterology consult was placed, and within two hours (10 PM), an endoscopy was performed. The gastric antrum revealed a 1.5 cm ulcer with active spurting of bright red blood, likely a non‑variceal ulcer. Endoscopic therapy was applied: hemostatic clips were placed around the bleeding vessel, and a dilute epinephrine spray was administered. The ulcer base was also photographed. The endoscopist noted moderate mucosal friability and diffuse gastritis. The team decided to proceed with a proton pump inhibitor (PPI) drip 80 mg IV q8h, and, given his warfarin requirement, they continued the anticoagulation in the evening with a 5 mg dose of warfarin (the usual daily dose) after correcting the INR, to avoid under‑anticoagulation for the atrial fibrillation.

**Sunday – A fragile equilibrium**

The next morning, John’s vital signs were borderline: BP 102/58, pulse 95, but he was alert and oriented. His stool remained black but was thinner. Hemoglobin had dropped to 10.8 g/dL. The team gave a unit of packed red blood cells (PRBCs) to keep his hemoglobin above 11.0 g/dL, as his baseline was 11.8. The decision to maintain warfarin (with a target INR 2–3) after the endoscopic bleed was weighed heavily — a necessary compromise for the atrial fibrillation’s embolic risk.

**Sunday afternoon – Deterioration**

At 3 PM, John complained of a new, crushing chest pain radiating to his left arm. His telemetry showed a non‑ST elevation acute coronary syndrome (NSTEMI) due to hemodynamic stress from ongoing bleeding and anemia. The cardiology team started aspirin 81 mg and clopidogrel 75 mg a day after an urgent cardiac cath that showed no culprit lesions but a diffuse 30 % stenosis in the left anterior descending artery. The team chose to hold warfarin and aspirin until the night of Thursday, hoping to reduce his bleeding risk while still addressing the ischemic heart disease.

Simultaneously, his blood pressure plummeted to 85/50 mm Hg, his heart rate spiked to 115 bpm, and his urine output fell to 250 mL in the preceding hour—signes of hypovolemic shock and acute kidney injury. He was transferred to the intensive care unit (ICU).

**Monday – The ICU stay**

John was intubated and sedated to control intrathoracic pressures and protect against aspiration, as the team deemed this essential for the re‑bleeding control. A central line was placed; renal replacement therapy was initiated. Serial labs showed hemoglobin 9.2 g/dL, INR 0.9, BUN 55, creatinine 3.2. A repeat endoscopy was scheduled to ensure hemostasis, but the team also considered packing or surgical intervention because of the high rebleeding risk in a patient on warfarin, with poor control of anemia and high cardiac risk. Unfortunately, the patient’s coagulation profile continued to shift, with the INR briefly peaking to 2.1 while on warfarin. Each time warfarin was held, the patient’s atrial fibrillation’s ventricular capture slowed, raising a paradoxical embolic risk.

**Tuesday – Complications cascade**

While sedated, John’s blood pressure decreased further to 75/40 mm Hg, his heart rate rose to 120, and the central venous pressure increased to 16 mm Hg. His chest pain escalated—a crescendo of chest discomfort, associated with diaphoresis. An ECG revealed a dynamic T‑wave inversion in leads V2–V4. The ICU team held anti‑platelets, ceased warfarin, and inserted an intra‑aortic balloon pump (IABP) to off‑load the heart. The decision to use IABP was a bold move, given the patient’s bleeding risk, but was deemed necessary to improve perfusion and prevent myocardial necrosis.

A second endoscopy revealed a new small ulcerated lesion in the stomach, with mild oozing. Hemostasis was re‑attempted with a combination of thermal coagulation and a single sclerotherapy bead. Despite interventions, the bleeding persisted.

**Wednesday – The downward spiral**

In the early morning, a sudden rise in heart rate to 140 bpm and blood pressure dropping to 68/42 mm Hg signaled a rebleed. The nasogastric tube drained 300 mL of fresh, bright‑red blood. The ICU team began transfusing PRBCs and started protamine to reverse the effect of warfarin further. They also placed a temporary femoral catheter for continuous hemodialysis, given the rise in creatinine to 4.5 mg/dL and oliguria.

Unfortunately, every attempt to stop the bleeding made the patient more unstable due to the balance required between anti‑coagulation for atrial fibrillation, antiplatelet therapy for coronary disease, and hemostatic control for the ulcer. The warfarin was held indefinitely. However, the patient’s INR began to rise again due to lingering pro‑coagulant factors, raising the risk of paradoxical emboli again.

**Thursday – The final day**

At 9 AM, the patient’s blood pressure suddenly collapsed to 55/30 mm Hg. Pulse was 170 bpm. He was in refractory hypotension. The bedside arterial line read 40 mm Hg. The ventilator was set on maximum support, and vasopressor support was escalated to a combination of norepinephrine and epinephrine. The ICU staff could not reverse the bleeding adequately. Meanwhile, the renal replacement machine alarmed frequently with rising central venous pressure and signs of venous congestion.

At 11:15 AM, John’s heart rhythm converted abruptly to asystole. In the brief interval before his pulse returned to 56 bpm from the resuscitation, the team performed two chest compressions and intubated. He had one transient, non‑progressing rhythm, but the clinical picture was grim. Within minutes, he lost palpable pulses, blood pressure reading ceased in the arterial line, and the ICU team, recognizing the futility, declared the condition lethal. John was pronounced deceased at 11:24 AM.

---

**A summary of the course**

- **Initial subtle symptom**: mild epigastric discomfort misinterpreted as reflux, delayed evaluation. - **Early blood‑stool changes**: first black stool at home, delayed presentation to emergency care. - **Diagnostic dilemma**: multiple comorbidities and anticoagulation complicated decision‑making about when to reverse warfarin. - **Therapeutic cross‑fire**: balancing the re‑bloody ulcer against cardiac events, with conflicting priorities. - **Complicating factors**: CKD limited the clearance of warfarin and other medications, smoking and alcohol impeded mucosal healing. - **Outcome**: worsening culminating in fatal rebleeding, shock, and death.

John’s case exemplifies how a seemingly straightforward “pain in the stomach” can unravel into an inter‑disciplinary crisis when risk factors, comorbidities, and medication interactions collide. The narrative underscores the critical importance of early recognition, rapid coordination, and the difficult choices clinicians face when multiple life‑threatening conditions coexist.
