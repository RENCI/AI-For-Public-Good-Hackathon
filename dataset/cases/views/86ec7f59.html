On a bright, overcast morning in early March, Thomas Mitchell, a 60‑year‑old retired electrician, awoke feeling an ordinary stiffness in the left hip after a brief bout of coughing. The stiffness felt like a dull ache, not unlike the discomfort he had endured for years from osteoarthritis in both hips. He tried a short stretch, and the pain did not abate. He went back to bed, intending to get up around noon to get a coffee with his wife, only to hear a sudden, heavy thump from the hallway. He had slipped on the narrow, uneven steps that lead from the stairwell to his first‑floor apartment. The impact was loud and immediate, and he was unable to feel his left foot for a second because the jolt had knocked his gait so violently that his usual steady steps had been replaced by a disorienting, painful collapse.

He lay in bed for a few minutes, his left leg straight and aching. He tried to sit up, but the pain became so sharp that he realized his body had been thrown out of alignment. He could no longer put weight on his left leg, and standing up required a dramatic effort to brace himself with handrails and a cane that he had worn for years because of his osteoarthritis. Thomas’s wife, who had been making tea on the kitchen counter, came in with concern and immediately called 911. By the time the paramedics arrived, Thomas was sitting upright, holding the left side of his femur, his face pale from the shock of pain.

In the ambulance, the EMTs quickly noted his medication list: metoprolol for hypertension, aspirin for coronary artery disease, and alendronate which he had been taking for three years for his known osteoporosis. Thomas also had a history of COPD, a moderate drinker who had cut down after a recent diagnosis of liver dysfunction, and a long‑time smoker who quit twelve years ago but still had a nicotine habit through his work environment. All of these factors added to the complexity of his presentation.

At the emergency department, a resident physician on call assessed the patient’s left hip. A dull, throbbing pain radiating up the lateral thigh and to the groin was reported. Thomas was unable to bear weight, and an initial assessment revealed an obvious shortening of the limb and a palpable deformity. The resident ordered a plain anteroposterior (AP) pelvis and oblique and lateral hip radiographs, which showed a displaced type 32A2 intertrochanteric fracture – a break between the lateral and medial columns of the femoral neck, just below the lesser trochanter. The fracture was clearly displaced and angulated, consistent with a high‑energy mechanism (a fall from a ladder) and his underlying osteoporotic bone.

An orthopaedic surgeon, Dr. Patel, was called and examined Thomas. He noted the fracture pattern and the likely need for intramedullary fixation. Dr. Patel discussed the situation with Thomas and his wife in the waiting room. He explained that the fracture could be managed with a short intramedullary gamma nail. He also emphasized the necessity of early weight‑bearing as tolerated due to the poor bone quality and the patient’s comorbid osteoarthritis, which would hinder prolonged bed rest. The risk of prolonged immobilisation was substantial, given his cardiovascular disease and his propensity for falls.

Before the operation could proceed, Thomas’s cardiologist was consulted to review the plan. Thomas, who had a history of stable angina, was on low‑dose aspirin and required careful peri‑operative management of his cardiovascular status. The cardiologist confirmed that the patient’s blood pressure was well controlled, but recommended that the operating team be prepared with a beta‑blocker if tachycardia developed, and that an intra‑operative arterial line be placed because of his older age and known vascular disease.

On the day of surgery (Day 0), Thomas was taken to the operating room. Standard pre‑operative antibiotics and a lower‑segment subcutaneous heparin for deep venous thrombosis (DVT) prophylaxis were administered. Under spinal anesthesia, Dr. Patel achieved reduction of the fracture by traction and gentle manipulation. The intramedullary gamma nail was then positioned, and the femoral shaft was reamed to accommodate the implant. The surgeon chose a lag screw through the lesser trochanter to compress the fracture ends, and the nail was locked proximally to prevent varus collapse. The incision was closed in layers, and Thomas was positioned for gentle transfer to the recovery suite. Post‑operatively, his vital signs were stable, and he was started on a low‑dose morphine infusion for post‑operative pain.

For his first two post‑operative days (Days 1–2), Thomas remained on a bed‐rest protocol with the left leg in a splint to protect the operation site. He was treated for osteoporosis with intravenous zoledronic acid (200 mg) on Day 1, aiming to improve bone density and reduce the risk of subsequent fractures. Thomas was also counselled on vitamin D supplementation and was provided with a prescription for a calcium supplement. Dr. Patel discussed his smoking habit with him, noting that nicotine can delay fracture healing, and the patient promised to quit using nicotine replacement while abstaining from alcohol during his recovery.

On Day 3, Thomas was ambulated to the bedside by a physical therapist (PT) with a toe‑off gait, weight bearing as tolerated (WBAT) with the use of a single‑sided walking frame. The PT assessed his pain and noted mild swelling and bruising at the surgical site. The weight‑bearing instruction included a gentle progressive gait plan: start with 10–15 steps per day, then 20–30, and eventually 60–80 with the frame, always focusing on careful foot placement and keeping the pelvis level. Thomas's pain was well managed with a multimodal regimen of acetaminophen, ibuprofen, and a scheduled dose of opioid, which was titrated down gradually.

By Day 5, the wound had dried and healing was progressing well. The incision looked pink with no signs of infection. The physical therapist encouraged light isometric exercises for the quadriceps and gluteal muscles while the patient remained seated. Thomas also began gentle active‑range‑of‑motion exercises using a dynamometer for the hip and knee, as he had previously used an exercise bike at the gym. His cardiovascular status was monitored in the hospital, and he was placed on an anticoagulation regimen of low‑molecular‑weight heparin for the next 14 days to reduce the risk of DVT, given his known cardiovascular disease.

On Day 7, Thomas had his first formal re‑examination by orthopaedics. Routine radiographs showed adequate fracture alignment and the implant in situ. The surgical incision had fully healed, and there was no evidence of infection. Thomas reported being able to do 30–40 steps with his walking frame before stopping for discomfort in the left hip. He noted that the stiffness from his osteoarthritis was still present but had not yet worsened. Dr. Patel encouraged continued participation in physiotherapy and recommended that Thomas begin a home exercise program once he was discharged on day 10.

Thomas was discharged on postoperative Day 10 with a home health aide scheduled to assist with bathing, dressing, and household chores. His home aide was instructed on fall prevention strategies — removing loose rugs, improving lighting, and installing grab bars in his bathroom. The therapist had devised an individualized, progressive walking plan: 60–80 steps using a walking cane (later to be replaced by a walker if needed) to maintain proper gait mechanics. The orthopedic surgeon scheduled a follow‑up visit in 6 weeks and advised Thomas to return sooner if he experienced any fever, increasing pain, or swelling.

In the next few weeks, Thomas participated in outpatient therapy sessions, attending twice a week for gait training, strength exercises, and balance work. He had a gradual, steady improvement in the strength of his left hip and the ability to take more continuous steps without pain. His radiographs in the six‑week follow‑up showed bony bridging at the fracture site, though the healing process was delayed compared to non‑ osteoporotic patients. Nevertheless, the callus formation was healthy, and the implant was stable. On a subsequent visit (eight weeks post‑fracture), Thomas reported that he could walk independently with a cane, do light activities of daily living, and had significant relief from both the acute fracture pain and the chronic osteoarthritis discomfort compared to pre‑fracture baseline.

Thomas’s narrative of recovery is shaped by his comorbid osteoporosis, osteoarthritis, and cardiovascular disease. Each factor required specific adjustments: his bone density therapy with zoledronic acid and vitamin D supplementation facilitated healing; the careful cardiovascular management ensured that the surgery and post‑operative period did not precipitate myocardial events; and the focus on gait training and fall prevention was crucial to avoid a repeat fracture. Moreover, addressing his risk factors—particularly encouraging smoking cessation, reducing alcohol intake, and improving nutrition—played a subtle yet essential role in the overall improvement of his functional status.

By the end of the first year after the fracture, Thomas had returned to many of his former hobbies, such as gardening and attending church services. He had adopted a stricter regimen of daily walking with a cane, kept his weight under control, and remained vigilant about fall risks. His cardiovascular medication were kept stable, and he continued regular blood pressure monitoring. While the intertrochanteric fracture was a significant event that altered his life trajectory, the combination of prompt surgical fixation, targeted comorbidity management, and a structured rehabilitation program enabled Thomas to regain independence and improve his quality of life.
