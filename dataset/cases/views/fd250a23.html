**A 65‑year‑old man with a rapidly worsening course of sigmoid colon cancer**

---

### Week 0: First warning signs

Thomas “Tom” Reed was a 65‑year‑old retired construction supervisor who had always been proud of his “hard‑body” and his love of hearty meals. He was a 1‑pack‑a‑day smoker, spent his retirement hours gardening or watching television, and enjoyed a diet high in red meat and processed foods. His medical history was dominated by type 2 diabetes (HbA1c 8.2 %), class II obesity (BMI = 32.7 kg/m²), chronic kidney disease stage 3 (eGFR 32 mL/min/1.73 m²), and diverticulosis diagnosed 10 years ago on an abdominal CT done for an unrelated abdominal pain episode.

During a routine walk in the park, Tom felt a dull, pressing ache on the left lower quadrant of his abdomen that had been building for about a week. It was mild enough that he went to bed the same evening, hoping it would subside. The next morning, he noticed a small amount of bright‑red blood on the toilet paper—the first time this had happened in his adult life. He chalked it up to hemorrhoids and did not tell anyone.

---

### Week 1: First primary‑care visit

Tom called his family doctor, Dr. Huang, after the stool‑blood episode. Dr. Huang asked about abdominal pain, changes in bowel habit, weight loss, fatigue, or night sweats. Tom mentioned “just the usual stuff, I guess. A little pain in my lower left side and some blood a couple of days ago.” He also reported a 10 lb unintentional weight loss over the last month—lost mainly from his thighs and hips. He added that his diabetes medications seemed to require dose adjustments, but the doctor had not been sure why. At the interview, Tom rated his pain 3/10, intermittent, occurring after heavy meals.

Physical examination revealed a soft abdomen with mild left‑lower‑quadrant tenderness but no palpable mass. No obvious bleeding source was found on digital rectal exam. Vital signs were within normal limits except for mild hypertension (150/88 mm Hg). Dr. Huang ordered a basic metabolic panel (BMP), complete blood count (CBC), HbA1c, and a stool guaiac test, and suggested a colonoscopy after a week.

---

### Week 2: Laboratory findings and colonoscopy

The BMP revealed creatinine 2.1 mg/dL (baseline 1.9 mg/dL), BUN 30 mg/dL, and mild hyperglycemia (FPG 172 mg/dL). CBC showed low hemoglobin 9.8 g/dL and leukopenia of 3.8 × 10⁹/L. Dr. Huang arranged an urgent colonoscopy.

During the procedure the gastroenterologist found a 4‑cm ulcerated, fungating mass in the mid‑sigmoid colon, with friable edges. The surrounding mucosa appeared inflamed, consistent with diverticulosis flare. Biopsy specimens were taken. The colonoscope was able to traverse the mass but could not fully visualize beyond it because of severe luminal narrowing.

---

### Week 3: Pathology and staging

The pathology report returned the next day, confirming a high‑grade undifferentiated adenocarcinoma with sarcomatoid features—consistent with a mucinous or signet‑ring component. The specimen showed high mitotic index and loss of glandular differentiation.

An urgent contrast‑enhanced CT scan of the chest, abdomen, and pelvis was ordered. The scan showed: a thickening of the sigmoid colon wall with a 4‑cm infiltrating mass; enlarged mesenteric lymph nodes; a 2.8‑cm hypodense lesion in the right hepatic lobe; multiple peritoneal implants—suggesting stage IV disease (T4N2M1). No distant metastatic disease beyond the liver and peritoneum was identified.

Given the aggressive histology and stage, Tom was referred to oncology on the same day.

---

### Week 4: First oncology consultation

Oncologist Dr. Liu met Thomas in a private office at the cancer center. Dr. Liu explained the findings: an aggressive, undifferentiated tumor that had already metastasized. He described the standard first‑line regimens (FOLFOX or CAPOX), but emphasized that Tom’s CKD and recent leukopenia limited the use of oxaliplatin and irinotecan, which are nephrotoxic or can cause profound myelosuppression. Capecitabine was also cautioned—its active metabolites are largely renally excreted, and dose adjustment is required. Thus, a full therapeutic trial would carry high risk of complications.

The plan was to start a **palliative dose‑reduced FOLFOX** (5‑FU + leucovorin + low‑dose oxaliplatin) every two weeks, using a prophylactic growth‑factor if leukopenia recurred. The goal was symptom palliation and modest survival benefit.

Tumor markers (CEA) were sent; baseline CEA was 23 ng/mL (normal <5). The oncology team also recommended a short course of corticosteroids for inflammation and a proton‑pump inhibitor to protect the stomach.

---

### Week 6: First chemo cycle and early toxicities

The first cycle of chemotherapy was delivered. During the 5‑FU infusion, Tom experienced mild nausea and a diffuse abdominal crampy pain for the rest of the day. He did report a mild increase in stool frequency (3–4 loose stools per day). The oncology nurse noted that his creatinine was 2.4 mg/dL and his leukocytes dropped to 2.9 × 10⁹/L. Dr. Liu decided to reduce the oxaliplatin dose by 25 % and to monitor his bone marrow counts closely.

Meanwhile, his diabetes flared – fasting glucose 210 mg/dL, requiring a small uptick in insulin. He also had a minor bleeding episode from the tumor site—bright‑red blood in stool—prompting the oncology team to give him an oral tranexamic acid to stabilize clots.

---

### Month 2: Radiologic progression and new symptoms

At the next follow‑up, Tom’s performance status had declined from ECOG 0 to ECOG 1 (some fatigue, but still able to drive). A repeat CT scan showed that the hepatic lesion had enlarged to 3.5 cm, the peritoneal implants had increased in number, and a new 1.2‑cm nodule appeared in the lung apex (pulmonary metastasis). The sigmoid mass was still present but had grown to 5 cm with impending obstruction.

He began to complain of an increasingly oppressive sense of fullness in the abdomen, especially after meals, and intermittent colicky abdominal pain. His weight had fallen another 15 lb in total over the past two months. Hemoglobin had dropped to 7.8 g/dL. The hematology team gave him erythropoietin and packed cell transfusions. In light of his CKD and the progressive tumor burden, the oncology team discussed that **palliative care** might be a more appropriate focus.

---

### Month 2.5: Attempts at re‑staging and management changes

Dr. Liu presented the case at a multidisciplinary tumor board. Several options were considered: 1. **Second‑line chemotherapy** (e.g., fluorouracil + bevacizumab). Bevacizumab, however, is contraindicated in patients with significant kidney disease as it may precipitate proteinuria or worsen hypertension. 2. **Brachytherapy or external beam radiation** directed at the sigmoid mass to relieve obstructive symptoms. 3. **Placement of a covered self‑expanding metal stent** to bypass the occlusion, allowing for oral intake and potentially a better quality of life.

The radiation oncology team suggested a low‑dose palliative radiation course (10 Gy in 2 fractions) targeting the sigmoid mass and peritoneal implants, hoping to reduce local pain and bleeding. Tom consented, understanding the risks of mucositis and limited long‑term benefit.

---

### Month 3: New complications and rapid decompensation

Two weeks after radiation, Tom admitted to the emergency department with a sudden onset of severe crampy abdominal pain, vomiting, and a feeling of distension. Imaging (CT abdomen with contrast) revealed a massive bowel obstruction at the site of the sigmoid mass, with a fluid‑filled proximal colon and evidence of fecal loading. The team also found worsening ascites, which had been slowly accumulating in previous scans. Laboratory values: hemoglobin 6.3 g/dL, leukopenia 2.1 × 10⁹/L, creatinine 2.8 mg/dL, BUN 42 mg/dL, and sodium 130 mmol/L (hyponatremia).

An urgent decompressive sigmoidoscopic procedure was attempted but failed due to extensive tumor invasion. He was then catheterized, started on broad‑spectrum antibiotics for suspected peritonitis, and scheduled for a **surgical oncologic resection** deemed high risk given his CKD, leukopenia, and overall frailty.

During this period, his diabetes remained poorly controlled; his insulin requirement doubled. The oncology team re‑evaluated his goals: given the rapid decline, a full oncologic operation was unlikely to benefit, and the risk of peri‑operative death was high.

The palliative care team was consulted. The patient, family, and clinicians discussed realistic expectations, including the transition from aggressive oncology to comfort‑focused care. The patient expressed a wish to remain alive as long as possible but did not consent to further major surgery or chemotherapy.

---

### Month 3.5: Transition to hospice and end of life

After a brief period on the inpatient step‑down unit—where he received palliative medication management, nutritional support (high‑protein, low‑solids diet) and psychosocial counseling—Tom was moved to a home hospice unit. His pain, managed with oral morphine and scheduled NSAIDs, was reasonably controlled until the last week. Blood transfusions were continued intermittently as needed. The family received supportive counseling and education regarding the disease process and expected trajectory.

On **Day 1 of hospice**, Tom passed away peacefully at home after a short period of confusion, an inability to retain liquids, and acute renal failure from persistent obstruction and sepsis. No autopsy was performed. The cause of death was recorded as complications of metastatic colorectal cancer with obstructive colitis complicated by renal failure.
