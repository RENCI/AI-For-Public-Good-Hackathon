**Clinical Narrative: A 20‑year‑old University Student with Acne Vulgaris and a Superficial Bacterial Infection**

When Maya first noticed the dark spots on her cheeks and the occasional red pimple on her chin, she thought it was simply the start of her teenage years. A 20‑year‑old female college sophomore living in a shared apartment, she described her skin as “oily, sometimes cracked, and definitely in need of a clean‑up.” She had been diagnosed with polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) and was on oral contraceptives (levonorgestrel‑containing pills) at her mother’s insistence to regulate her cycle and to help with her acne. Maya also had a BMI of 32 kg/m², classifying her as obese, and was a daily smoker of one pack of cigarettes. Her diet consisted mainly of processed carbs and sugary drinks, and she admitted to occasionally using heavy foundation that she left on the skin all night.

---

### Month 1, Week 1 — First Signs

Maya began to see a few blackheads on her nose and a handful of tiny papules on her jawline. Within a week she noticed that the lesions on her cheeks had slightly enlarged and become tender, with a faint yellow‑colored crust on the upper lip. She described the pain as a dull ache amplified by touching the area, and she had begun feeling “self‑conscious” about her appearance. Maya purchased a can of over‑the‑counter (OTC) benzoyl peroxide (5 %) wash and a mild moisturizing lotion, but she stopped using the cleanser after a day because the skin felt too dry. She mentioned in a brief call to her friend that “something smells like a bad cough in the morning.” That night she noticed the appearance of a single, relatively large, superficial pustule on her forehead that had a slightly opaque, purulent core—she recognized it as a stye of sorts, but it was on her skin, not her eye.

---

### Month 1, Week 2 — Self‑Care Decision Point

Maya’s skin did not improve. On that second week, she noticed a faint halo of redness around the purulent pustules, and the purulent material seemed to spread to neighboring follicles forming a “small cluster.” She began to experience mild warmth and mild swelling around the affected area. Maya’s mother, who had a history of recurrent boils, cautioned her that “this could be a bacterial infection on top of the acne.” Maya expressed anxiety about going to a dermatologist because of potential costs. Her online search led her to a local community health clinic that offered low‑cost dermatology appointments.

---

### Month 2, Week 1 — Dermatology Visit

At her first clinic appointment, the nurse assistant noted the characteristic comedones, papules, pustules, and superficial abscess-like lesions clustered on the nasal and maxillary region, with mild edema extending to the malar cheeks. The dermatologist, Dr. Patel, performed a gentle inspection and confirmed a superficial infection with Staphylococcus aureus superimposed on her acne. The lesions were erythematous, non‑spreading, and the purulent exudate was minimal. Dr. Patel explained the common interplay between acne vulgaris and superficial bacterial infection, noting the importance of controlling inflammation to reduce bacterial colonization.

The dermatologist prescribed a 4‑week course of topically applied clindamycin 1 % gel three times daily for bacterial control, supplemented with adapalene 0.1 % cream nightly for comedonal and inflammatory control. Maya was also told to use a gentle non‑comedogenic cleanser (pH‑balanced, fragrance‑free) twice daily and advised to avoid occlusive cosmetics overnight. Dr. Patel added a brief lifestyle counseling session, where Maya was encouraged to reduce her daily cigarette consumption, switch to an easier-to‑digest high‑protein snack instead of pastries, and to limit her usage of processed high‑glycaemic foods—all as potential adjuncts to her skincare routine.

---

### Month 2, Week 4 — Progress Check and Adjustment

Following two weeks of topical therapy, Maya had noted a moderate decrease in redness and a reduction in purulent discharge. However, a few new papules had appeared on her chin, especially after a late‑night study session where she’d rinsed her face with hot water and pressed a pillow against her cheeks while sleeping. Dr. Patel recognized the potential for occlusion and heat to aggravate comedones and advised her to avoid hot showers, use a cool compress on inflamed areas, and to shower at least 2–3 times a week.

Maya was prescribed oral doxycycline 100 mg twice daily for 2 weeks, as her superficial infection had not fully resolved. She was directed to resume clindamycin and adapalene for the full duration of the topical treatment, but to hold doxycycline after the 2‑week course unless recurrence occurred. Her dermatologist also discussed the risks of antibiotic over‑use and the need to limit systemic medication to clear bacterial infections only.

---

### Month 3, Week 1 — Final Improvements

During the third week of the combined therapy, Maya saw a significant improvement. The pustules had cleared, leaving behind faint pink spots that slowly faded, and the comedones were fewer in number. There was no evidence of a purulent discharge in the previous locations. Dr. Patel evaluated her progress, asked about adherence to treatments, and confirmed that Maya had remained compliant with her topical regimen and had taken a single dose of oral antibiotic, which she had finished with only a brief delay. Dr. Patel decided to taper out the clindamycin and adapalene to avoid long‑term irritation and called for a follow‑up in four weeks to confirm complete resolution.

After Dr. Patel’s last visit, Maya continued her daily moisturizing routine and a more disciplined approach to skin cleaning. More importantly, she attended the campus counseling service and reduced smoking to half a pack per day, with the plan of full cessation within a month. She also scheduled a nutritionist appointment to adjust her diet to a balanced protein–fiber–healthy‑fat plan, limiting sugary and high‑glycaemic items.

---

### Month 4, Week 1 — Resolution

When Maya returned for her follow‑up, the dermatologist noted a near‑complete resolution of her lesions, with only trace hyperpigmented patches remaining—expected mild post‑inflammatory hyperpigmentation. The superficial bacterial infection was fully cleared, and no new purulent lesions had formed. The skin texture had improved, and there was a noticeable reduction in sebaceous activity.

Dr. Patel thanked Maya for her compliance and explained that her acne vulgaris was now in a remission phase. She encouraged Maya to be vigilant for flare‑ups in the future, particularly during hormonal changes, periods of stress, or if she returns to smoking or high‑glycaemic nutrition. Maya left the clinic feeling hopeful, with a clear plan for maintaining her skin health and a resolved course of disease.
