In the early spring of 2025, Thomas had been feeling as if a small weight had settled in his chest. A three‑month history of an irritating, dry cough that wouldn’t respond to over‑the‑counter antihistamines, he had started to notice a growing sense of breathlessness, especially on the right side of his body where he felt a dull ache that spread from the ribs to his shoulder blade. With a body weight that had slipped 12 pounds in the last two months, he began to avoid the short walks he used to take along the riverbank with his wife, Margaret. The cough had begun to come with a faint bloodstain on the first night of each episode; it was brief, but the sight of it was unsettling. His family noticed that the patient’s routine laughter was replaced with quiet groans, and the man who had once spent hours in the garden now lay almost entirely in bed.

Thomas had always been a hard‑working man, but the same qualities that had made him a reliable coal miner and later a long‑time plumber had also placed him in the highest risk categories for lung disease. He had smoked a pack a day for forty years, until four years ago when he finally gave up. He had also spent a decade living in a modest apartment in an industrial district, exposed to fumes from nearby factories. Although his family had never tested the home for radon, the landlord had never mentioned it, and Thomas had never installed a detector. His health history was a maze of comorbidities that had been managed but never truly cured: COPD with a forced expiratory volume in one second of 58 % predicted, progressive emphysema evident on chest radiographs, a diffuse network of bronchiectasis in the upper lobes, and a mild interstitial fibrosis of the lung parenchyma. He also suffered from anemia (hemoglobin 10.5 g/dL) that had been partially corrected with oral iron, and he had been prescribed fluoxetine for mild depression for the past year. A history of hypertension and coronary artery disease had resulted in a percutaneous coronary intervention on one of the bypass vessels two years earlier.

On March 12, Thomas’s breathing began to feel more labored with any exertion, and his cough was now productive with sputum that was occasionally mucoid but more often a thin, clear liquid. He described an odd sensation that something “was stuck in his chest.” Margaret noticed a new, mild, persistent low‑grade fever of 99.5 °F that appeared on the evenings. Concerned and in search of a diagnosis, Thomas finally agreed to visit the urgent‑care clinic.

A chest X‑ray at the clinic showed a new, dense opacity at the superior segment of the right lung, the size of which was not fully appreciable on the upright view. A chest CT was ordered promptly for better visualization. In the days that followed, Thomas’s appetite had further decreased, and he began to feel dizzy each time he attempted to sit up. He also started to notice that his pulse rate was irregular, and a faint, pale complexion signified worsening anemia.

On March 20, a high‑resolution CT scan revealed a 4.3 cm spiculated mass occupying nearly the entire right upper lobe, with evidence of mediastinal lymph node enlargement and a small pleural effusion. The lesion had a central area of low attenuation consistent with necrosis. No distant metastases were seen on the same study. The radiologist commented that the mass had a classic appearance of a high‑grade tumor, possibly a broncho‑alveolar carcinoma or a small‑cell carcinoma. However, the size and spiculation raised suspicion for a poorly differentiated carcinoma of unknown primary, an ominous diagnosis in any patient but especially in someone with Thomas’s comorbidities.

His oncologist, Dr. Singh, discussed the implications with Thomas and Margaret during a second‑day visit. The patient’s COPD and pulmonary fibrosis made him a poor candidate for standard lobectomy; the risk of postoperative respiratory failure was significant. Thoracic surgery recommended a bronchoscopic needle aspiration to obtain tissue; however, an endobronchial ultrasound (EBUS) was already booked. Meanwhile, the patient’s anemia, depression, and cardiovascular status were re-assessed and optimized. Dr. Singh discussed a “palliative intent” approach, emphasizing quality of life while considering systemic chemotherapy if the patient and family agreed.

On March 25, Thomas underwent an EBUS‑guided transbronchial needle aspiration (TBNA) of the right hilar lymph node. The pathology report returned five days later: a poorly differentiated adenocarcinoma with frequent mitotic figures and no squamous differentiation. Immunohistochemistry confirmed strong TTF‑1 positivity, ruling against primary squamous cell changes. The tumor was staged as T3N2M0 (Stage IIIA according to the AJCC), but the medical team quickly understood that the aggressive biology and comorbidities would make cure unlikely.

In an effort to preserve life‑sustaining quality, Dr. Singh recommended a chemotherapy regimen of carboplatin combined with pemetrexed, a schedule that had manageable cardiopulmonary side‑effects in many COPD patients. Thomas was scheduled for the first cycle on April 2. His first cycle, however, was interrupted by profound fatigue, exacerbated anemia (hemoglobin dropped to 8.8 g/dL), and an exacerbation of COPD that required a prednisone steroid taper and a short course of antibiotics for pneumonia. Despite this, Thomas elected to proceed with a second cycle.

By late April, Thomas was still battling a 90‑minute breathlessness episode, which made walking from the restroom to bed an ordeal. He had begun to express feelings of helplessness – a pattern that had been noted during his depression screening. Dr. Singh and the psychiatric team initiated a more aggressive antidepressant protocol with sertraline, but Thomas remained emotionally drained. At his end‑of‑month follow‑up, the CT scan revealed an increase of the right upper lobe mass to 5.2 cm and a markedly larger, homogenous pleural effusion.

The pleural fluid was aspirated under ultrasound guidance. Cytology was positive for malignant cells, confirming malignant pleural effusion and revealing advanced disease with possible pleural involvement. The oncology team discussed that, given the mass’ growth despite systemic chemotherapy, and the new pleural involvement, the disease had advanced to Stage IV. The patient no longer tolerated the chemotherapy regimen; his performance status had declined to ECOG 3. The decision was made to shift from a curative “palliative” intent to an aggressive hospice‑focused care.

On May 5, Thomas was transitioned to a hospice unit. Margaret attended the last morning meeting; the medical team emphasized symptom management: oxygen supplementation at 2 L/min via nasal cannula to alleviate dyspnea, scheduled IV bisphosphonate infusions for bone pain control, and daily low‑dose morphine for breakthrough pain. His anemia was re‑evaluated; packed RBC transfusion was given to reach a hemoglobin of 11 g/dL, but the benefits were limited. The oncology nurse encouraged Margaret to focus on the patient’s comfort rather than invasive diagnostics.

In the weeks that followed, Thomas’s cough became less frequent but more productive, turning to thick, blood‑laden sputum on occasions. Episodes of shortness of breath intensified with minimal exertion; the hospice oxygen requirement escalated from two to four litres. His fevers, although intermittent, became more pronounced; a repeat chest X‑ray showed a right‑sided, fluid‑filled chest cavity but no new consolidations. A palliative stent was suggested but deemed unnecessary. Thomas’s depression remained, but the hospice psychiatrist offered supportive counseling to Margaret.

On June 11, Thomas collapsed during a brief bathroom bout. The emergency call and ambulance arrival resulted in the patient being unresponsive. Despite resuscitative efforts with chest compressions, intravenous vasopressors, and emergent intubation, the heart rate remained arrhythmic and the oxygen saturation did not recover. The decision was made—after consultation with the hospice physician and Margaret—to withdraw CPR after fifteen minutes and return the patient to comfort; Thomas was noted deceased at 8:07 p.m.

Thomas’s disease course, though it spanned a little over three months from the point of his first urgent‑care visit to his passing, was marked by rapid progression of a poorly differentiated pulmonary carcinoma, complicated by chronic lung disease, anemia, depression, and cardiovascular comorbidities that limited therapeutic options. The aggressive biology of the tumor outpaced the supportive care that could have been offered given his fragile pulmonary and systemic reserve. The narrative ends in a stark reminder that for patients with multifactorial lung disease, the window to intervene is often small, and the trajectory quickly shifts from aggressive management toward comfort and dignity.
