### A 28‑Year‑Old Woman with a Worsening Crohn’s Disease Flare – A Clinical Narrative

**Prelude – Ten Years of Chronic Inflammation** At 18, Maya Patel (28 f) had been living with Crohn’s disease for a decade. A young woman who loved sports and a “fast‑food” lifestyle, her mother’s history of ulcerative colitis had given her a family genetic backdrop. The early‑onset condition was complicated by peripheral arthritis, erythema nodosum, and an anal fistula that had been intermittently draining. Her disease course was “classic” for an early‑onset, NOD2/CARD15‑positive phenotype: frequent flares, progressive stricturing, and a predilection for the terminal ileum and perianal region.

Maya had managed her symptoms on a baseline regimen of mesalamine and a tapering course of low‑dose prednisone at each flare. She also used ibuprofen at the onset of joint pain, convinced it was “just a normal ache.” She smoked cigarettes nightly, a habit that she admitted to herself as a coping mechanism for workplace stress. Her diet was skewed toward high‑fat processed foods, reflective of her busy life as a full‑time graphic designer.

Over the years, Maya had developed anemia of chronic disease, manifested by low ferritin levels, but her hemoglobin had remained above 10 g/dL, a threshold she and her doctor found tolerable. Her peripheral arthritis, while painful, had been managed with non‑steroidal drugs and occasional steroid bursts. Erythema nodosum had flared sporadically, mostly in the shin, and the anal fistula had required two seton placements.

---

#### Episode Begins – “I Can’t Ignore the Pain Anymore”

**Week 0 – First Symptoms** Maya noticed a dull, cramping pain in the RLQ that had started a week ago. It came with 2–3 loose stools a day, an intermittent low‑grade fever (99.5 °F), and an abrupt, unintentional 6 lb weight loss over the past month. She also had a mild rash over her shins that developed as red nodules—typical of erythema nodosum. However, in her mind these were “stress‑related” and could be the after‑effects of a late‑night project deadline.

She tried over‑the‑counter NSAIDs for joint pain, but they seemed to worsen the abdominal discomfort. The next day, the pain intensified around the anus, and she noticed a greenish discharge that smelled foul. She feared an infection and decided to see her primary care physician (PCP) at a local urgent‑care clinic.

**Week 1 – The First Clinical Encounter** The urgent‑care physician, focusing on a possible gastrointestinal infection, ordered a CBC and a basic metabolic panel. The labs were notable for mild anemia (Hb 10.2 g/dL) and a mildly elevated ESR (35 mm/h). The patient’s CRP was 9 mg/L (normal <5). Because of the greenish discharge, a PPD test for tuberculosis was performed, and the result was negative. The physician, interpreting the picture as “acute infectious proctitis,” prescribed a short course of oral doxycycline (100 mg bid for 7 days) and NSAIDs for pain.

Maya left the clinic with a prescription for ibuprofen 400 mg every 6 hours as needed. She rationalized that the discharge was “just a minor issue” and that the antibiotic would help.

**Week 2 – Rapid Deterioration** After a few days, the abdominal pain persisted and worsened. The stool frequency rose to 4–5 episodes per day, now containing mucus and occasional blood. She also noted that the erythema nodosum rash had become more pronounced and that she could not lie down due to perianal pain. The diarrhea brought a mild fever (100.1 °F) and night sweats. Her fatigue had escalated—she could not work through the day without stopping.

Maya’s wife urged her to see a gastroenterologist. Maya, feeling a sense of fatalism due to a decade of chronic illness, scheduled an appointment for the next day.

**Week 3 – The Endoscopy Decision** At the gastroenterology clinic, the physician (Dr. Singh) performed a physical exam: RLQ tender, erythematous perianal skin, a fistulous opening with purulent drainage, and a tender ankle joint consistent with peripheral arthritis. She noted a small, shallow ulceration at the anal verge. A punch biopsy would be required to differentiate between Crohn’s and ulcerative colitis.

Dr. Singh recommended a colonoscopy with terminal ileum intubation and biopsies to confirm inflammatory activity. Maya’s anxiety spiked; she was worried about the costs and procedural risks. She did not fully grasp that the colonoscopy was not merely an investigation but an early therapeutic decision.

**Week 4 – Colonoscopy & Findings** The colonoscopy revealed cobblestone mucosa, deep ulcerations, and skip lesions extending from the terminal ileum to the sigmoid colon. Multiple linear fissures and a small fistula tract were confirmed in the perianal area. Histology confirmed active Crohn’s disease with granulomatous inflammation—no evidence of infectious colitis or malignancy. The colonoscopy also uncovered a 5 mm ulcer in the ileum and a 2 cm stenosis proximal to the ileocolic valve.

The procedure established the flare: acute, transmural inflammation with evidence of stricturing and fistula activity. The doctor recommended initiating a biologic agent (anti‑TNF α therapy) and discontinuing NSAIDs. However, Maya’s smoking history and NOD2 positivity placed her in the “high‑risk flare” category, raising concern for rapid progression even with biologic therapy.

**Week 5 – First Biologic Trial** A two‑week delay (clinic scheduling, insurance authorization) ensued. Maya finally started infliximab 5 mg/kg at infusion center. The first infusion was uneventful but a week later she developed a mild upper respiratory infection (URTI) with cough, low‑grade fever, and fatigue. She was managed with amoxicillin. After the second infusion (after the URTI resolved), she reported “a little better”—her stool frequency decreased to 3 times per day and the perianal discharge improved. She felt hopeful, feeling that her disease was finally under control.

Unfortunately, the relief was short‑lived. By the end of month 1, she began experiencing a resurgence of symptoms: RLQ pain, 4–5 loose stools daily, the perianal region started draining again, and her rash returned with new lesions. She felt fatigued, had lost an additional 5 lb, and her Hb dropped to 9.4 g/dL. She still used ibuprofen sporadically for ankle pain.

#### Hard “Worsening” – A Complicated Course

**Month 1 – Clinical Decision Point** At her gastroenterologist’s office, Dr. Singh noted a rising CRP (41 mg/L) and rising fecal calprotectin (800 µg/g). She recognized the pattern: a non–response to infliximab. In a high‑risk patient, the standard of care is to measure anti‑drug antibodies. A trough level of 0.2 µg/mL indicated loss of response, and anti‑infliximab antibodies were present.

Dr. Singh discussed options: switch to a second anti‑TNF (adalimumab) with higher dosing or add immunomodulators (azathioprine). Maya, tired of switching, worried about the cost and potential side effects. She also felt that her daily reality — her “fast‑food” meals, her work demands, and her smoking habit — might be contributing to the relentless flare. She reluctantly agreed to add azathioprine.

**Month 2 – Azathioprine Trial & Adverse Effect** Azathioprine (2.5 mg/kg) was started with a 3‑month run‑in to monitor for leukopenia. Two weeks later, she developed a sore throat, low‑grade fever, and a mild rash over the chest; the labs revealed a drop in absolute neutrophil count (ANC 0.3 × 10⁹/L). She was held on azathioprine and admitted for supportive care. A bone marrow biopsy revealed myelodysplastic changes consistent with azathioprine toxicity. The medication was discontinued.

In parallel, her pain escalated. She started using a higher dose of ibuprofen (600 mg TID) for peripheral arthritis, which she did without notifying her doctors. Her physician noted that NSAIDs can worsen Crohn’s symptoms and perianal fistulas.

**Month 3 – Escalation to Combination Biologic Therapy** A repeat colonoscopy was planned; however, Maya, feeling hopeless, missed the appointment. In the meantime, Dr. Singh performed a transrectal ultrasound which showed new fluid collections around the fistula tract and a 12 mm abscess cavity. She recommended IV methylprednisolone 40 mg daily for a week to control inflammation and surgical consultation for incision and drainage (I&D).

Maya’s anxiety and fear of hospitalization limited her compliance: she skipped appointments and missed the steroid taper. When she eventually went to an urgent‑care visit for worsening perianal pain, the MRI revealed an “increasing complex perianal fistula” with multiple tracks and abscesses. Dr. Singh, now seeing a frank flare with complications, decided to initiate a second biologic: adalimumab 160 mg subcutaneously at week 0, 80 mg at week 2, then 40 mg every other week.

**Month 4 – Clinical Deterioration** She started on adalimumab but only two weeks later developed a high‑grade fever (102 °F), chills, and new-onset bloody diarrhea. Labs: Leukocytosis (WBC 15 k/µL), CRP 75 mg/L, ESR 60 mm/h. The stool culture was positive for E. coli O157:H7, but she was given empiric ciprofloxacin. By the end of the week, the perianal abscess had enlarged, and her skin now had new necrotic lesions over the shins (likely a severe flare of erythema nodosum or even a more systemic infection).

She was admitted for IV antibiotics and surgical consultation. Dr. Singh considered a combined surgical‑medical approach: incision, drainage, and possible colectomy. Maya was heavily fatigued, had lost 10 lb, and her Hb had dropped to 8.5 g/dL.

**Month 5 – Surgical Decision** The surgeons recommended a terminal ileostomy with possible future resection pending disease control. The GI team advised that the surgery was a definitive step for a patient with a high risk of complications, NOD2 positivity, perianal disease, and poor response to biologics. In spite of her hesitation, she consented.

In the operating room, the surgeon found a thickened, fibrotic ileocolic junction with several abscesses. A partial ileocolic resection with a hand‑sewn anastomosis was completed. The perianal tract was packed.

**Month 6 – Post‑Operative Course** Maya recuperated for a week in the surgical unit. Her stool frequency decreased, RLQ pain improved, and perianal drainage stopped. However, she developed a moderate postoperative ileus, requiring nasogastric lavage and parenteral nutrition for 3 days. Her hemoglobin plateaued at 8.3 g/dL. She was discharged with a low‑fat diet plan, a prescription for amoxicillin to cover for the postoperative infection, and a new dose of adalimumab.

Despite the surgery, Maya’s disease did not settle. A follow‑up colonoscopy 8 weeks after surgery revealed mild active disease in the remaining colon and a small residual fistula; the inflammatory markers remained high (CRP 50 mg/L). She was started on vedolizumab (an integrin inhibitor) as a third biologic. However, the slow onset of action and her high body mass index impaired its efficacy. While on vedolizumab, she relapsed again: 4–5 bloody stools daily, fever, and new perianal swelling.

Maya’s psychological stress grew. The relentless cycle of flare‑treatment‑relapse took a toll; she felt “trapped” and started using cigarettes more heavily (up to 20 cigs/day). Her depression worsened, and she stopped attending therapy.

**Month 9 – Final Decline** At a routine visit, her labs showed: Hb 8.0 g/dL, CRP 60 mg/L, ESR 55 mm/h. Colonoscopy revealed a new area of ulceration in the ascending colon, and imaging suggested a new, small abscess near the anastomosis. Dr. Singh explained that with each flare, the disease was evolving into a refractory, transmural type: stenosis forming, fistulae forming, and the mucosal lining compromised.

After a lengthy discussion and with Maya’s consent, a decision was made to proceed to a total proctocolectomy with ileo‑anal pouch. Maya’s parents had no family support structure; she had been working part‑time to make ends meet. The surgery was scheduled in 2 weeks, with a projected recovery period of 8–12 weeks.

**Month 12 – Unresolved Outlook** Maya’s disease course remains one of chronic flare, refractory to multiple biologics, compounded by her active smoking, high‑fat diet, and NSAID use. A new surgery has been scheduled; the outcome remains uncertain—her quality of life, while improved in pain control, is still heavily affected by ongoing anemia, psychological stress, and the fear of further surgical interventions. The story underscores the interplay of genetics, lifestyle choices, comorbidities, and the relentless nature of a hard‑to‑treat Crohn’s disease flare.
