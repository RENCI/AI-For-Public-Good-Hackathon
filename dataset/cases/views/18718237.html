**Mr. Jamal Carter: A Clinical Narrative**

*Patient profile* 68‑year‑old African‑American man, retired factory worker, former smoker (30 pack‑years, quit 5 years ago), with a 12‑year history of type 2 diabetes mellitus, hypertension, chronic kidney disease Stage 3, benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH), and chronic prostatitis. Lives with a wife; diet high in saturated fats and processed foods; sedentary lifestyle. Family history positive for prostate cancer in his father (diagnosed at 65). Works in an agricultural setting, with known pesticide exposure.

---

### Visit 1: August 5 – “I’m tired and the bathroom trips are never-ending”

- **Chief complaints**: Persistent lower‑extremity fatigue, increased urinary frequency (approx. 12–15 times/day), nocturia (4–5 times/night), weak stream, occasional “hesitancy” on voiding. - **History**: Symptoms had been gradually worsening over the past 6 months. No hematuria, no fevers. Referred to PCP after a routine check‑up. - **Physical exam**: BMI 32 kg/m². BP 160/95 mmHg. Abdomen flat. Digital rectal exam (DRE) showed a 3‑cm, mildly hard, non‑tender prostate. - **Labs**: PSA 7.8 ng/mL (slightly above normal for age). Hemoglobin A1c 8.2%. Creatinine 2.1 mg/dL (baseline 2.0). CBC normal. - **Imaging**: Urinalysis normal. Trans‑abdominal ultrasound of kidneys: mild cortical scarring. - **Plan**: Initiate finasteride 5 mg daily for BPH, schedule a urology consult. Continue metformin and losartan, counsel on diet, encourage physical activity (walk).

---

### Visit 2: September 12 – “The bladder feels full all the time”

- **Chief complaints**: Intense bladder pressure, new onset of pain while urinating, occasional dribbling. - **History**: Urinary frequency now 15–18 trips/day. Reports a “tightness” sensation that feels as if the bladder is overfull even after voiding. No visible blood. - **Exam**: Unchanged DRE with 3‑cm prostate. No suprapubic tenderness. - **Labs**: PSA increased to 12.4 ng/mL. Fasting glucose 160 mg/dL. Creatinine 2.3 mg/dL. - **Imaging**: Pelvic MRI ordered: shows a 4 cm prostate mass infiltrating the bladder wall (extraprostatic extension) and enlarged right pelvic lymph nodes (approx. 1.5 cm). - **Plan**: Referral to urologist for staging. Discuss possible prostatectomy vs radiotherapy; note renal function may limit imaging contrast.

---

### Visit 3: September 28 – Urology Consultation

- **Assessment**: Clinical stage T3bN1M0 (extraprostatic extension into bladder, regional lymph nodes). PSA 12.4. - **Discussion**: Options: radical retropubic prostatectomy with bladder cuff excision vs definitive external beam radiation ± brachytherapy. - **Considerations**: Chronic kidney disease limits MRI contrast; potential for postoperative complications (ureteral injury). - **Decision**: Based on comorbidities and patient's preference for a definitive surgical approach, the plan is robot‑assisted radical prostatectomy with bladder cuff resection and bilateral pelvic lymph node dissection. - **Pre‑op**: Optimize blood pressure (switch losartan + add amlodipine), hold finasteride for 4 weeks. Continue metformin. Renal consultation for perioperative renal-protective strategies.

---

### Visit 4: October 6 – Surgery Day

- **Procedure**: RARP with bladder cuff excision; 12 lymph nodes harvested, 3 positive for metastasis. - **Intra‑op**: Slight difficulty due to prostatitis‐related fibrosis; operative time 4.2 h. - **Post‑op**: Foley catheter in place. Drainage output modest. Hemodynamics stable. - **Adverse event**: Mild postoperative nausea, managed with ondansetron.

---

### Visit 5: October 14 – Post‑operative Follow‑up

- **Status**: No acute complications. Catheter removed on day 7. - **Pathology**: Gleason 4 + 4 = 8, pT3b, margin‑positive on anterior wall. Lymph nodes: 3/12 positive. - **Adjuvant therapy**: Discussed radical retropubic radiation (70 Gy) and androgen blockade. Due to diabetes and CKD, hormonal therapy chosen: leuprolide 3.6 mg SC q3 months, enzalutamide 160 mg PO daily. - **Side effects**: Early signs of fatigue, mild hot flashes. - **Plan**: Initiate radiation 4 weeks post‑op. Continue PSA monitoring monthly.

---

### Visit 6: December 10 – First PSA after 4 weeks of radiation

- **PSA**: 10.1 ng/mL (drop from pre‑op 12.4 but still above baseline). - **Symptoms**: No urinary retention; mild dysuria that improves with analgesia. - **Imaging**: Follow‑up CT chest/abdomen/pelvis shows a new 2.5 cm pulmonary nodule and an enlarged sacral lymph node. - **Diagnosis**: Distant metastatic disease (M1a).

---

### Visit 7: January 20 – Multidisciplinary Tumor Board

- **Discussion**: At stage pT3bN1M1, overall survival improves with systemic therapy. - **Plan**: Transition to systemic chemotherapy plus androgen deprivation. Start docetaxel 75 mg/m² IV q3 weeks + prednisone 5 mg BID + continue leuprolide. - **Kidney**: Nephrology recommends dose adjustment; docetaxel 60 mg/m². - **Supportive care**: Bisphosphonate therapy for bone metastases prophylaxis, vitamin D and calcium.

---

### Visit 8: March 15 – Chemotherapy Cycle 2

- **Symptoms**: Progressive fatigue, dyspnea on exertion. - **Physical exam**: Palpable left upper leg swelling (deep vein thrombosis confirmed). - **Labs**: Platelet count 90 k/µL. INR 1.1. - **Treatment**: Started enoxaparin 1 mg/kg SC daily for DVT.

---

### Visit 9: April 28 – Third Cycle, Worsening Course

- **Symptom progression**: Severe anorexia, weight loss of 12 lb over 3 months. Urinary symptoms recur: frequency, urgency, urinary retention necessitating intermittent catheterization. - **Imaging**: PET/CT shows progression of pulmonary and hepatic metastases. Bladder wall thickening with residual extraprostatic disease. - **Lab**: Creatinine 3.0 mg/dL (CKD Stage 4). - **Plan**: Evaluate for hospice enrollment; however, the patient expresses desire to continue aggressive therapy.

---

### Visit 10: May 30 – Final Clinical Decision

- **Assessment**: Rapid disease progression, metastatic burden high, organ dysfunction (kidneys) limits further chemotherapy. - **Discussion**: Palliative radiation to symptomatic lesions (bone, bladder). Initiate best‑available systemic therapy: palliative docetaxel (dose 45 mg/m²) + steroids. - **Goals of care**: Pain control, maintenance of urinary function, and quality of life.

---

*Outcome:* Despite an aggressive multimodal approach—initial surgical resection, adjuvant radiation, androgen deprivation, systemic chemotherapy—the disease progressed rapidly. The patient’s cancer became widespread with extraprostatic and distant metastatic disease, compounded by comorbid diabetes, hypertension, CKD, and chronic prostatitis. By the end of the documented course, his renal function deteriorated, his mobility limited, and his overall prognosis was poor. Care pivoted to palliative measures, aligning with the intended outcome of worsening disease.
