**A 35‑Year‑Old Woman’s Journey Through a Worsening Course of Relapsing‑Remitting Multiple Sclerosis**

When 35‑year‑old Maya first noticed a flicker of double vision at the office, she chalked it up to the long hours at her computer and the stress of a looming deadline. She hadn’t been feeling well in the way that a flu would make her feel, but she did notice a mild, burning tingling on the inner left thigh that came and went, almost like the phantom sensation some patients feel after a long flight. Maya had been living with relapsing‑remitting multiple sclerosis (RRMS) for six years, diagnosed at 29 after a first optic neuritis episode that left her with a subtle field defect. She was on intramuscular interferon‑beta‑1a (Avonex) once a week and had had a dozen relapses—most of them brief and self‑limited—over the past five years. She was also grappling with a chronic, multifaceted medical profile: a 10‑year history of rheumatoid arthritis (RA) she managed with low‑dose methotrexate and adalimumab, hypertension on lisinopril, sleep‑disturbance insomnia that kept her up for hours, and a depressive disorder that she had been treating with sertraline. With a BMI of 30.8 and a 10‑pack‑year smoking history, her risk profile was high for disease progression.

---

### Episode One – A Subtle Onset

**Week 0‑1 – Quiet Unsteadiness**

Maya awoke with a feeling of “stiffness” in her left arm. She told her sister she’d felt “like a bolt of static” running down the arm, but nothing else. She pushed the thought aside as stress and decided to finish a pending spreadsheet. However, as the day progressed, faint, intermittent blurriness hit the left side of her vision, especially when she switched between her computer's high‑contrast white background and the dark gray of the monitor. She also found her bladder feeling “always full.”

She tried to ignore the symptoms. Her depression had made her wary of seeing new doctors; she feared that further medical interventions would only compound her anxiety. She continued her nightly dose of sertraline (100 mg) and drank a glass of wine each evening as a way to ease the creeping anxiety that had, in the months since her last relapse, been a constant companion.

**Week 2 – The Urgent Care Visit**

Maya’s daughter noticed the wobble in Maya’s gait the next morning, a subtle clumsy misstep that became apparent when Maya walked the two‑block subway to the grocery store. She told her mother, who insisted she see a doctor. Maya walked into the urgent‑care center, clutching a phone‑case that had been shaken in a spill. Dr. Patel, the attending, noted a mild left‑sided upper‑extremity weakness (Medical Research Council grade 4/5) and a slightly slowed left‑sided hand grip. No ocular abnormalities were noted on bedside fundoscopy. The neurologic exam was largely unremarkable except for the subtle gait imbalance and the mild visual blurring that resolved after the patient rested in the waiting room.

Because of the subtlety of the findings—a mild weakness that could be attributed to the “morning stiffness” many office workers experience—the urgen­t‑care visit ended with a recommendation to return to her primary care provider if symptoms worsened. Maya left the center, noting only a slight improvement after a short rest.

**Week 4 – Outpatient Neurology Appointment**

The next month, Maya’s symptoms grew slightly more pronounced. She began to feel a “tickling” sensation in her left leg that spread to the left forearm. The visual blurring, once intermittent, now lingered for a few minutes. Maya’s daughter reported that Maya had been having trouble sleeping; she was waking up gasping before having to use the bathroom.

Maya's primary care doctor, Dr. Lin, asked if she had done any imaging since the last MRI two years ago. Maya had missed her scheduled MRI appointments for months, citing her depression, lack of energy, and transportation issues. Dr. Lin scheduled a neurologic consultation for that same week.

At the neurology office, Dr. Patel performed a thorough neurologic exam: there was a 60‑% reduction of sensation to pinprick in the left thigh and calf, mild left arm pronation weakness, and an inability to perform finger‑to‑nose coordination on the left side. She asked Maya about any recent trauma or infections—none— and she also inquired about her sleep quality, medication adherence, and lifestyle habits.

**Decision Point 1 – MRI and Steroid Trial**

Given the constellation of subtle but progressive symptoms, Dr. Patel advised a brain MRI with contrast. Maya had not had a scan in three years. She reluctantly agreed; the radiology technologist noted that Maya had a faint aura of anxiety as she lay in the MRI machine, but she completed the scan successfully.

The MRI revealed a new enhancing lesion in the left posterior parietal white matter (size 1.2 cm) with T2 hyperintensity, and a second non‑enhancing area in the right juxtacortical region. There were no cystic changes or mass effect. Maya’s MRI findings coupled with her neurologic exam confirmed a new inflammatory demyelinating event—a relapse.

Dr. Patel recommended starting high‑dose intravenous methylprednisolone (1 g daily) for five days, followed by a taper of oral prednisone over four weeks, to control the inflammatory process. Maya expressed concern about the side effects: elevated blood sugar, insomnia, mood swings. She also worried about her hypertension and had been told by her cardiologist that steroids could worsen her blood pressure. Dr. Patel explained that the benefits outweighed the risks in the acute setting and that she would monitor her blood pressure daily at home and during follow‑up visits.

Maya agreed, perhaps because a relapse that could damage her vision or speech would be too painful to risk. She received the steroid infusion in the hospital and started tapering upon discharge. She was advised to keep her hypertension medications, watch for mood changes, and re‑check blood glucose.

### Episode Two – A Relapse That Reverses Her Progress

**Week 5 – Slippage**

Two weeks after finishing the steroid taper, Maya noticed a new symptom: on the left side of her face, a “tight‑jaw” feeling that would spike on the edge of a conversation. She had a subtle left facial droop when she mimicked a big smile. She thought it might be a migraine or a dental issue and postponed her follow‑up. At the end of the month, she could no longer hold her tablet's grip when checking the grocery list—her left hand tremored like a shivering leaf. Her daughter reminded her she needed to see the neurologist.

**Week 7 – Return to Neurology**

At her neurology visit two weeks later, Dr. Patel noted left facial weakness (House‑Brackmann grade III), mild left hand tremor, and increased spasticity in the left leg. Maya had also had a faint, transient sensation of “feeling like someone was touching her left forearm”. Dr. Patel ran a new MRI, which revealed enhancement along the left pyramidal tract (corticospinal tract). The scan also showed an old lesion in the left internal capsule that had not been previously noted.

The clinical picture suggested another new inflammatory event—yet Maya was still on low‑dose prednisone taper (50 mg daily), and her depression medication, sertraline, had been at a steady 100 mg daily for the last three months.

Given the rapidly escalating relapse pattern, Dr. Patel considered stepping up to a higher‑efficacy disease‑modifying therapy (DMT). She explained the options: natalizumab, alemtuzumab, ocrelizumab, cladribine. Maya was uneasy about immunosuppression, citing her rheumatoid arthritis. She had been on adalimumab (Humira) for RA—an anti‑TNF biologic that is not indicated for MS. She worried that adding an immunosuppressant would increase infection risk and might exacerbate her RA. She also feared the possibility of worsening her hypertension and depression. Maya preferred to stay on interferon but recognized the pattern of frequent relapses.

**Decision Point 2 – Escalation with Ocrelizumab Amid Dual Immune Challenges**

Dr. Patel suggested starting ocrelizumab (the first anti‑CD20 monoclonal antibody for RRMS and primary progressive MS). Ocrelizumab requires an infusion every six months; it depletes B‑cells and has a lower infection risk compared to alemtuzumab. Dr. Patel also proposed tapering methotrexate over several weeks to reduce overlapping immunosuppression. She coordinated with Maya’s rheumatologist, who agreed to reduce methotrexate to 7.5 mg one‑in‑two‑weeks, and advised the patient to report any new infections quickly.

Despite these reassurances, Maya’s anxiety flared; she refused to commit to the infusion clinic and chose to continue monthly interferon. She also missed her next neurologic follow‑up.

The clinical picture worsened. In the months that followed, Maya’s left arm weakness progressed from 4/5 to 3/5, and she experienced increased difficulty with speech intelligibility. Her sleep remained disrupted, and she reported a low mood with feelings of hopelessness. Her wife began to feel that Maya was “taking on less responsibility”.

### Episode Three – Severe Neurologic Decline Requiring Plasma Exchange

**Month 10 – A Worsening Relapse**

Three months after her last neurologic appointment, Maya collapsed into her office chair, the left arm shaking uncontrollably. She was taken to the emergency department where a third MRI revealed new enhancing lesions in the cervical spinal cord, specifically at the C3‑C5 level, with the “doughnut” appearance that sometimes signals active plaques at the spinal level. Her neurologic exam was dramatic: left upper limb strength 2/5, left lower limb strength 2–3/5, brisk reflexes in left extremities, and gait that required a hand‑crank. She also had urinary urgency and constipation.

Dr. Patel, now in her care, initiated a high‑dose methylprednisolone pulse (1 g IV daily) for the first five days. Maya’s blood glucose rose from 115 mg/dL to 210 mg/dL, and her blood pressure spiked to 170/100 mmHg. She was treated with insulin sliding‑scale and required a morning dose of labetalol for her hypertension. During the steroid pulse, her insomnia worsened, and she developed a tearful episode at night, citing a “fear of being alone” and “not wanting to lose my dignity”.

When her neurologic status remained unchanged after the steroid therapy, Dr. Patel proposed therapeutic plasma exchange (plasmapheresis), a salvage treatment that removes circulating pathogenic antibodies and inflammatory mediators. Plasmapheresis, however, requires central venous access and repeated sessions—two or three per week for three to five weeks.

**Decision Point 3 – The Harsh Reality of Plasmapheresis**

Maya, who was already coping with a depressive episode manifested as suicidal ideation (she had a brief phone conversation with a crisis line earlier), was hesitant to undergo the procedure. Dr. Patel scheduled a meeting with Maya’s primary care, rheumatology, and a psychiatric liaison. Together, they weighed the risks of infection (due to low B‑cell counts if ocrelizumab were started), the chance of worsening RA flares (due to the immunomodulatory shift), and the psychosocial burden of repeated hospital visits. Maya eventually agreed to three sessions of plasma exchange, but with the proviso that she wanted to have a scheduled psychiatric session after each session to process the trauma of the hospital experience.

After the third plasma exchange, Maya’s weakness modestly improved (to left upper limb 3/5, left lower limb 3/5). She still had urinary retention and had to use intermittent catheterization. Her MRI after the last session showed a decrease in contrast enhancement of the cervical cord, but a new lesion appeared in the right thalamus—an MRI sign of continued disease activity.

### Episode Four – A Stalled Plateau and a New DMT Dilemma

**Month 15 – A New Era of Stagnation**

Maya’s neurologic status plateaued. She had recovered a bit—left arm movements more controlled, speech clearer—but she was no longer able to stand without assistance. She had chronic low‑grade pain in knees and shoulders—a comorbidity of RA and MS—requiring periodic physiotherapy. Her sleep remained fragmentary. She now used a manual wheelchair at home and in the clinic. Her depression had worsened; she was no longer taking sertraline because she felt it induced “brain fog” and “exhaustion.” Dr. Patel had suggested restarting sertraline at a lower dose (25 mg) and adding a sleep aid (eszopiclone) to her regimen, but Maya remained skeptical.

Her rheumatologist had stopped methotrexate after her most recent infection (nasopharyngitis) and had switched her adalimumab to a biosimilar. Yet her RA remained well controlled. Dr. Patel continued to advocate for a high‑efficacy DMT but recommended a cautious approach due to the risk of opportunistic infections.

After detailed counseling with Dr. Patel, Maya agreed to start cladribine tablets. Cladribine was chosen because it offers a short course (two annual treatment courses) and may be more acceptable to Maya because it does not require daily infusion or frequent clinic visits. The plan included a complete blood count and immunoglobulin assessment prior to initiation.

**Cladribine Course and Adverse Events**

Maya received cladribine 3.5 mg/kg over five days in her first 10‑day cycle, then again six months later. A week after the first cycle, she developed a fever (38.9°C) and mucosal thrush. She was prescribed oral fluconazole and a course of broad‑spectrum antibiotics for a possible sinus infection. Her blood counts dropped modestly, but her neutrophil count remained above 1.0 × 10^9/L. Because she had a high BMI, her appetite was poor, and she lost about 4 kg over the next month.

Maya’s depression flared anew during the cladribine course. She reported feeling “lost” and “like nobody cared.” She saw a psychotherapist twice a week. Her wife had been involved in counseling to improve communication and coping. Maya began a daily journal to monitor mood swings, food intake, and sleep patterns.

---

### Episode Five – The Unavoidable Deterioration

**Month 21 – New Symptoms Appear**

Three months after the second cladribine cycle, Maya reported a new problem: a sudden, severe burning pain in her right calf that persisted at night, along with a tingling sensation in her right toes. Clinically, Dr. Patel noted an absent ankle jerk and reduced proprioception. She ordered a lumbar puncture that revealed an oligoclonal band pattern (typical of RRMS). An MRI of the lumbar spine identified a new enhancing lesion at the L5‑S1 level—consistent with a progressive demyelinating process.

Maya had been following the same sleep‑inducing protocol as before, but she still woke up multiple times during the night. Her blood pressure had fluctuated, ranging between 120/70 and 160/96 mmHg; she was not fully compliant with her antihypertensives due to a fear that steroids or immunosuppressants would worsen her hypertension. In an early morning emergency visit, the neurologist found that her urinary retention had worsened: she could no longer void naturally and needed intermittent catheterization every 12 hours.

**Clinical Decision Point – Acceptance of a Progressive Trajectory**

At a comprehensive neurologic review, Dr. Patel explained that Maya’s disease had entered a state of secondary progressive worsening—a transition from early relapse-remitting to a continuous decline. This transition is not reversible; the focus shifts from controlling acute relapses to managing chronic progression, symptom management, and supportive care. Dr. Patel recommended a multidisciplinary approach: neurologist, rheumatologist, psychiatrist, physiotherapist, occupational therapist, dietitian, and social worker.

Maya was advised to maintain her RA medication (adalimumab remained, but the rheumatologist advised careful monitoring for infections). She was counselled on a low‑salt, low‑fat diet to control blood pressure. A nutritionist devised a meal plan to counter her weight stagnation (BMI 30.5) and address vitamin D deficiency—she was started on 2000 IU vitamin D daily. The physical therapist designed a program of gait training with a wheelchair, stretching, and gentle strengthening exercises within her endurance limits.

Her sleep continued to be disturbed; Dr. Patel referred her to a sleep specialist for polysomnography. Early evidence of REM sleep dysregulation was common in MS. The sleep specialist suggested cognitive behavioral therapy for insomnia (CBT‑I), along with timed exercise during the day to promote natural sleep cycles.

**Mental Health – The Unrelenting Depression**

Maya’s psychiatric care intensified. The mental health team shifted her antidepressant to venlafaxine, monitoring for blood pressure changes. She attended weekly psychotherapy focusing on coping strategies, grief over lost mobility, and acceptance of disease progression. Family support groups were introduced, and her husband attended counseling sessions to adapt.

**Outcome – Worsening**

Despite intensified, multidisciplinary efforts, Maya’s condition continued to decline. Over the next nine months, she began to lose further voluntary motor control in her left hand, developing a claw hand that impaired her ability to handle daily tasks. She required assistance with feeding and personal hygiene. Her urinary incontinence increased, leading to urinary tract infections. Her blood pressure became more labile, requiring two antihypertensive medications concurrently.

Her depression deepened. She reported feelings of guilt for requiring so much care and a sense that life’s meaningful activities were gone. She struggled with nighttime insomnia and had frequent nightmares of being unable to move.

In the last clinic visit, Maya expressed a desire to “take control” by asking if a spinal cord stimulator might help with spasticity and pain. Dr. Patel discussed spinal cord stimulators with a specialized pain clinic. Maya had a comprehensive consultation and was placed on a trial. After a month, she reported mild reduction in muscle spasticity but still had persistent pain episodes. She opted to continue with the trial but remained uncertain about the long future.

### Postscript – Lessons from a Hard Case

Maya’s journey illustrates a realistic, medically plausible course of a highly active RRMS patient with multiple risk factors and comorbidities. Her disease trajectory demonstrates:

- **Atypical/subtle initial manifestations** that can masquerade as stress or other nonspecific symptoms; a subtle optic neuritis and tingling early on.

- **Compounding factors**: obesity, smoking, vitamin D deficiency, high baseline disease activity; these factors accelerate relapse frequency and disease progression.

- **Complicated comorbidities**: depression, anxiety, RA, hypertension, sleep disturbance, and the psychological burden of frequent hospital visits and complex medication regimens.

- **Decision points** that challenge both patient autonomy and clinical recommendations: refusing high‑efficacy immunomodulators, hesitating to undergo plasma exchange, declining certain antibiotics due to fear of infection, and balancing RA treatment with MS management.

- **Progressive deterioration** despite multimodal interventions: escalation to cladribine, yet the disease ultimately transitioned into a secondary‑progressive trajectory with continuous loss of function.

- **Holistic supportive care** as the final frontier: psychosocial support, physiotherapy, occupational therapy, dietitian guidance, and comprehensive pain management.

Maya’s case is emblematic of real-world scenarios where early subtle symptoms are misattributed, comorbidities add layers of complexity, and multiple risk factors accelerate disease progression—a sobering reminder that early proactive intervention and coordinated multidisciplinary care are paramount.
