**A Narrative of a 28‑year‑old Athlete’s Concussion Course**

When the 28‑year‑old university football player, Jared, slipped on the wet turf during a Friday night training session, the world did not just tilt— it slammed. A collision with a teammate’s chest and a sudden jolt to the back of his head knocked him off his feet. He fell hard, eyes closed for a brief, bewildering span that left him feeling confused and disoriented. He was quickly escorted to the sidelines, where teammates—some of whom were on the field at the same time—helped him sit up.

Jared’s first post‑collide feelings were unmistakable: a throbbing, pressure‑like headache behind his eyes; a faint ringing in his ears; a dizzy spinning sense that made it hard to focus. He also had a nagging nausea that made him double‑take each sip of water. Jared’s history made his coaches nervous: he had suffered a concussion in the spring of 2019 that left him with days of blurred vision and a persistent headache; he had never fully recovered from that; he was currently feeling restless, on edge, and with a low mood that had been punctuated by a few weeks of migraines. On the night of the practice, he had also partied at a campus celebration, drinking a small amount of beer and arriving home without a proper night's sleep. All these risk factors had converged right at that moment.

**The first 24 hours: Immediate concerns and evaluation**

After the incident, Jared’s coach called the stadium nurse. She handed him a neuro‑cognitive screen— a rapid version of the Sport Concussion Assessment Tool (SCAT5). He scored lower than usual on the memory test and had a sluggish reaction time. The nurse was concerned enough to call the student health center. At 6 pm, Jared was taken to the campus urgent‑care clinic, where the physician repeated the SCAT and asked a series of questions concerning his post‑collision symptoms. The physician noted that Jared had no loss of consciousness beyond a brief flash, no vomiting, and no focal neurologic deficits. However, given his prior concussion and the current headache, the doctor explained that they would monitor him closely and would likely not order a CT scan unless symptoms worsened.

The clinic doctor gave Jared an analgesic— acetaminophen 500 mg every 6 h as needed—and explained that ibuprofen should be used sparingly because of Jared's recent alcohol intake and his tendency to develop headaches. He was told to rest, limit screen time, and to avoid alcohol for at least two weeks. Jared’s first question— "Will I have to miss the rest of the season?"— was met with an answer that was both reassuring and realistic: "You may need a few days, but most people recover in a week or less."

**Day 1‑2: Rest and symptom check**

Back at home, Jared tried to get a nap, but his insomnia made this difficult. Instead, he spent his afternoon indoors, scrolling through his phone, watching videos of his team’s games for comfort. His headaches continued— a dull, band‑like pressure that became more intense during the mid‑afternoon. He felt anxious about missing the next practice. The doctor had told him to monitor his symptom “check‑in” each hour; he noted a score of 8/10 on the headache severity scale and felt nauseated when standing quickly.

In the evening, after finishing a few episodes of his favorite show, Jared’s anxiety flared— he felt claustrophobic, his heart rate spiked, and he began questioning his own sanity. He stared at his phone while the lights flicked. The next morning, the doctor had called him in for a brief telehealth visit. Jared admitted to having difficulty sleeping and feeling "off" mentally. The doctor reassured him that the concussion can worsen mood and anxiety temporarily. She suggested a calming routine: dim lights at night, avoiding stimulating screens before bed, and a mild stretch session at a later time.

**Day 3‑4: Cognitive rest and gradual re‑introduction**

By the third day, the raw, racing headache began to subside. Jared could now sit, read for short bouts, and walk around the home without dizziness. However, he still avoided the gym or any physically demanding activity. His therapist, who was concurrently seeing him for his depression, recommended “cognitive rest” that included limiting complex tasks: no heavy studying, no detailed sports strategy sessions, no gaming. Jared accepted this, acknowledging that the brain needed space to heal.

The next day, the sports medicine physician scheduled an in‑person evaluation. Jared brought his SCAT results and was met in a quiet room where a neurologist conducted a thorough exam. No neurological deficits were present, but the neurologist used a computerized neurocognitive test (ImPACT) and found his reaction time slower than baseline by about 10 ms, and memory slightly reduced. The doctor explained that this was common in the early days post‑concussion. A key point came up: Jared’s past migraines might mimic and amplify headache severity, and the new headache could be a different “migrainous” pattern caused by brain injury. They agreed to treat him with a low dose of a migraine preventative (topiramate 25 mg nightly) to minimize the headache spikes that might derail his rest period.

**Day 5‑7: A cautious return to activity**

Jared’s pain was mild by day five; he could stand with his balance intact, had no vomiting, and his headaches were mostly mild throbbing that resolved with simple rest. The sports medicine team recommended a graded return to activity. Day six involved a short walk around the campus, staying under 12 minutes, and staying seated afterward. By day seven, he was allowed to sit on the sidelines during a practice, no participation yet, but he could re‑observe the team for about 20 minutes. He felt a mixture of relief – a gentle re‑introduction to the field's energy – and residual nervousness about pushing too soon.

Throughout this staged re‑introduction, the neurologist monitored his symptoms with SCAT5 daily. The scores improved: cognitive testing returned to baseline, reaction time to expected ranges. His sleep schedule improved; he began setting out a bedtime ritual: reading a book and avoiding screens half an hour before bed. The medication for migraine managed the occasional “red‑flag” headaches that might otherwise have prompted him to over‑rest. His anxiety remained, but the sports medicine physician suggested mindfulness exercises to reduce the panic around “missing the game”.

**Day 8‑10: Full recovery and a return to routine**

By day nine, Jared had no headaches, no dizziness, and a normal cognitive score. He returned fully to the field during a training drill on the evening of the tenth day. He was cautious but enthusiastic, aware of his limits. He felt his depression and anxiety have leveled; the lingering sense of loss of his prior equilibrium was replaced by a new confidence in the protocols that had guided his recovery.

**Clinical decision points**

- **Initial assessment**: Choosing to observe without imaging after confirming a low risk concussion profile, while planning close follow‑up. - **Medication**: The use of acetaminophen for analgesia, and the thoughtful decision to avoid ibuprofen because of alcohol history. - **Sleep hygiene**: The doctor’s specific advice on sleep to address Jared’s insomnia and its impact on recovery. - **Migraine prevention**: The introduction of a low dose topiramate to address overlapping migraine symptoms and decrease headache frequency. - **Graded return to activity**: Implementation of a structured, progressive return to play to protect against second impact.

**Outcome: Improving**

As Jared progressed through daily check‑ins, the pain subsided, his cognitive testing normalized, and by Day 10 he could participate in a controlled practice without symptoms. His sleep returned to a regular rhythm, his anxiety managed through a short mindfulness routine, and his mood improved. The narrative closes with Jared laying on the field after a final drill, staring at the sky, grateful for the patience and guidance from his medical teammates. The once‑dramatic collision had resulted in a challenging but manageable concussion, and the story concludes with Jared’s recovery and renewed focus on both his sport and his mental well‑being.
