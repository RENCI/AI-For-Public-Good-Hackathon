**Patient Narrative – A 35‑year‑old Female With an Acute Migraine Episode**

---

### Background

Lena, a 35‑year‑old graphic designer, has a long‑standing family history of migraine—her mother and two aunts suffer from episodic attacks. Lena’s life is punctuated by the usual stresses of a demanding job, intermittent depressive mood swings, and a chronic tendency toward anxiety. She experiences tension‑type headaches at least twice a month and has a history of difficulty falling and staying asleep. Her caffeine intake is high (about 4–5 cups of coffee a day), and she is currently on a combined oral contraceptive pill. Her menstrual cycle is fairly regular; the last period began exactly three weeks ago.

---

### Day 0 – The Inciting Moment

**5:30 p.m.** – After a long day at the studio, Lena’s left temporal region tingles with a low, dull ache. She attributes it to the hours she spent in front of the monitor and to her restless sleep the previous night.

**6:30 p.m.** – The ache intensifies into a throbbing pain, now involving the right orbital area. A mild visual aura (fleeting bright spots in her peripheral vision) flashes across the room. The aura lasts roughly **10 minutes** before the headache fully envelops her.

**7:00 p.m.** – She decides to stay home to finish a client presentation. Before bed, she drinks a **240 mL** cup of coffee, hoping the caffeine will keep her alert for a few more hours and also “mask” the headache flare. She then attempts to sleep in a 7‑hour interval, but her mind races, and she wakes repeatedly.

**Night** – Sleep is fragmented. The headache does not subside. Her anxiety rises as she feels the pressure to meet an upcoming deadline. She starts taking “tension‑type headache” analgesics (a 200 mg ibuprofen with a cup of tea), but the effect is minimal.

---

### Day 1 – Awakening to the Attack

**7:30 a.m.** – Lena wakes up to the sound of her phone vibrating. The headache is now **8/10** on the pain scale, throbbing with each heartbeat. The pain localizes to the right orbital region and spreads to her jaw and lower temples. She has nausea, but no vomiting. Her vision is clear except for occasional shimmering lights.

**8:30 a.m.** – She has a coffee to try to alleviate the pain, but the **caffeine withdrawal** from the previous night and the lingering headache diminish its effect. She tries a 600 mg dose of acetaminophen, with no relief. She also begins to feel dizzy, a sign of migraine progression.

**9:30 a.m.** – She feels the migraine start to “lock in” and realizes her work will be compromised. She leaves her office and calls her primary care provider (PCP).

---

### Day 1 – Clinical Decision Point 1: PCP Tele‑Visit

**10:00 a.m. (Tele‑visit)** – On the call, Lena describes her aura, throbbing pain, nausea, and lack of response to OTC analgesics. She mentions that the headache started **overnight** from her usual pattern of a tension headache that typically resolves within a few hours. She is also aware of multiple risk factors: familial migraine, hormonal fluctuations (she is mid‑cycle and on oral contraceptives), stress, irregular sleep, anxiety, high caffeine intake.

The PCP asks the standard red‑flag questions:

- Any visual loss, weakness, or speech difficulty? **No.** - Any preceding vomiting? **No.** - Recent head trauma? **No.** - Any neurological deficit? **No.**

All answers are reassuring. The PCP confirms that this is most likely an acute migraine episode.

**Prescription** – The PCP writes a **sumatriptan 6 mg oral tablet** (taken on a single dose) and a **diphenhydramine 25 mg** as an anti‑nausea adjunct. She is also given a rescue prescription for ibuprofen 600 mg to take if the triptan fails.

The PCP urges Lena to:

- **Hydrate** with water or electrolyte drinks. - **Sleep** as soon as possible (she is told not to stay awake further for the day). - **Limit caffeine** to the next day to avoid withdrawal‑induced flare. - **Use cold compress** or a pillow under the head. - **Reduce ambient light** and stay in a quiet room. - **Keep a headache diary** so she can see patterns for the future.

---

### Day 1 – Response to Acute Therapy

**10:45 a.m.** – Lena takes the sumatriptan, having followed instructions to have a light snack first. About **30 minutes later** (i.e., 11:15 a.m.), the throbbing pain starts to fade; the pressure lessens, and the nausea subsides. By **12:30 p.m.** (70 minutes after dosing), her pain rating falls to **2/10** and the aura has completely resolved. She feels relieved; the medication worked.

She notes in her diary: *“Triptan took about 30 minutes to start, total relief at 90 minutes. No side‑effects.”*

She also acknowledges that the **sleep interruption** might have contributed to the severity. She decides to adjust her habits.

---

### Day 1–2 – Post‑Acute Evaluation

**1:00 p.m.** – Lena calls her PCP again, requesting a follow‑up visit to discuss possible preventive measures. The PCP schedules her for an **in‑person appointment on Day 2** (Wednesday).

---

### Day 2 – In‑Person Visit

During this meeting, Lena reports:

- **One acute migraine** this month that resolved with triptan. - **Tension‑type headaches** monthly. - **Difficulty sleeping** after caffeine use. - **Cyclic mood changes** from mild depression to heightened anxiety. - **Regular use of caffeine** (4–5 cups per day). - **High levels of stress** at work.

The PCP performs a physical exam: normal neurological exam, blood pressure 118/72 mm Hg, heart rate 78 bpm. No neurological deficits. The PCP acknowledges the need for a preventive strategy due to the frequent occurrence of migraines exacerbated by hormonal fluctuation.

**Preventive Recommendation** – The PCP prescribes **topiramate 25 mg nightly** at first, with gradual titration to **100 mg nightly** over two weeks. She advises:

- **Caffeine reduction**: switch to half‑size cups, use non‑caffeinated tea later in the afternoon. - **Regular sleep schedule**: maintain a consistent bedtime and wake time. - **Stress management**: short breathing exercises, brief meditations, and a “worry period” scheduled each evening. - **Hydration**: 2–3 L water daily. - **Dietary adjustments**: avoid known migraine triggers such as chocolate, aged cheese, and alcohol.

She also receives a **brief CBT‑based anxiety coping plan** and is referred to a therapist specializing in chronic pain and anxiety.

---

### Day 3–7 – Implementation Phase

**Day 3** – Lena begins the topiramate and sees the first side effect: a mild tingling in her fingers. She notes this in her diary.

**Day 4–5** – She reduces caffeine to **2 cups per day** (morning). She uses a blue‑light blocking filter on her computer screen and takes breaks every 45 minutes—improving focus and reducing tension headaches.

**Day 6** – In her diary, Lena reports no new migraine attacks, but one tension‑type headache over the right frontal bone lasting **2 hours**, manageable with a 200 mg ibuprofen dose. She feels that the anxiety‑induced tension is less prominent.

**Day 7** – She attends a counseling session. The therapist introduces a weekly mood‑tracking sheet. She also receives resources on mindful breathing and progressive muscle relaxation.

---

### Day 8 – Follow‑Up Appointment

The PCP reviews her diary: One acute migraine with triptan resolved in 90 minutes, one tension headache, no new seizures. The topiramate serum level is satisfactory; the side effect of tingling persists but is mild. She feels that her migraines are well controlled.

**Decision** – Continue topiramate at 100 mg nightly. The PCP orders a blood work panel to check electrolytes, uric acid, and vitamin A levels in a few weeks.

---

### Aftermath – Disease Course Summary

- **Initial acute migraine**: started with aura, throbbing pain, nausea; resolved with sumatriptan by 90 minutes. - **Risk factor evaluation**: family history, hormonal status, caffeine use, stress, anxiety, sleep disorder. - **Acute management**: sumatriptan + diphenhydramine + hydration, sleep, avoidance of caffeine escalation. - **Preventive management**: topiramate, caffeine reduction, sleep hygiene, CBT for anxiety, stress‑management techniques. - **Outcome**: the acute episode resolved fully within 90 minutes; subsequent headache burden reduced; mood and sleep improved. The patient remains monitored with preventive therapy and supportive counseling.

---

### Key Clinical Decision Points

1. **Recognition of red flags** – No neurologic deficits, normal exam; no imaging needed. 2. **Choice of acute medication** – Sumatriptan chosen due to aura; rescue NSAID available. 3. **Transition to preventive therapy** – Topiramate initiated after one acute episode with risk factors. 4. **Addressing comorbidities** – Anxiety/depression addressed with CBT and therapy referral; sleep hygiene taught. 5. **Lifestyle modifications** – Caffeine reduction, stress coping, regular sleep schedule.

This narrative reflects a realistic, medically accurate journey through an acute migraine episode in a woman with multiple comorbidities and risk factors, culminating in successful resolution of the acute event and a proactive plan for long‑term management.
