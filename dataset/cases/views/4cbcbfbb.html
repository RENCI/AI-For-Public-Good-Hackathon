**A Sudden Storm in the Calm of a Busy Life**

**Background**

Ana was thirty‑five, a senior marketing strategist for a medium‑sized tech firm. Her work demanded long days, frequent client calls, and the perpetual churn of tight deadlines. Three years earlier, after an internal promotion, Ana’d begun to feel the weight of the new role. She had a history of generalized anxiety disorder (GAD) and major depressive disorder (MDD), both well‑controlled with an SSRI (escitalopram) and intermittent psychotherapy. Her mother’s history of panic attacks and her father’s chronic insomnia were known family risk factors. Ana was an avid coffee drinker—about three cups a day—often after lunch to keep her alert through the late shifts, and she drank a modest amount of wine on weekends. Sleep had slipped to an average of four to five hours a night. She also reported using nicotine and occasional marijuana to relax, especially after work.

**Day 1: The Trigger**

On Thursday, Ana’s team was slated to present a new product launch plan to the company’s executive board. She’d spent the last nine months refining the pitch, rehearsing data slides, and drafting a script. Throughout the week, anxiety crept up on her: a sense of looming failure, fear that the meeting would expose her inadequacy, and a nagging feeling that “something had to go wrong.” Sleep remained disrupted; she only slept for six hours after a long night of coffee and late‑night editing.

Two hours before the meeting, she was in the office kitchen, taking her third cup of black coffee. She reached for her tablet, scrolling through last night’s emails and the presentation timeline. As her heart rate climbed, she told herself she was just nervous. She could feel her breathing getting shallow.

**Day 1, 1:30 PM – The Panic Attack**

When the meeting started, the room’s fluorescent lights seemed to glow too bright. Ana’s breathing was rapid, chest tightness growing, her hands began trembling, and a wave of dizziness hit her. Fear surged: she imagined the board laughing at her, that her career would implode. Her vision blurred, a sense of impending doom rushed through her. She tried to speak, but a lump formed in her throat. The panic attack lasted roughly thirty minutes, peaking at 2:00 PM. After the meeting ended, she sat in the conference room, panting, her heart rate still high.

The panic attack did not resolve spontaneously; she realized she could not focus on the next task. A colleague noticed her distress and sent her a message asking if she was okay. Ana, unable to stay at her desk, went home for the night.

**Day 2 – Morning of Symptoms**

The next morning, 7:00 AM, she woke with a persistent feeling of shortness of breath and an unsettled heartbeat. She forced herself to go to work while the residual panic lingered, but it made her feel exhausted. She tried deep‑breathing exercises and drank a glass of water to steady herself. Yet, by 11:00 AM, she was again feeling a lightheadedness and a pounding in her chest.

Ana decided to call her primary care provider, Dr. Patel, to schedule an urgent appointment. She also called her therapist, Mara, to reschedule her weekly talk therapy session with a short break. The call to Dr. Patel was a critical decision point: Ana anticipated medical causes, such as a myocardial infarction, and wanted immediate evaluation.

**Day 2, 3:30 PM – Urgent Care**

After a thirty‑minute drive, Ana arrived at the urgent care center. The receptionist noted she had a “panic attack.” Dr. Patel took the history: the attack had begun after the meeting, the anxiety history, her regular SSRI dose, and her caffeine use. Physical exam revealed normal heart rate resting at 78 bpm, normal blood pressure, no abnormal lung sounds. Dr. Patel ordered an ECG to rule out cardiac arrhythmia and a set of routine labs to exclude thyroid dysfunction or electrolyte imbalance. All results would be available the next day.

During waiting, Dr. Patel explained the differential diagnosis: panic attacks can mimic myocardial infarction but are distinguished by absence of ischemic ECG changes. He suggested a trial of short‑acting benzodiazepine (lorazepam 0.5 mg orally) to help her recover rapidly from the residual symptoms. Ana accepted, and her breath normalized within 30 minutes.

**Day 2, 6:00 PM – Return Home**

Ana’s heart rate slowed, but she remained on edge. Dr. Patel sent her home medications (lorazepam) and advised that she must reduce caffeine intake, limit alcohol, and get at least eight hours of sleep. She was also offered immediate referral to psychiatry for acute anxiety and medication management.

The next morning, Dr. Patel’s lab results were normal: TSH, CBC, CMP within reference ranges; ECG showed normal sinus rhythm with no Q waves. He called her at 10:00 AM to share the results.

**Day 3 – Psychiatric Consultation**

Ana accepted a psychiatry appointment at the hospital’s outpatient clinic. She was greeted by Dr. Singh, a psychiatrist specialized in anxiety disorders. During the interview, Dr. Singh confirmed her GAD and MDD history and noted the recent stressor. She inquired about substance use: Ana admitted to having three drinks on the weekend and occasionally smoking marijuana late at night to unwind. She had a history of using nicotine daily.

Dr. Singh’s primary decision point was medication management. She increased Ana’s escitalopram dose from 10 mg to 20 mg daily, citing a sub‑optimal response to the prior dose and recent escalation in anxiety. She also prescribed propranolol 20 mg twice daily as a prophylactic against autonomic symptoms (palpitations, tremor). Dr. Singh scheduled weekly psychotherapy sessions and recommended a sleep hygiene plan: reducing caffeine to one cup post‑lunch, establishing a bedtime routine, and limiting TV before bed.

**Day 4 – Return to Work**

Ana returned to work on Thursday, feeling slightly better. She still felt a residual anxiety but could manage the tasks. She took her first dose of propranolol at 8 AM, then her standard escitalopram at noon. She also took her first dose of lorazepam on an as‑needed basis, only if she felt an anxiety flare before tasks. Her heart rate stayed within normal limits; she no longer had the chest tightness.

**Day 7 – Follow‑up with Dr. Patel**

Ana had a follow‑up appointment with Dr. Patel on Monday. She reported feeling stable; the panic attacks had subsided, and her anxiety was mild. She still had occasional worries about work and had been sleeping 7–8 hours nightly. Dr. Patel confirmed her labs remained normal. The decision point was whether to taper the benzodiazepine. Dr. Patel discontinued lorazepam, explaining that with her controlled SSRI and propranolol, she would no longer need the anxiolytic. He advised her to report any new symptoms immediately.

**Day 14 – Check‑in with Dr. Singh**

Two weeks later, Ana returned to psychiatry. Her anxiety had decreased to a mild level; she had not experienced a panic attack in 14 days. Depression symptoms (sleep, appetite, anhedonia) improved. Dr. Singh maintained her escitalopram at 20 mg and propranolol 20 mg twice daily. She also scheduled a group CBT program focusing on relapse prevention.

**Day 28 – Final Assessment**

A month after the initial crisis, Ana attended a routine psychiatry follow‑up. She was in good spirits, the anxiety had resolved, and depressive symptoms were largely gone. Sleep was regular, caffeine intake reduced, and alcohol consumption cut to one day per week. Dr. Singh advised her to maintain a regular therapy schedule and to continue the propranolol for at least six months, after which it could be tapered.

**Conclusion**

Ana’s acute panic attack was a watershed event precipitated by a high‑stress presentation, chronic caffeine use, sleep deprivation, and personal substance use patterns. Through a combination of rapid evaluation in urgent care, judicious pharmacotherapy (benzodiazepines, SSRIs, beta‑blockers), adjustment of lifestyle factors, and targeted psychotherapy, she achieved a full resolution of symptoms within a month. The narrative underscores the importance of addressing comorbidities, risk factors, and the interplay of biological, psychological, and social elements in the acute management of panic disorders.
