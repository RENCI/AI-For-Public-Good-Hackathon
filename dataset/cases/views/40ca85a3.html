**A 55‑Year‑Old Man in a Spiralling Spiral of Disease**

On a rainy Thursday morning, Thomas—55, a mechanic, and a longtime heavy drinker—rushed into the emergency department clutching his belly, his stomach a tight, aching knot that seemed to have been tightening for the previous three days. He’d had a rough week at work, a bout of cold, and, as always, a bottle of cheap whiskey waiting at home. He told the triage nurse that the pain had started as a dull, vague gnawing in the middle of his belly, then turned into a sharp, burning agony that sent waves radiating to his back. He could barely lie flat in bed, and the discomfort worsened with every breath.

Thomas had a habit of masking his problems. He denied recent binge drinking, said the beer at his workplace was “just a little," and tried to dismiss the pain as heartburn or indigestion—classic red herrings for someone of his age and risk profile.

### Day 0 – ED Presentation

The first nurse, a seasoned worker who’d seen her share of gallstone crises and alcoholism, let the clinical team know that Thomas had a history of type‑2 diabetes managed with metformin and intermittent insulin, obesity (BMI 33), a chronic kidney disease of stage 3, a prior cholecystectomy that had failed to resolve his gallstone problems, and a stubborn hypertriglyceridemia that hovered around 400 mg/dL in routine lab work. Most concerningly, Thomas had been on long‑term azathioprine and prednisone for “autoimmune pancreatitis” that the gastroenterologist had diagnosed a year ago based on a single elevated IgG4 and a pancreas biopsy showing lymphocytic infiltration. He had been taking prednisone 15 mg daily and azathioprine 150 mg daily, both of which had been tapered but not stopped.

In the ED, the fast‑track doctor drew blood. The lipase was only mildly elevated at 260 U/L (normal <160), but the glucose was a high 280 mg/dL, and potassium was 3.0 mEq/L. His blood urea nitrogen (BUN) was 32 mg/dL and creatinine 1.8 mg/dL, reflecting his CKD. He was afebrile, and his blood pressure was a modest 110/65. The physical exam was revealing: epigastric tenderness, but no guarding or rebound; the abdomen was soft to deep palpation, and his stools were unchanged. An initial abdominal ultrasound showed gallbladder sludge and a dilated common bile duct (8 mm). There were no obvious stones in the ducts; the pancreas was not conspicuously enlarged, and no peripancreatic fluid was seen.

The attending, a gastroenterologist with a keen eye for atypical presentations, decided the best course was conservative management in a medical ward: aggressive IV fluids (0.9% normal saline, 3 L in first 24 h), electrolyte correction, insulin drip to manage the hyperglycemia, and antiemetics. Prednisone was held for the time being because of the elevated infection risk; azathioprine was also paused pending the full workup.

### Day 1 – The “Mild Episode” Misleading

The night after admission, the clinical team did a CT scan of the abdomen with contrast as a routine confirmatory test. It showed a slightly swollen pancreas with peripancreatic fat stranding—a textbook grade Ⅲ acute pancreatitis per revised Atlanta classification—but the pancreas did not appear necrotic, and there was no evidence of ductal obstruction beyond the mild dilation of the CBD. The radiologist noted no pseudocysts. The CT was interpreted as an uncomplicated, mild bout.

Thomas’s pain, which had initially seemed “worse than usual,” actually began to improve. His serum amylase was 190 U/L. He tolerated a clear liquid diet and his insulin requirements dropped modestly. He fell asleep; the nurse woke him at 8 a.m. to find him sleeping soundly. The team had started him on a low‑carbohydrate diet, in line with his diabetes, and monitored his electrolytes. No antibiotics were given because the infection risk was low. The plan was to observe: if the pain recurred or worsened, to consider ERCP for stone removal.

### Day 2 – The First Clinical Decision Point

On the second morning, Thomas’s pain returned—not the dull ache of the previous day but a sharp, tearing pain that seemed to cut into his abdomen with each breath. His vital signs shifted: heart rate 110 bpm, mild fever of 100.6°F, blood pressure 95/60 dropping. His BUN rose to 38 mg/dL, creatinine 2.0 mg/dL. Labs showed new, dramatic elevation in lipase to 1,260 U/L and mild transaminitis (AST 78 U/L, ALT 112 U/L). His triglyceride levels, measured after an overnight fast, were a startling 1,020 mg/dL.

At this juncture, his care team convened. The decision was made to:

- **Transfer to the ICU** because of unstable vitals and escalating pain. - **Initiate an aggressive fluid resuscitation** with lactated Ringer's at 150 mL/h, adjusted as per central venous pressure readings. - **Start broad‑spectrum antibiotics** (piperacillin‑tazobactam) given the suspicion of a septic source and the immunosuppressed state from steroids and azathioprine. - **Hold all immunosuppressive medication** and reintroduce prednisone at a low dose (5 mg) only if clear evidence ruled out infection. - **Order a repeat CT** after 24 h to assess for necrosis.

The team also considered the gallstone disease. With imaging showing a dilated CBD, though no stone visible, there was a strong possibility of a transient stone that had passed or a microlith. In the absence of visible stones, the plan was to proceed with an ERCP in 36 h, assuming the patient's condition stabilized.

### Day 3 – An Unexpected Twist

While in the ICU, Thomas’s condition deteriorated further. His pain spiked to a maximum pain score of 10/10. He became hypotensive, requiring dopamine infusion to maintain MAP >65 mmHg. Laboratory panels revealed leukocytosis (WBC 18,000), elevated C‑reactive protein (CRP 130 mg/L), and rising lactate (3.2 mmol/L). The repeat CT scan, performed emergently, painted a new picture: the pancreas was markedly enlarged, with extensive areas of hypodensity consistent with necrosis in both the head and body. A sizeable peripancreatic fluid collection measured 5 × 4 cm near the tail. There were no bile duct stones; the ducts remained dilated, but the bile duct was obstructed at the ampulla by a large “impaction” of sludge that could not be seen on CT but was suspected due to the degree of dilation.

The gastroenterology team now faced a triad of challenges:

1. **Necrotizing pancreatitis with evolving infected necrosis.** 2. **Severe hypertriglyceridemia contributing to ongoing pancreatic inflammation.** 3. **Biliary obstruction from sludge in the ampulla.**

The decision was: *perform urgent ERCP with sphincterotomy and common bile duct stent placement*. Meanwhile, *initiate insulin therapy to aggressively lower triglycerides* (target <500 mg/dL). The medical team considered plasmapheresis but decided that insulin‑fructose‑glycine therapy would be sufficient initially, given the patient’s renal insufficiency (plasmapheresis may cause volume shifts and worsen renal function).

### Day 4 – Interventional Success? A Setback

The ERCP was technically successful. A 10 Fr plastic stent was placed in the common bile duct, and the ampulla was gently dilated; no stone was retrieved, but sludge was aspirated, and contrast filled the ducts completely, relieving the obstruction noted previously. Post‑procedure, Thomas’s abdominal pain decreased modestly. His bilirubin fell from 2.5 mg/dL to 1.2 mg/dL. However, his renal function worsened rapidly: creatinine spiked to 2.6 mg/dL, BUN climbed to 48 mg/dL. The renal consult noted that his fluid status was precarious—over‑resuscitation had led to interstitial edema, but a high vasopressor requirement had limited kidney perfusion.

Meanwhile, the infectious workup grew culture positivity: *Enterococcus faecium* isolated from a percutaneous catheter aspiration of the fluid collection. The antibiotics were switched to vancomycin, with a plan for percutaneous drainage after the septic source was controlled.

### Day 5 – The First Organ Failure

Within the next 24 h, Thomas developed early acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS). His oxygen saturations dropped from 96% on 3 L/min to 84% on 10 L/min. A chest X‑ray revealed bilateral alveolar infiltrates. The ICU team started a lung‑protective ventilation strategy (tidal volume 6 mL/kg predicted body weight, plateau pressure <30 cm H₂O). They also placed a central venous catheter to administer albumin for oncotic pressure management and began prone positioning after several hours of desaturation. His blood cultures again grew *Enterococcus*, suggesting bacteremia from the abdominal infection.

His diabetes became a nightmare. Glucose spikes pushed above 350 mg/dL despite an insulin infusion of 4 U/h, with intermittent fluctuations to 180 mg/dL. The Endocrinology Team decided to increase insulin infusion to 8 U/h, titrate based on glucose every hour, and give insulin glargine at night. However, his insulin pumps were problematic due to his chronic kidney disease, which interfered with drug clearance.

### Day 6 – Recurrent Hemodynamic Instability

Despite continuous pressor support, Thomas's blood pressure remained shaky, dipping to 85/45 even on norepinephrine 0.15 µg/kg/min. His lactate spiked to 5.8 mmol/L, indicating septic shock. The surgical team performed an urgent percutaneous drainage of the peripancreatic collection under image guidance, placing two 14 Fr pigtail drains. The drain output was thick, foul‑smelling, and a few hours later, a repeat culture again grew *Enterococcus*.

The interventional radiology note explained that the necrotic tissue and infected fluid were preventing the patient’s immune system from clearing the infection effectively. The drains improved local circulation but did not prevent the persistent systemic inflammatory response.

### Day 7 – A Tipping Point

By day 7, Thomas was in a deepening multiorgan failure syndrome. The kidneys were failing: creatinine plateaued at 3.2 mg/dL, and the patient was anuric. The team had to decide on renal replacement therapy. Because his hemodynamics were unstable, continuous venovenous hemofiltration (CVVH) was chosen. Meanwhile, the infection continued to linger; the blood cultures remained positive for *Enterococcus* on days 7 and 8. The infection control team recommended adding daptomycin in conjunction with vancomycin, hoping to achieve synergy.

The decision also involved whether to continue or restart steroids for the autoimmune pancreatitis. The endocrinologist, in consultation with the rheumatology team that had been supervising his azathioprine, argued that withholding steroids would reduce his risk of further immunosuppression and help fight the infection. Azathioprine was permanently discontinued.

The patient’s oxygenation remained marginal: in the ICU, the ventilator settings were adjusted to 10 cm H₂O plateau pressures, with FiO₂ at 0.6 and PEEP at 12 cm H₂O. The oxygenation index remained poor; the decision to ventilate with a lung‑protective strategy was reinforced.

### Day 8 – Reversible? Not Yet

In the early morning, a rapid CT with contrast was done again to evaluate the necrotic collection for possible transition into an infected walled‑off necrosis. The imaging showed no evidence of a new fluid accumulation, but the necrotic tissue remained as a hazy area in the pancreatic tail. The interventional radiology team decided to avoid a second drainage procedure due to the complexity and the risk of bleeding in the context of heparin used for CVVH.

Blood work revealed worsening transaminases: AST 540 U/L, ALT 620 U/L. The liver was suffering an ischemic insult from the sepsis‑induced hypotension, compounded by the high doses of antibiotics. They had to stop piperacillin‑tazobactam and switch to meropenem to cover for possible resistant organisms.

His bilirubin had trended down to 0.9 mg/dL, indicating that the biliary obstruction had been successfully relieved. However, the pancreatic head necrosis had not resolved.

### Day 9 – A Delicate Balance

The nephrology team adjusted the dialysis prescription, adding a bicarbonate buffer to correct metabolic acidosis. Thomas’s acid–base status improved, but the urea nitrogen remained elevated. The endocrine team noted that the insulin requirement had plateaued at 10 U/h, but the glucose range remained 250–310 mg/dL.

The ICU nurse noted that Thomas's skin was becoming mottled; mottling score was 3/4—classic sign of impaired perfusion. They initiated an early goal‑directed therapy for sepsis with a goal of lactate clearance to <2.0 mmol/L within 6 h. After 12 h, lactate fell to 3.2 mmol/L, but not below 2.0 mmol/L. His blood pressure remained dependent on norepinephrine 0.25 µg/kg/min.

The decision was now, after an interdisciplinary round, to consider the possibility of multi‑organ failure that might become irreversible. They had to weigh the risk of continuing aggressive support versus palliative care. Thomas's wife called in, her face tight with worry, and the team arranged a family meeting with the attending, the critical care nurse, the palliative care specialist, and the cardiology team. The family decided to continue life support for now, hoping that the drainage would eventually control the infection.

### Day 10 – Final Slide

At the beginning of the 10th day, the patient’s condition took a dramatic turn. His oxygenation fell precipitously; his PaO₂ dropped from 68 mmHg on FiO₂ 0.6 to 45 mmHg, despite increased PEEP to 16 cm H₂O. His arterial blood gas revealed a pH 7.25, PaCO₂ 52 mmHg, lactate 6.5 mmol/L, and a metabolic acidosis with a base deficit of 10. The CVVH therapy was switched to predialysis, and the patient developed a sudden drop in blood pressure to 70/40 despite maximum vasopressor support (norepinephrine 0.35 µg/kg/min, vasopressin 0.03 U/min).

The bedside team realized that the diffuse alveolar damage had progressed to a severe form of ARDS. The lung-protective strategy was further intensified, but the oxygenation index stayed above 200. A rapid assessment revealed that the patient's immune response had collapsed, and, combined with the persistent infected necrosis and a failing kidney, the cascade of organ failure was unstoppable.

Within 24 h, there was no significant change in vital signs, and the family, after a stern discussion, opted for transition to comfort care. The ICU team switched to palliative sedation, the ventilator was weaned, and life‑sustaining treatments were withdrawn. Thomas’s heart rate decreased, his pupils became fixed and dilated, his breathing deepened and uneven, and within hours, his pulse stopped. He was pronounced deceased at 08:30 h.

### Reflection

Thomas’s journey illustrates how the overlap of alcohol use disorder, gallstone disease, severe hypertriglyceridemia, chronic kidney disease, diabetes, obesity, smoking, and autoimmune pancreatitis—coupled with long‑term immunosuppression by azathioprine and steroids—can produce a deceptively subtle onset of acute pancreatitis. The initial mild presentation confounded the team, as the classical signs of severe disease were muted by immunosuppression and co‑existing conditions. By the time radiographic evidence of necrosis emerged, septic shock, ARDS, and renal failure had already been set in motion. Aggressive supportive care, interventional drainage, and broad antibiotic coverage could not arrest the relentless escalation of multiorgan failure, and the patient ultimately succumbed. This narrative underscores the importance of early recognition and high index of suspicion in patients with multiple overlapping risk factors for acute pancreatitis.
