On a brisk March afternoon, John‑P. B., a 65‑year‑old mechanic from the south‑side of the city, sat in a humming exam room at his family physician’s office. He had been coughing for about six weeks, the cough now constant and dry, but it had evolved into a frequent, sputum‑laden cough that left him short of breath after a walk down the street. “I’ve got a cold that won’t go away,” he said, rubbing a thin film of mucus off his throat. The sputum was gray, occasionally streaked with a darker hue. His wife mentioned that he’d lost about 10 pounds over the past month and that their bedroom light was always dimmed because he was tired.

He had a long history of smoking—he had smoked 2 packs a day from the age of 20 until recently, when he cut back to one pack for the last year. He also had a decade of exposure to asbestos while working at a shipyard and had spent years in dusty sawmill work, exposing him to silica particles. He had regularly ridden in diesel‑fuel trucks, and there had been occasional concerns of radon levels in his two‑story home in the same area, although he had never tested the air. His baseline pulmonary status was well‑known to the office: chronic obstructive pulmonary disease with a moderate emphysematous component on a CT scan three years prior, chronic bronchitis flare‑ups every summer, and a diagnosis of pulmonary hypertension two years back, managed on a low‑dose calcium channel blocker and supplemental oxygen at 3 L/min.

---

### First encounter (March 2)

During the visit, John’s vital signs were telling: a resting heart rate of 98 beats/min, oxygen saturation of 94 % on 3 L/min O₂, and a wheeze at the right lung base on auscultation. The primary physician—Dr. Martinez—asked a series of questions to differentiate an infectious exacerbation from a more sinister cause. “Any fever?” John shook his head. “Any chest pain?” He declined. “Do you see blood in your sputum?” John’s eyes widened—“A little bit, a streak, especially after coughing. It’s not a lot, but it’s there.” Dr. Martinez noted this hemoptysis, which prompted the ordering of a chest X‑ray.

In the radiology suite, the AP and lateral films revealed a subtle opacity in the right mid‑lung zone, partially obscuring the right mainstem bronchus. There was also a mild enlargement of the right heart border, consistent with his known pulmonary hypertension. The radiologist’s preliminary report mentioned “possible central mass” but suggested a CT thorax for better characterization.

---

### Pulmonology referral (March 9)

Two days later, John visited his PCP once more after the patient experienced a new episode of coughing, producing copious yellow sputum. Dr. Martinez felt that a more definitive workup was warranted. He referred John to Dr. Lee, a pulmonologist in the same medical group, noting the suspicion of a central mass at the tracheobronchial junction.

At Dr. Lee’s office, John recounted the past week’s worsening dyspnea, especially when climbing the stairs—he now needed a cane. The pulmonologist reviewed John’s medication list: albuterol, ipratropium, budesonide–formoterol, low‑dose steroids for exacerbations, and the low‑dose calcium channel blocker for pulmonary hypertension. Dr. Lee ordered a high‑resolution CT scan of the chest with contrast, along with a bronchoscopy scheduled within a week.

Before the procedure, Dr. Lee had a brief discussion with John about the possibility of advanced lung disease given the occupational exposures and smoking history, and about the potential need for a multidisciplinary management plan that might involve oncology, thoracic surgery, and palliative care. John agreed to a “full‑body” evaluation when possible.

---

### CT & bronchoscopy (March 16)

The CT scan returned a bright, 3.2 × 2.8 cm enhancing mass within the right mainstem bronchus, occluding 70 % of the lumen, with extension into the peribronchial fat and mild mediastinal lymph node enlargement. The emphysematous bullae were noted, and there was no evidence of pleural effusion or distant metastasis at that time. Dr. Lee called John into the office after the imaging results. He relayed that the mass was likely malignant and ordered a flexible bronchoscopy to obtain tissue samples.

John was prepped for bronchoscopy on March 17, after a pre‑op evaluation including a repeat blood count, renal panel, and discussion of anesthesia risks. He was admitted for the day of the procedure, on 2 L/min of oxygen, with the knowledge that the risk of peri‑procedural airway compromise was non‑negligible given the obstruction.

During the bronchoscopy, the visualized right mainstem bronchus was narrowed and erythematous, with friable plaques. A biopsy taken from the most obstructing part revealed dysplastic squamous epithelium with areas of invasive carcinoma—carcinoma NOS by histopathology, later clarified as squamous cell carcinoma with a high proliferation index on Ki‑67 staining.

---

### Hospital admission (March 18‑24)

After the biopsy, John’s wheezing worsened; his oxygen saturation dropped to 88 % at rest. He developed sputum‑producing cough and a mild fever. Dr. Lee recognized that his COPD exacerbation, coupled with bronchial obstruction from the tumor, necessitated inpatient care.

He was transferred to the hospital for a 7‑day course of IV methylprednisolone, nebulized albuterol–ipratropium, and a course of oral amoxicillin‑clavulanate pending culture results from the sputum. Oxygen flow was increased to 4 L/min via Venturi mask. A chest CT was repeated in the hospital, revealing slight pleural thickening but no new effusion.

John’s clinical course during that week was unstable; his sputum increased, and he had three brief episodes of coughing up streaks of blood. He reported increased fatigue and shortness of breath on lying flat. His wife noted that John was using a recliner more often to avoid exertion. Despite aggressive pulmonary therapy, his oxygen saturation fluctuated between 85–92 %.

On March 24, after three days of relative stabilization, Dr. Lee scheduled a multidisciplinary tumor board meeting to discuss management options. The board consisted of an oncologist, thoracic surgeon, pulmonologist, and a palliative care specialist. The discussion centered on John’s poor pulmonary reserve, the large central tumor’s location, and the associated pulmonary hypertension. The consensus was that curative surgery was high risk and likely to result in peri‑operative death. Instead, the board recommended a short course of palliative chemotherapy (cisplatin plus etoposide) alongside radiotherapy to the thorax, with a goal of symptom control rather than long‑term disease resolution.

---

### Chemotherapy initiation (April 1)

On April 1, John was admitted to the oncology unit for a start‑up regimen. Prior to therapy, his cardiopulmonary evaluation included a trans‑esophageal echocardiogram that showed a mean pulmonary artery systolic pressure of 70 mmHg, indicating severe pulmonary hypertension. With the assistance of the cardiothoracic team, John was placed on a short-acting vasodilator infusion (IV inhaled iloprost) to blunt the pulmonary vascular response during chemo infusion.

During his initial chemotherapy infusion, John complained of chest pressure and lightheadedness. The infusion was halted temporarily; the infusion pump was restarted a few minutes later, and John's blood pressure remained stable at a high 120/80 mmHg. His oxygen saturation fell to 86 % during the infusion, and he was placed on a high‑flow nasal cannula temporarily.

Post‑chemotherapy, John developed mild mucositis and a persistent low-grade fever (38.0°C). He was treated with a 7‑day course of oral doxycycline and acetaminophen. His chest X‑ray after the first cycle showed no significant changes but revealed a moderate right pleural effusion, likely related to malignant infiltration and inflammatory response.

---

### Worsening clinical course (April 15)

Two weeks after the initial chemotherapy, John’s symptom burden had increased markedly. His sputum was now thick and purplish, and he reported a new onset of orthopnea; he could no longer sleep upright without a pillow. Physical examination revealed pitting edema in both lower extremities up to the knees, jugular venous distension, and a new holosystolic murmur at the left sternal border. A portable chest X‑ray illustrated an expanded right pleural effusion and a slightly more opacified right lung field, suggesting malignant progression.

A bedside thoracentesis removed 800 mL of fluid that was hemorrhagic and cytology-positive for malignant epithelial cells. The pleural fluid cultures were negative. John’s pulmonary hypertension worsened further; his mean pulmonary artery pressure rose to 85 mmHg. On Dr. Lee’s instructions, the team initiated a diuretic regimen (furosemide 40 mg BID) to manage right‑heart failure signs.

Despite aggressive diuresis, John’s edema persisted, and his weight had increased by five kilograms over a month, mainly due to fluid retention. His quality of life deteriorated rapidly: he became unable to perform chores, required assistance to ambulate even for a few steps, and reported worsening pain in his chest wall, localized to the right hemithorax. John’s family became concerned over his frequent nighttime awakenings and inability to maintain adequate sleep.

---

### Decision for palliative care (April 28)

At the end of April, Dr. Lee initiated a formal discussion with John and his wife regarding the goals of care. John expressed a desire to be comfortable and to avoid invasive procedures that would compromise his breathing or further diminish his autonomy. The hospital’s palliative care team was consulted. Their assessment concluded that continued chemotherapy and radiotherapy would likely add significant toxicities—especially in a patient with severe pulmonary hypertension—and fail to improve quality of life or survival.

The palliative care team recommended comfort measures, including low‑dose opioid analgesia for chest pain, non‑invasive ventilation support for hypoxia, and careful fluid management. They also discussed the possibility of hospice enrollment if home care resources were insufficient. John and his wife agreed to discontinue chemotherapy and accept a hospice referral.

He was placed on a low‑dose oral morphine regime for pain, with the caveat that his oxygen needs were managed with a 2‑L nasal cannula to avoid hypoxia. An oxygen tank was brought into the bedroom, and a breathing fan set to low speed was arranged at the bedside. John’s cough became less productive; however, he continued to experience intermittent wheezing and bronchial hyperreactivity. His pulmonary medications were streamlined to include only albuterol/ipratropium nebulization and a single dose of oral methylprednisolone at bedtime to blunt nighttime airway inflammation.

---

### Final hospitalization and hospice transition (May 12–15)

The following month, John’s wife noticed that his breathing had become irregular. The wife called the emergency department at 10 am on May 12, and John was brought to the ED. His vitals were: oxygen saturation 85 % on 3 L/min, heart rate 110 bpm, blood pressure 120/85 mmHg. On examination, he was in mild distress, but no obvious signs of infection existed. Lab work was notable for elevated BUN/creatinine, mild leukocytosis, and a mild anemia. Radiology showed a still-pleural effusion, now more significant at 1.2 L by CT volumetry, along with an increased right mainstem bronchial narrowing from the mass.

Given his limited respiratory reserve and the expectation that oxygenation would deteriorate rapidly, the team decided to transition John to hospice care. He was discharged from the ED on May 14 after a short observation stay, with a hospice home-care team scheduled for a home visit the same day.

John received comfort care at home, with his wife providing support with feeding and personal care. He was occasionally visited by a hospice nurse who administered subcutaneous morphine as needed. Oxygen was supplied via portable concentrator, and a small fan provided gentle airflow over his face. No further interventions were pursued, and John passed away peacefully on May 18 surrounded by his family.

---

### Reflection

John’s cancer course was a rapid, aggressive progression of a central lung carcinoma that compounded pre‑existing chronic obstructive pulmonary disease, pulmonary hypertension, and comorbidities from decades of occupational exposures. The disease’s anatomical location led to significant airway obstruction, prompting a cascade of respiratory failures, exacerbations, and ultimately the inability to tolerate curative interventions. The narrative illustrates the complexity inherent in managing a patient with multiple overlapping risk factors, and the difficulty clinicians face when balancing the promise of aggressive oncologic therapy against the reality of diminishing quality of life. The medical team’s eventual decision to prioritize comfort over curative treatment exemplifies patient‑centered care that recognizes the natural limits of intervention in a severely ill individual.
