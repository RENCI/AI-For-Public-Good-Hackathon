**Mr. Thomas Harding** was a 98‑year‑old retired machinist who lived alone in a modest two‑story home. He had a lifetime of heavy tobacco use—he smoked over a pack a day for 45 years—and a history of occupational dust exposure in a dusty foundry, plus intermittent exposure to radon and asbestos in a rental apartment in the 1970s. His medical record carried a dense list of chronic illnesses: irreversible airflow limitation due to COPD and chronic bronchitis, a prior bout of pulmonary tuberculosis at age 54 that had left residual calcified lesions and a dilated right lower lobe bronchi, bronchiectasis that produced a persistent, low‑grade sputum production, essential hypertension, type‑2 diabetes mellitus poorly controlled on oral agents, stable coronary artery disease with a history of percutaneous coronary intervention, stage 3 chronic kidney disease, osteoporosis, and the inevitable geriatric frailty that had made his gait increasingly cautious.

---

### 1. First Red Flag – The Persistent Cough (Week 0–4)

In early March, Mr. Harding began to notice a dry cough that no longer seemed to be an old habit. He reported the cough as “sudden and persistent” and found it more disturbing at night, often waking in the middle of the night. For his own part, he blamed it on a new cold; for his primary care physician, it was deemed an exacerbation of his chronic bronchitis. The patient was advised to increase use of his albuterol inhaler and to try an inhaled steroid. A chest X‑ray sent to the urgent‑care clinic showed a patch of increased opacity in the right lower lobe but was interpreted as “likely post‑influenza bronchitis” given the context of his known bronchiectasis.

During this period, the cough was accompanied by a mild wheeze, but he insisted his breathing felt “normal enough.” He began to lose 1–2 pounds, which he shrugged off as part of normal aging. His routine bloodwork, done in the week of his first visit, showed a mild normocytic anemia (hemoglobin 11.0 g/dL), modestly elevated ESR, and a fasting glucose of 138 mg/dL. No abnormality in his urinalysis.

### 2. The First Imaging – The Turning Point (Week 5–8)

In a follow‑up visit at the community clinic, the pulmonologist reviewed the chest radiograph again, now noting a more defined mass‑like appearance in the mid–right lower zone with an ill‑defined peripheral margin and associated calcifications reminiscent of his old TB lesions. A thoracic CT scan was ordered. He went to the urban hospital for the scan, arriving in a wheelchair with a mild palliative‑grade frailty.

The CT scan revealed a 5.5‑cm spiculated lesion in the lower lobe of the right lung, with evidence of regional hilar and mediastinal lymph node enlargement. A PET‑CT scan demonstrated hypermetabolic activity within the mass and scattered uptake in the left adrenal gland and right hepatic lobe, raising immediate concern for metastatic disease. A CT‑guided core needle biopsy of the lung mass confirmed an anaplastic squamous cell carcinoma, with immunohistochemistry positive for p63 and cytokeratin 5/6. Molecular testing for EGFR, ALK, and KRAS was negative.

On the same day, a pulmonologist convened a multidisciplinary discussion via teleconference—including cardiology, anesthesiology, oncology, and palliative care consult—about the implications of a stage IV carcinoma in a 98‑year‑old with significant comorbidities. After a lengthy family call in which the daughter in Florida was on the line with tears, it was decided that curative intent therapy would be futile. The goal was to preserve comfort and dignity.

The oncology team advised against systemic chemotherapy, citing the patient’s poor renal function, frailty, and poor performance status (Karnofsky Score 30). The patient was advised that short‑course radiotherapy to the primary site (two fractions of 2000 cGy) might palliate dyspnea related to mass effect but would not prolong survival. Mr. Harding consented, opting for the less invasive option.

### 3. Early Palliative Measures (Month 2)

Mr. Harding’s first radiation session was administered on the 12th of April. He reported mild fatigue and a sense of relief during the subsequent days as the cough subsided. However, a week later, he developed a productive cough with purulent sputum and became tachypneic, reaching 28 breaths per minute and a PaCO₂ of 58 mmHg on a simple pulse oximetry. At this point, his COPD was in apparent exacerbation triggered by the developing pneumonia.

He was sent to the hospital. A low‑dose CT scan revealed a new left lower lobe infiltrate, likely secondary bacterial pneumonia. Empiric antibiotics were started with ceftriaxone and azithromycin. The chest tube was not inserted as his pleural effusion was minimal. He was discharged on oral prednisone 20 mg daily for one week, a change from his usual inhaled corticosteroid, and advised to stay in the hospital for a repeat CT or to seek emergency care if breathing worsened.

During that month, he had a sudden, mild blood‑tinged sputum episode. He denied hemoptysis, but the pulmonologist felt this could herald bleeding from the tumor. A bronchoscopy schedule was moved to the following month.

### 4. Complications – The First Episode of Hemoptysis (Month 3)

In early May, while in his bedroom, Mr. Harding coughed up a small amount of dark‑red sputum. He went to urgent care, where an emergent chest X‑ray showed a slight hyperdensity adjacent to the right lower lung lesion. Hemoptysis was brief, with no significant blood loss. On the day of the visit, he had a blood pressure of 170/92 and an elevated heart rate of 102 bpm. The pulmonologist decided to proceed with a diagnostic bronchoscopy in the hospital’s endoscopic unit to determine the source.

Bronchoscopy found an ulcerated mass at the base of the lobar bronchus of the right lower lobe. There was active bleeding when the scope passed the lesion, but a topical epinephrine spray and cautery achieved hemostasis. The biopsy reaffirmed squamous cell carcinoma. The tumor had a high proliferative index (Ki‑67 35%). Importantly, at this juncture, Mr. Harding’s creatinine had risen to 1.6 mg/dL, and he was now stage 3b chronic kidney disease, limiting the use of nephrotoxic agents.

Given the patient’s frailty, the oncology team opted not to pursue any systemic therapy beyond symptomatic management. The hospitalist increased the prednisone to 40 mg daily, a plan to keep with a taper, expecting to counter tumor‑related inflammation. The patient, however, developed a mild cough again around day 5, now producing thick sputum with occasional blood streaks, prompting the addition of inhaled ipratropium to his regimen.

### 5. A Rapid Deterioration – The Acute Exacerbation (Month 4)

In mid‑June, Mr. Harding returned to the hospital on a Thursday morning with worsening shortness of breath, confusion, and a sudden drop in oxygen saturation to 84% on room air. He was in his bedroom, pacing anxiously, holding a blanket tighter around his chest. He was drowsy but not obtunded, and his pupils were equal. The nurse called for an emergency physician to examine him. His vitals were: BP 140/88 mmHg, HR 112 bpm, RR 30/min, Sat 86% on 3 L/min nasal cannula, T 99.5°F.

The ED team performed a rapid bedside chest X‑ray that highlighted a new left pleural effusion, a possible left diaphragmatic elevation and the right lower lobe mass now appearing more rounded. The left pleural fluid appeared serosanguineous on aspiration, and a thoracentesis was performed. Fluid analysis showed 45 cells/µL, mostly neutrophils, exudative protein level 3.8 g/dL, LDH 900 IU/L, negative Gram stain, and cultures pending. No malignant cells were seen on cytology, although the cytology specimen was often inadequate from pleural fluid in elderly patients.

Simultaneously, the ED received a call from the neurology team: his daughter reported that over the past 12 hours he had been increasingly disoriented and confused, unable to answer simple questions, and now could not sit upright without assistance. A bedside head CT scan was performed without contrast, showing no acute intracranial hemorrhage but mild cortical sulci effacement and no mass effect. The neurology consulted for a possible metabolic encephalopathy. The labs showed a serum sodium of 128, a serum glucose of 110 mg/dL, BUN 28 mg/dL, creatinine 1.7 mg/dL.

The ICU team was consulted on Thursday night. With the patient’s advanced age, significant comorbidities, and the rapidly progressing respiratory compromise, they considered the utility of intubation and mechanical ventilation uncertain. In a shared decision‑making session with the patient’s daughter (present in the ICU), the family expressed desire to limit the intensity of interventions to avoid further suffering. They declined intubation and heavy sedation, preferring to stay on non‑invasive oxygen and comfort measures. The ICU team instituted high‑flow nasal cannula at 50% FiO₂ and offered a low‑dose opioid infusion for dyspnea control. Palliative care was consulted the next morning.

### 6. The Final Weeks – Worsening Illness and Transition to Hospice (Months 5–6)

In July, Mr. Harding’s condition continued to falter. He had persistent tachypnea, an oxygen requirement climbing to 4 L/min to maintain saturation above 88%. His dyspnea and cough progressed to a constant, deep‑throated moan. The pain score rose to 7/10 for chest pain that he denied earlier; he became increasingly restless. His appetite had dwindled further; he refused to swallow. Blood tests indicated a rising white count of 15,000/µL, mild hyponatremia 125, and a rising creatinine 2.0 mg/dL.

On the second week of July, while sleeping in his bedroom, Mr. Harding suddenly became unresponsive. His daughter called 911, and the ambulance arrived within minutes. EMS found him in an altered mental state, no pulse detected after 9 minutes of basic CPR. Emergency services declared a presumed cardiac arrest due to underlying metabolic derangements. No return of spontaneous circulation.

The family was notified in the hospital. After discussion, the daughter chose to place the patient on hospice care with comfort measures only; pain was managed with morphine infusion, and the focus shifted to providing a dignified end. Within days, Mr. Harding had passed peacefully during the night, surrounded by his daughter and pet cat, the family and hospice nurses providing reassurance, and the care team documenting the patient’s wishes.

---

**Reflection**

Mr. Thomas Harding’s disease course illustrates the relentless trajectory of Stage IV squamous cell carcinoma of the lung in the very elderly with extensive comorbidities. The initial cough, misinterpreted as a COPD exacerbation, eventually revealed the malignant mass on imaging that could not be resected or treated aggressively. His frailty, chronic respiratory disease, and the interplay of systemic inflammation, hypercapnia, and infection propelled his condition to a point where aggressive life‑prolonging measures were not only unlikely to succeed but might have caused undue suffering. The narrative underscores the importance of early palliative intervention, shared decision‑making, and a compassionate focus on quality of life for patients at the end of life.
