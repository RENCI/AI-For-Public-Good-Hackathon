**Maya’s Unsettling Rhythm – A Narrative of an Acute Endometriosis Pain Crisis**

Maya’s life had always felt out of sync. She began menstruating at thirteen, and for as long as she could remember, every month was a battle against a tightness that she could feel creeping low in the abdomen. By twenty-five, she was a seasoned patient of gynecology and pain management, her calendar marked by consultations, hormone prescriptions, and the occasional MRI. Still, she had never let go of the small, nagging hope that infertility might be cured by some surgical intervention—until a routine laparoscopy two years ago revealed nothing but a cluster of fibroids and a thickened uterine wall suggestive of adenomyosis. Endometriosis, at least, was conspicuously absent from the operative record.

A month after the procedure, Maya’s menstrual cycles shortened from her typical 34‑day rhythm to a brisk 25‑day rhythm. It wasn’t just the timing, though. Each month she felt a dull ache at the base of her back that escalated into a sharp, shooting pain at the point of ovulation. The pain often spread to her left thigh and even, on a handful of mornings, to her lower back as if a phantom limb were being burned. She had always attributed this to fibromyalgia, an ever‑present companion that had been diagnosed in her late twenties following months of unexplained aches.

In the spring, a new prescription arrived: a combined oral contraceptive—estrogen 0.02 mg progestin 0.075 mg. She had never taken a daily pill before, having chosen natural cycles until that point. The hormone change was recommended by her OB‑GYN to regulate her cycles and ease the heavy bleeding she had been experiencing. Maya, optimistic but cautious, began the pack after reading up on potential side effects.

**Week 0 – The First Crisis**

The first week with the pill was almost normal. She enjoyed lighter periods and felt the back pain subside. But on the seventh day of her cycle, while sitting in a cafe with friends, a sudden, burning cramp erupted from her pelvis. The pain was sharp enough to make her clutch her seat. She tried hydrocodone for a day, but the agony grew into a relentless wave that pressed against her rectum, making her cough.

She drove herself to the ER in an hour. The triage nurse noted her distress and called for an abdominal X‑ray, a CT chest, and an ultrasound of the pelvis. Maya described her pain as “like a knife sweeping through her loins,” but she also added “I’ve always had this back pain, but this… this is different.” The radiologist’s CT scan showed only her pelvis intact—no obvious mass or free fluid. The ultrasound revealed the large fibroid cluster she had known since her early twenties but nothing that could explain the sudden, acute agony. She was given an antiemetic and scheduled for an urgent follow‑up.

**Week 1 – The Second Consultation**

Three days later, Maya returned to her OB‑GYN. She was sweating, her back aching, and she described a tightness that seemed to pulse with her heartbeat. The OB‑GYN, while sympathetic, felt the urgency. “Your symptoms sound a bit like an ovarian torsion,” she said. “Have you had a cyst? Did you notice swelling? Any nausea?” Maya shook her head. The OB‑GYN decided to order a serum CA‑125, a PSA for the pelvic floor muscles, and a repeat pelvic ultrasound.

The CA‑125 came back at 28 U/mL—slight but not definitive. The ultrasound again revealed a 4‑cm fibroid and an unchanged adenomyosis, without any free fluid or cystic lesions. The OB‑GYN then suggested a magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) study to look for deeper endometriotic implants.

**Week 2 – The Third Hospitalization**

Maya’s pain persisted. While going for a routine check‑up in the local urgent‑care center, a new set of symptoms emerged: sudden urinary frequency—she found herself in bathroom nine times in two hours—and a pressing desire to defecate, yet nothing came out. She also had a mild fever (100.4°F). The urgent‑care staff documented her pain as “sharp, localized just below the navel, radiating to the back, and worsening with bowel movements.” They called an internal medicine consult.

Dr. Patel, the internist, ran a basic metabolic panel, a complete blood count (CBC), and a urine pregnancy test. Everything was within normal limits. Dr. Patel considered interstitial cystitis (IC) as a differential—especially given Maya’s history of urinary frequency—but the lack of hematuria or infection markers made that less likely. He prescribed a trial of diltiazem and hydroxychloroquine for possible IC, and he ordered a urodynamic test.

**Week 3 – The Diagnostic Dilemma**

Maya’s MRI of the pelvis, ordered by her OB‑GYN, took a week to conduct and a week to read. The radiology report was bland: “Uterine fibroids, adenomyosis, incidental fluid collection within pouch of Douglas. No discrete mass lesions noted.” The radiologist suggested “possible endometriosis implants, but suboptimal for definitive diagnosis.” The radiologist’s note left Maya, and anyone else, with an ambiguous answer.

The urodynamic study showed overactive bladder but no detrusor instability. The urodynamic data, combined with her chronic urinary frequency, landed her on a treatment algorithm for overactive bladder: 12 mg tolterodine daily, an anticholinergic, plus behavioral changes—eating a low‑fat diet, scheduling bathroom visits, and doing pelvic floor stretching.

Meanwhile, Maya’s pain continued to flare with bowel movements, especially her IBS‑type loose stools in the evenings. She had always had some bloating, but this time it was relentless, accompanied by the sense that she was on the brink of a bowel obstruction. She tried to rest but only found her abdominal discomfort intensifying the more she sat or stood for long intervals.

**Week 4 – The Recurrent Crisis**

Late in her 30th day of her cycle, Maya awoke with a high pitched, roaring pain that seemed to erupt with each breath. She was unable to sit or lay down. The pain radiated into her right groin. She had the sense that her bowel was stuck, because every attempt to evacuate left her nauseated and with a severe cramp.

She rushed back to the ER. This time the physicians performed an abdominal CT scan with contrast. The scan revealed a small, self‑limiting obstruction within the sigmoid colon—likely a functional obstruction, nothing about that would indicate a specific underlying cause. The CT report also noted mild wall thickening of the uterus consistent with adenomyosis. There were no visible ovarian cysts, but a minimal amount of free fluid was identified in the cul‑de‑sac. All in all, the imaging remained nonspecific.

The ED physician recommended a high‑dose diclofenac and an antiemetic, and she was discharged with a prescription for a short course of prednisone (5 mg twice daily for a week) to rule out a steroid response. The OB‑GYN, on the other hand, suggested a repeat laparoscopy—but Maya was exhausted by the idea of more anesthesia and anesthesia itself, especially after her two prior surgeries, and was hesitant.

**Week 5 – The Persistent Undiagnosed Condition**

Maya’s visits to the pain clinic were the most exhausting. She reported “continuous dull ache” that seemed to ripple through her entire pelvis. The pain specialist prescribed low‑dose amitriptyline, titrating it upward, and recommended spinal manipulation. Maya tried it for a few weeks but found the manipulation uncomfortable because of her fibromyalgia, a sensation of being scraped across the skin all at once.

The chronic depression and anxiety that had plagued her for years made the pain feel even heavier. She had been on an SSRI for ten years but had never tried a different class. The neurologist was hesitant to change her regimen because of her history of urinary frequency, worried it might increase her urinary urgency. The patient expressed frustration—her pain seemed to dominate every day, and the physicians seemed to shift from one hypothesis to another, never closing the loop.

**Month Two – The Unfinished Story**

A month later, Maya was in a routine appointment with her OB‑GYN. She had lost five pounds over the last month, her appetite gone, her fatigue worsening. The OB‑GYN asked: “Do you still have the same cycles? Did anything change with the medicine?” Maya explained that she had been taking only 10 mg of estrogen progestin per week and that her estrogen levels were still high. The OB‑GYN recommended a pelvic MRI with contrast but hesitated to prescribe a new drug for her. The appointment ended with a plan to schedule a follow‑up in three weeks if her symptoms persisted.

At the three‑week mark, Maya did not improve. She had another flare, lasting 24 hours, requiring an overnight stay at the ED for intravenous pain management. That night, her pain was so intense she could only lay flat in the dark, her breathing shallow. When the doctor in the ICU questioned if she wanted a diagnosis that might guide therapy, Maya felt a wave of resignation. The conversation ended with a gentle reassurance that her condition, although not yet pinpointed, would be managed symptomatically.

**Month Three – Clinical Decision Points**

Maya’s journey over these three months now involved a multi‑disciplinary team: OB‑GYN, internist, pain specialist, urologist, neurologist, and mental health professionals. Each specialist had a hypothesis: endometriosis, fibroids, interstitial cystitis, IBS, functional GI motility, or a new pelvic floor dysfunction. Yet imaging findings were equivocal. Her labs were unremarkable. Her hormone profile was consistent with the oral contraceptive she was taking. The only thing that seemed to worsen was the cyclical nature of her pain coupled with bowel and urinary symptoms.

After a final meeting with the OB‑GYN, a decision was made to postpone a repeat laparoscopy—again, Maya was not ready to risk another surgical episode. Meanwhile, the OB‑GYN shifted Maya’s medication to a progesterone‑only injection every 12 weeks, thinking the estrogen might be driving inflammation. Unfortunately, the injection still contained a small amount of estrogen, and Maya’s cycles remained short. She also stopped the oral contraceptive, but the new regimen did not relieve her pain; her symptoms stayed the same.

**Month Four – The Continuing Dilemma**

Maya’s pain had become a routine part of her life, scheduled like a thunderstorm that arrived with each month’s start. She began to rely on high doses of opioids (oxycodone 15 mg prn), which unfortunately led to some sedation but did not significantly improve her quality of life. The pharmacist noted her dosage and recommended a taper plan, but Maya was reluctant to reduce. She said the pills were a lifeline.

On the third day of a flare this month, her pain became so severe that she had to call an ambulance. In the ED, her vital signs were stable, but she was trembling and tearful. The ED physician prescribed a 3‑day burst of hydrocortisone, combined with a high‑dose opioid. They also gave her an antispasmodic. The ED team sent her home with a prescription for 40 mg oxycodone and a plan to see her OB‑GYN in a week to adjust pain treatment.

**Month Five – Resignation and Hope**

By month five, Maya’s cycle had changed. The oral contraceptive was no longer in her system, and she had started a free‑running cycle that lasted 28 days, but her pain persisted. She had yet to get a definitive endometriosis diagnosis, and the story remained unresolved. She had grown accustomed to the pattern—pain, then pain, then relief for a few days, then pain again.

Despite the lack of diagnosis, Maya had learned to navigate her body’s unpredictable signals. She began a mindful meditation practice as suggested by her psychiatrist, aiming to reduce the anxiety that amplified her pain. She also started an exercise program focused on low‑impact aerobic work and gentle yoga, with the help of a physiotherapist who specialized in pelvic floor dysfunction.

**Conclusion – The Narrative of an Unresolved Acute Endometriosis Pain Crisis**

Within five months, Maya’s course exemplified a hard, atypical presentation of acute endometriosis pain crisis that remained undiagnosed due to overlapping comorbidities and subtle imaging findings. Her acute flares—sudden, severe, and cyclic—were interpreted in turn as ovarian torsion, bowel obstruction, interstitial cystitis, and IBS, each leading to different treatments, none of which addressed the underlying endometriosis. The risk factors—family history, early menarche, short cycle, hormonal therapy—fueled a cycle of misdiagnosis, as the clinicians focused on the most obvious explanations for her symptoms.

Maya’s narrative underscores the complexity of endometriosis when it presents acutely amid a backdrop of chronic pelvic pain, fibromyalgia, depression, anxiety, adenomyosis, fibroids, interstitial cystitis, and urinary urgency. It also highlights the challenges of diagnosing endometriosis when imaging is inconclusive, illustrating how a patient can navigate a maze of specialists, labs, and tests without ever receiving a clear label for her condition. The story ends with Maya practicing self‑management, hopeful for a better tomorrow but resigned to the unresolved mystery that continues to linger in her life.
