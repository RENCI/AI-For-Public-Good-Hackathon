**Acute Stone Episode: A 35‑Year‑Old Man with Metabolic Syndrome**

**Day 0 – Sudden Onset of Pain** In the late afternoon, Mark Thompson, a 35‑year‑old software engineer, woke at the office from a cramped lunch break. The first thing he noticed was a sharp, constant pain in the left flank that shot toward his groin. Within thirty minutes, nausea filled his nausea and he vomited once. Mark’s family history – his mother and an older brother both had kidney stones – flashed across his mind, but he had never thought his weight (BMI ≈ 33 kg/m²) and his penchant for salty, protein‑rich meals could predispose him that way. He called a rideshare and described the pain to the driver, asking to be taken to the nearest urgent‑care center.

**Day 0 – Urgent‑Care Evaluation** At the urgent‑care clinic, the nurse documented a 10‑on‑10 left‑sided flank pain, a temperature of 98.6 °F, and blood pressure of 160/95 mmHg. Mark admitted a two‑day history of limited fluid intake (roughly 1.5 L water in total), a high sodium lunch (≈ 3000 mg), and a protein‑rich dinner (≈ 100 g of protein). He had a history of metabolic syndrome – elevated fasting glucose (108 mg/dL) and triglycerides (180 mg/dL) – but never had formal diabetes care, and his hypertension medications had recently been uptitrated from amlodipine 5 mg to 10 mg due to elevated readings at his last PCP visit.

The nurse ordered a complete metabolic panel, a urinalysis (UA), and a non‑contrast CT scan of the abdomen and pelvis for diagnostic clarity. A bedside ultrasound was deferred because the clinical suspicion for an obstructing stone was high.

**Day 0 – Imaging and Diagnosis** The emergent non‑contrast CT scan revealed a solitary, 8‑mm calcific density in the left renal pelvis with mild left hydronephrosis. No surrounding fat stranding or perinephric fluid collections were present. The right kidney showed no abnormalities and the ureters were smooth and caliectatic. The imaging findings were classic for a sizable, obstructing renal pelvic stone.

**Day 0 – Initial Management** Physician‑assistant Dr. Patel reviewed the imaging and Mark’s vitals. He noted the stone’s size was beyond the likelihood of spontaneous passage (stoke 8 mm in the renal pelvis historically passes <10% spontaneously). He decided to proceed with an interdisciplinary approach: urgent urology consult, aggressive hydration, pain control, medical expulsive therapy, and blood‑pressure management.

- **Hydration**: A 0.9% saline infusion was started at 250 mL/h to raise urine volume and dilute stone fragments. Mr. Thompson was instructed to sip water and avoid further high‑salt meals. - **Analgesia**: An IV ketorolac 30 mg was given, followed by oral oxycodone‑acetaminophen 5/325 mg twice daily as needed, with a scheduled taper plan. - **Medical Expulsive Therapy (MET)**: Tamsulosin 0.4 mg orally once daily was started to relax ureteral smooth muscle. - **Hypertension**: Labetalol 100 mg IV bolus (repeat every 15 min up to 500 mg total) was given to bring systolic pressure below 140 mmHg before discharge. A short‑acting calcium channel blocker (nicardipine) infusion was considered but ultimately deferred due to satisfactory pressure control. - **Monitoring**: Urine output was recorded; urine pH was 6.2. The UA showed gross hematuria and microscopic red blood cells consistent with stone passage. No leukocyte esterase or nitrites; cultures were not pending.

Dr. Patel scheduled a follow‑up urology visit in 14 days and recommended a repeat non‑contrast CT to confirm stone passage.

**Day 3 – First Signs of Stone Passage** Mark began to feel a shift in pain intensity; the worst cramp was less intense, and he reported passing a pink, papery piece of tissue on the way to work that morning. He called the urology clinic to confirm the passage; a phone consultation confirmed his symptom was consistent with stone expulsion. The clinic advised him to use the same hydration regimen and to keep a diary of pain episodes.

**Day 7 – Symptom Resolution and Follow‑Up Imaging** Mark’s pain had almost completely resolved by the seventh day. He logged a final episode of sudden flank discomfort followed by a clear burst of urine with no hematuria. He was still taking the tamsulosin and had tapered the opioids. His blood pressure held between 120/70–130/80 mmHg on his current amlodipine 10 mg regimen.

**Day 10 – Routine Follow‑Up** At his routine 10‑day follow‑up with his PCP, Mark noted no residual pain, no flank tenderness, and that his weight had decreased by 3 kg after starting a modest exercise routine. Blood work showed a serum creatinine of 0.9 mg/dL, and his metabolic panel was unchanged. The PCP referred him to a dietitian and reinforced the importance of limiting sodium, moderating protein intake, and maintaining 2–3 L of fluid per day.

**Day 14 – Clearance and Resolution** The urology clinic confirmed Mark’s stone had passed. A repeat non‑contrast CT scan showed the left kidney free of calculi, no residual obstruction, and normal hydronephrosis resolution. Mark was instructed to maintain the same hydration and urine-pH‑manipulating strategy for a month to prevent recurrence, and to continue his blood‑pressure regimen. He was advised to keep a monthly fluid intake log.

**Retrospective Summary** From the first sharp cramp in the late afternoon to full symptom resolution 14 days later, Mark’s journey exemplified the textbook presentation of acute nephrolithiasis due to an 8‑mm renal pelvic stone in someone with metabolic syndrome and obesity. His pre‑existing risk factors — dehydration, high sodium and protein intake, and family history — set the stage for stone formation, but prompt imaging, aggressive hydration, appropriate analgesics, and tamsulosin facilitated a swift and uncomplicated passage. His blood‑pressure was managed effectively, underscoring the importance of addressing comorbid conditions parallel to stone workup. The case ended with a resolved stone, a clear imaging window, and an actionable plan to reduce future recurrence risk.
