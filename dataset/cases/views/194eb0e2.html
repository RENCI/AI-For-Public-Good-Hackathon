Maya, a 35‑year‑old woman with a long history of sickle cell disease (HbSS), found herself on a rapid, exhausting journey of pain, fear, and eventual relief. Her life outside the clinic had been a delicate balance: chronic anemia (hemoglobin 7.5 g/dL on baseline repeat labs), persistent renal insufficiency (creatinine 1.8 mg/dL, GFR ≈ 45 mL/min), a history of osteonecrosis of the femoral heads noted on a hip MRI a year ago, mild pulmonary hypertension (pulmonary systolic pressure 35 mmHg on echocardiography), a cerebral infarct in 2018 that left her with mild right‑side weakness but otherwise independent, and gallstones that had caused her a recent laparoscopic cholecystectomy three months earlier. She lived with these conditions like a seasoned runner: keeping track of dehydration, avoiding extremes of temperature, and staying diligent with her hydroxyurea (15 mg/kg daily) and folic acid.

---

### Day 0 – The Spark

The morning began in a small apartment in the Midwest. Maya had just finished a short flight from New York to Denver (approximately 1,500 m above sea level), a trip she’d taken for a conference on pain management. She felt the thinness of the air but dismissed it; after all, her baseline oxygen saturation on room air usually hovered around 94% at sea level. A quick cup of coffee left her slightly dehydrated, and she had been sipping only water through a large soda bottle on the plane, not realizing the thin cabin air accelerated sweat loss.

Later that afternoon, she noticed an unusual, dull ache in her left thigh—an area with a known history of osteonecrosis. It was mild, so she did not think much of it, but by evening, the pain erupted into a throbbing, whole‑body ache. She also felt a strange cold‑like chill in the room, although the HVAC was on low. Her blood pressure dipped to 100/60 mmHg, heart rate 95 beats/min. No fever yet, but her throat felt scratchy, and she remembered a recent urinary tract infection treated with a course of ciprofloxacin.

Maya's thoughts drifted to the risk factors that could be compounding her sickle cell crisis: the altitude, the dehydration, the ciprofloxacin (known to sometimes precipitate hemolysis in sickle cell), and even the mild cold from rapidly changing temperatures in the airplane cabin to an outdoor conference setting. She tried a few ibuprofen tablets, but the pain only intensified.

---

### Day 1 – Descent into the Crisis

Morning arrived with a slight fever (99.5 °F/37.5 °C). On waking, she noticed her toes were a pale blue, and there was a dark shadow under her left eye—classic micro‑perfusion changes to a sickle. She called her nurse practitioner; a prompt home visit revealed her pulse to be 108 beats/min, SpO₂ 89% on room air, a wet‑appearing nasal discharge, and a slightly elevated white count of 11 000 cells/µL. The nurse recommended IV hydration in an urgent care center; Maya accepted, hoping for an easy day.

At the urgent care, the nurse practitioner—her second‑line provider—performed a quick CBC. Hemoglobin was 6.8 g/dL, hematocrit 22%. She had elevated reticulocytes (9%) and bilirubin of 3.5 mg/dL, indicating a hemolytic picture. Blood urea nitrogen was 22 mg/dL, creatinine rose to 2.0 mg/dL (reflecting her chronic renal insufficiency). A point‑of‑care SpO₂ remained low at 88%. Physical exam revealed a tender left groin and right flank pain; the latter could be a sign of ureteral obstruction or a complication of her recent UTI.

Maya’s urgent care physician ordered a urine culture, a chest X‑ray, and a CT scan of the abdomen due to a lingering right flank discomfort. The chest X‑ray showed mild right basal atelectasis, and the CT revealed one large gallstone lodged in the cystic duct—a known residual problem. The urine culture returned positive for Escherichia coli, consistent with her recent UTI. Her pain was worsening: she reported 9/10 in multiple joints and a constant throbbing in her left femur.

The physician decided to transfer Maya to the hospital, anticipating a severe vaso‑occlusive crisis with risk of multi‑organ involvement.

---

### Day 2 – Hospitalization and Management

**Morning:** Maya arrived at the regional tertiary care center in a wheeze of shortness of breath. She was greeted by an interdisciplinary team: a hematologist, a nephrologist, a pulmonologist, and a pain medicine specialist. They intubated her oxygen mask; SpO₂ rose to 94% with 4 L/min O₂. The pain was now reported as 10/10. Her pain score spiked the pain medicine team’s alert.

**Pain Management:** The first step was aggressive IV hydration. Because of her chronic renal insufficiency, the nephrologist carefully matched fluids to avoid volume overload but ensured she received 1 L/hr of 0.9% saline. A high‑dose opioid regimen—morphine 8 mg IV every 4 hrs with an IV drip of 4 mg/h as a background infusion—was titrated to keep her pain below 5/10. Adjunctive acetaminophen 1 g IV QID was added. Because she has osteonecrosis and chronic opioid use, the team used a multimodal approach with NMDA antagonists (ketamine 0.5 mg/kg IV), gabapentin 300 mg PO BID, and a small dose of lidocaine patch for localized relief.

**Transfusion:** The hematology team evaluated the need for a transfusion. Maya’s blood pressure remained stable, but the hemoglobin remained low at 6.5 g/dL, and her oxygen saturation never fully recovered. The decision was to issue a partial exchange transfusion (PEP), aiming to lower her HbS concentration from approximately 80% to less than 30%. They sent two units of antigen‑matched blood, giving her a net hematocrit rise to 27% and hemoglobin 8.0 g/dL. This was performed under close monitoring for transfusion reactions, something her past history had taught them to anticipate.

**Infection Control:** The ID specialist started empiric IV ceftriaxone 1 g q12h and metronidazole 500 mg q6h for possible aspiration pneumonia. The culture grew Klebsiella pneumoniae sensitive to ceftriaxone. She was also admitted to the infectious disease ward for a 7‑day course for her UTI. A bladder ultrasound showed no hydronephrosis. Her urinalysis resolved with a marked decrease in leukocytes.

**Pulmonary Considerations:** The pulmonologist noted mild right basal atelectasis on the chest X‑ray, but no pulmonary emboli on a low–dose CT angiogram. A pulmonary function test confirmed a mild restriction, typical of her baseline pulmonary hypertension. Inhaled oxygen and a non‑invasive ventilation mask ensured the SpO₂ remained above 92%. No escalation to high‑flow nasal cannula was needed.

**Renal Monitoring:** Her nephrologist kept a strict eye on the creatinine, which decreased to 1.6 mg/dL within 48 hrs of fluid resuscitation. The team also instituted mannitol for renal perfusion and diuretics when needed.

**Neuro‑Monitoring:** After her past stroke, they monitored her for any new or worsening neurological symptoms. Her NIHSS score remained at 0. There were no new deficits.

**Mental Health & Support:** She had an anxious state—anxiety was a known comorbidity in a 35‑year‑old with chronic pain and was exacerbated by her hospitalization. The psychiatric team offered counseling and a low‑dose benzodiazepine for short‑term use. A social worker liaised with her insurance and family.

---

### Day 3 – The Turning Point

By Day 3, Maya's pain had reduced from 10/10 to 4/10. Her Hb had risen steadily to 9.2 g/dL. The oxygen saturation stayed above 95% on 2 L/min; the need for high‑flow oxygen went away. Her urinary symptoms resolved. The chest X‑ray cleared partially: the atelectasis was less obvious.

Her pain management shifted from a continuous infusion to scheduled doses of oxycodone 10 mg PO Q6h with acetaminophen. The gabapentin and ketamine were tapered. The partial exchange transfusion seemed to have mitigated the risk of recurrent crises by reducing sickled cells. Her fluid balance remained in slight negative, preventing edema while maintaining adequate renal perfusion.

However, a new challenge appeared: the day after the CT scan, Maya’s gallstone had lodged in the cystic duct. She complained of right upper quadrant pain and a sensation of fullness. The surgical consult recommended a scheduled laparoscopic cholecystectomy, but the team decided to wait until her pain crisis was fully resolved to avoid surgical stress—a classic example of weighing the benefits and risks in a patient with pulmonary hypertension and renal insufficiency.

---

### Day 4 – Gradual Weaning

Maya's physical exam on Day 4 showed improved pain thresholds; she could ambulate short distances without assistance, albeit with a mild limp left. Her urine output remained well above 1,500 mL/day, and creatinine had plateaued at 1.6 mg/dL. The nurse practitioner monitored vital signs: heart rate 86, BP 110/68, SpO₂ 97% on room air. She reported occasional mild chest discomfort, but an ECG demonstrated no new arrhythmias and a normal QTc.

Her pain regimen had narrowed to oxycodone/acetaminophen with an as‑needed ketamine infusion for breakthrough pain. The infusion was discontinued after 24 hrs. She also had daily physiotherapy sessions focusing on gentle range‑of‑motion for her osteonecrotic hips to maintain joint mobility and prevent flare-ups.

The ID team finished the 7‑day ceftriaxone course, and her inflammatory markers (CRP 2.5 mg/dL, ESR 30 mm/h) had trended toward normal. A final CBC on Day 5 revealed a stable hemoglobin of 9.0 g/dL. The hematology team continued hydroxyurea therapy, reinforcing the dosage.

---

### Day 6 – Discharge Planning

Having met the clinical criteria—pain score <5, stable hemoglobin and creatinine, no acute infection, pulmonary pressures within acceptable limits—the interdisciplinary team drafted a discharge plan. She received an outpatient follow‑up with hematology in 2 weeks and was encouraged to keep a pain diary. Her nephrologist recommended regular eGFR monitoring, fluid balance education, and avoiding NSAIDs unless medically necessary. The pulmonologist advised maintaining supplemental oxygen during high‑altitude activities such as camping, with a target SpO₂ > 90%.

The surgery board scheduled a laparoscopic cholecystectomy on Day 14, after confirming she was clinically stable enough for anesthesia, and planned for an analgesic regimen that minimized narcotics.

On the day of discharge, Maya was still mildly anxious but hopeful. She had seen the entire spectrum of acute crisis care—from the initial home visit to the complex inpatient multi‑specialty care. She had learned to identify early warning signs, to maintain hydration, and to seek prompt medical attention. Her oxygen sat stayed above 95% for the day, and she was given instructions on how to monitor her own SpO₂, when to call for help, and how to manage low‑grade pain with non‑opioid agents, before the outpatient oncology‑derived plan could guide her.

---

### One Week Post‑Discharge

In subsequent outpatient visits, Maya’s hemoglobin stabilized at 9.1 g/dL, her creatinine at 1.5 mg/dL, and her pain threshold remained high. The pain diary noted that her pain episodes were now less frequent and less intense. She continued with her hydroxyurea therapy and was scheduled for a cholecystectomy, which proceeded uneventfully. Her pulmonary hypertension remained controlled with low‑dose sildenafil. The patient’s overall quality of life improved measurably; she returned to full productivity at her job and scheduled a routine ophthalmologic exam to examine for retinal changes of sickle cell disease.

---

**The Takeaway**

Maya’s acute vaso‑occlusive crisis evolved over a week from an impulsive, multi‑factor precipitating set of events—high altitude, dehydration, infection, and medication—to an intensive inpatient recovery that involved fluid resuscitation, aggressive pain control, hemoglobin modulation via partial exchange transfusion, targeted antibiotic therapy, and interdisciplinary oversight. Her comorbidities—renal function, pulmonary hypertension, osteonecrosis, a history of stroke, and gallstones—required careful balancing, but with timely intervention, the crisis resolved. Maya emerged from this episode strengthened by insight into her disease, a more robust care plan, and a renewed sense of control. The story underscores that a typical crisis can be navigated successfully when risk factors are promptly recognized and managed within a cohesive, patient‑centered medical framework.
