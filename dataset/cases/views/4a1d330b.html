**March 12 – first signs of disquiet**

Ms. Eleanor Jones, a 78‑year‑old woman, was brought by her daughter to the primary care clinic after two days of fever (102 °F) and “a little trouble getting around.” The family noted that Ms. Jones, who has a long history of hypertension, type 2 diabetes, chronic kidney disease stage 3, and a previous TIA, had been feeling “off” recently. She complained of a “pressure” in her lower abdomen and the urge to go to the bathroom every two hours, often with cloudy, foul‑smelling urine. Her daughter—who manages much of Ms. Jones’ medication list—had added a new antihypertensive (amlodipine) and an anticholinergic decongestant 48 hours earlier due to a cold.

On examination, Ms. Jones appeared mildly diaphoretic, with a blood pressure of 158/96 mmHg, heart rate 96 bpm, and a slightly slow respiratory rate. A urinalysis showed leukocyte esterase, nitrites, and a large number of bacteria. Her serum electrolytes were slightly deranged: sodium 135 mEq/L, potassium 4.2 mEq/L, chloride 102 mEq/L, bicarbonate 24 mmol/L. Creatinine was 1.9 mg/dL, consistent with her baseline CKD. The doctor ordered a UTI work‑up: urine culture, CBC, and started intravenous ceftriaxone 1 g daily for 7 days, given her renal function.

Ms. Jones was scheduled to stay overnight for observation. The nurse noted, “She’s a little more scattered than usual—she can’t keep a straight conversation going, and she’s trying to get to the bathroom while at the bedside. Her daughter says she has been looking at her hands, not knowing if she’s the right patient for this ward.” The nurse recorded no delirium screening; she was relieved that the “confusion” seemed to be a temporary side effect of the new medications.

**March 13 – the night of confusion**

During the night shift, the patient became markedly disoriented. She repeatedly misidentified the nurse’s name, insisted she was “not a resident” but "the mother of the patient," and wandered into the hallway, holding the daughter’s phone so she could talk to a “faraway” landline. She also started talking to herself, describing “hidden doors” in the room. The nurse called the primary clinician for a rapid assessment. No formal delirium screening was performed; the clinician attributed the agitation to the ongoing UTI and the new medication changes.

An EEG and CT of the head were not obtained at this point. Instead, a quick finger test for blood glucose revealed a reading of 65 mg/dL, leading the team to attribute the confusion to possible hypoglycemia from her diabetes regimen. A sliding‑scale insulin was started, and her diet was adjusted. After several hours, Ms. Jones appeared mildly more alert but still had difficulty following instructions.

**March 14 – admission to the hospital**

Because of persistent fever and urinary symptoms, Ms. Jones was admitted to the internal medicine service. The care team reviewed her medication list: lisinopril, metformin (dose unadjusted for renal function), carvedilol, acetaminophen, a recent addition of fexofenadine (an anticholinergic), and now the newly started amlodipine. Her daughter recalled that Ms. Jones had previously been on antipsychotics for brief nighttime agitation after her stroke, but these had been stopped five years ago.

On day one of admission, the internal medicine resident started a delirium protocol: obtain a full metabolic panel and electrolytes, review medications, assess for potential infections. The labs were sent: sodium 134, potassium 4.7, creatinine 2.1, BUN 54. The urinalysis confirmed a high bacterial count. Blood cultures were taken, and the UTI antibiotic regimen was switched to cefepime 2 g IV every 12 hours, taking her reduced renal clearance into account.

During the nursing shift, Ms. Jones had an episode of “talking to imaginary people,” then settled into a quiet state for the next 4 hours. The nurse noted that she seemed “unusually quiet” afterward, almost as if she had a “deep sleep.” She stayed on cefepime, and her temperature decreased to 99.6 °F. Her daughter mentioned she had not felt very clear the previous day, and that her mother was “trying to pick up a picture of a house she once knew, but couldn’t quite find the right house.”

**March 15 – day two of admission**

With the UTI apparently improving, Ms. Jones’ confusion persisted. Her daughter reported that she had been “talking about being on a long boat that she couldn’t see.” She was often wandering around the ward in search of “a door she had been told to open.” The resident opted for a trial of haloperidol 0.5 mg IV q8h for agitation. The dose was increased to 1 mg IV q8h the following day after no improvement. The nurse noted that Ms. Jones seemed “more agitated” after the second dose, and her blood pressure climbed to 170/95. The resident discontinued haloperidol after a single dose due to concerns about orthostatic hypotension and potential QTc prolongation.

The internist consulted the geriatric team to review her medications. The geriatrician recommended stopping all anticholinergic medications (fexofenadine, antihistamines) and reducing the dose of carvedilol, pending a full medication reconciliation. The resident ordered a psychiatric consult to evaluate for possible dementia; the psychiatrist, however, considered the acute presentation too variable for a primary neurodegenerative diagnosis.

**March 16 – third day of admission**

Blood cultures returned negative; the UTI organism remained sensitive to cefepime. Her potassium was now 5.1 mEq/L, sodium 133 mEq/L, and creatinine had increased to 2.4 mg/dL, likely reflecting the cumulative effect of multiple IV antibiotics and dehydration. The internal medicine team corrected the electrolytes with 2 L of normal saline and started a low‑dose dose of a diuretic to manage fluid overload. They also increased the frequency of blood glucose monitoring given her persistent hypoglycemia episodes (two readings of 55 mg/dL overnight).

Despite these interventions, Ms. Jones remained significantly disoriented. She had trouble recognizing family members and occasionally fell to the floor when turned by a staff member. The nurse commented that she “looked very tired, like she had been at war.” The resident noted that no clear cause for her delirium was evident. The pharmacist, performing a medication reconciliation, discovered that she was on an anticholinergic burden totaling nine points per the Anticholinergic Cognitive Burden Scale.

Ms. Jones’ daughter expressed frustration, saying that she felt “the doctor had no idea what was going on.” The resident suggested a repeat head CT, as there was a possibility of a silent subdural hematoma after her previous TIA, but the family declined due to fear of anesthesia and transport in a febrile, confused patient.

**March 17 – decision to discharge**

On day four, Ms. Jones’ fever resolved: 98.7 °F. Her vitals stabilized on the lower end of her baseline. The internal medicine team felt the UTI was responding and that the patient’s kidneys were coping with the antibiotics. They decided to discharge her with a plan to follow up in the geriatrics clinic within 10 days and to continue cefepime until day 10 of her course. They prescribed oral metformin 500 mg bid (but warned the family about renal considerations) and instructed her to hold her antihypertensive and antihistamine until the next appointment.

The discharge summary highlighted the “ongoing confusion” but did not assign a definitive diagnosis. The family’s request to avoid “further investigation” due to the patient’s anxiety was noted. She was discharged home with a scheduled clinic visit on March 27.

**Post‑discharge – March 27 – geriatrics clinic**

At her first follow‑up, Ms. Jones still reported “being easily confused” and sometimes “mistaking her slippers for the wrong shoes.” A brief cognitive assessment using the Mini‑Cog revealed a “score of 3/5,” which the geriatrician noted but did not classify as dementia. Blood work again showed sodium 136 mEq/L, potassium 4.3, creatinine 2.3. There was no mention of brain imaging or further investigations for delirium. The plan was to adjust her anticholinergic meds further, monitor her electrolytes, and manage her blood pressure more aggressively.

**Outcome: undiagnosed**

Throughout the sequence, Ms. Jones’ acute confusion persisted despite reasonable interventions targeting infection, electrolytes, and medication burden. Although her delirium seemed to fluctuate, every consult or test returned either “no acute neurological insult” or “normal,” and no definitive diagnosis—be it delirium, a subacute vascular event, or early Alzheimer’s—was established. The patient continued with vague documentation of confusion, and her care team remained uncertain whether she was experiencing a reversible delirium precipitated by multiple risk factors or the first manifestation of a neurodegenerative process masked by her comorbidities. The story ends with Ms. Jones taking a daily walk with her daughter, still occasionally misplacing familiar objects, a lingering, unresolved cloud over her cognitive status.
