**A Year‑Long March of Pain: The Undiagnosed Story of Anna K.**

Anna K., 45, spends most of her days as a sales‑floor manager at a local department store. Her routine is a relentless cycle of standing, walking, and occasionally sprinting to the back‑office supply room. She has carried a “plummy” figure all her life—she weighs 215 pounds, 5’6”, giving her a BMI of 34. Her tongue‑labeled comorbidities, managed but not cured, lie in the background: Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus, kept on metformin, and osteoarthritis of both knees that gnaws her during long shifts.

For several months, Anna kept the story of her pain to herself. She told herself the aches were simply the price of a hectic life and a new, more ambitious workout routine she had started a month ago. She had begun a 5‑day “HIIT for beginners” program at the local gym, adding daily 30‑minute jogs to her schedule. She bought a pair of glossy, low‑profile running shoes—brightly colored—at a discount store because they looked “trendy” and they were inexpensive. The sole was thin, the arch support minimal, and the heel cushioning barely there.

---

### **Weeks 1–2: A First-Step Worry**

Anna’s heel pain began after an early morning walk around the office parking lot. Each step felt like a small, sharp jolt. She laughed it off: “That’s just my feet getting used to the new shoes.” By the end of the day she’d had a single 10‑minute stretch session of the calves and the plantar fascia. She slept on a mattress that had lost a lot of its spring, and the next morning the pain persisted, especially when she took the first steps after her bed had been cold.

She tried over‑the‑counter ibuprofen, a dab of menthol ointment, and a hot‑water bottle for the heel. The pain was worse at night, a subtle throbbing that forced her to rest her feet up on a pillow. She told her coworkers she’d taken a short break, but she continued to stand all day.

---

### **Month 1: The Early Visits**

By the end of the month, the heel pain had migrated from “sharp” to a dull, aching pressure that spread into the arch. She began to notice that her walking gait had subtly changed: her heel hit first, then her foot rolled inward—a tell‑tale sign of overpronation. Her knees also began to feel more sore after a full day of duty.

Anna visited her primary care physician (PCP) with “ankle and foot pain” on an appointment slot that was booked for a routine check‑up. The doctor asked, “Anything affecting your knees?” and, after a brief physical exam, suggested that her knee osteoarthritis might be causing referred pain into the foot. The recommendation was simple: “Rest, ice, and ibuprofen as needed.” He ordered an HbA1c test to check her diabetes control but did not explore foot-specific pathology. Anna left the office still uncertain if it was “just my knees” or something more specific to her feet.

---

### **Month 2–3: The Misattributions Continue**

Undeterred, Anna began a basic stretching routine at home—standing calf stretches, hamstring pulls, and a gentle ankle roll. The pain continued. She attempted to buy an arch‑supporting shoe at a nearby shoe store. The sales clerk suggested a generic “comfort” line; Anna, wary of spending extra on what she thought of as a “comfort” issue, paid the $50 for a brand‑new pair of slippers. They were thin and offered little arch support.

A month later she went to urgent care during a foot‑pain flare‑up. The nurse described a “heel spur suspicion” and suggested an X‑ray of the foot. The doctor, with a stack of test reports, made a short comment: “Heel spurs are common in your age group with high BMI. Continue physical therapy; this will settle down.” He didn’t ask about the rest of her foot biomechanics or her increased exercise routine. Anna left with a prescription for naproxen and a “light” physiotherapy referral, which she declined because of time constraints.

---

### **Month 4–5: The First Physiotherapy and Orthotics Misstep**

Anna finally saw a physiotherapist after another week of relentless pain that made it impossible to finish her shift. The PT performed a quick assessment, noted limited dorsiflexion, and recommended an Achilles tendon stretch and a foot‑roll routine. She suggested an orthotic insert from the local shoe store, claiming it would “prevent overpronation.” Anna purchased a generic foam insert, slipped them into her ill‑fitting running shoes, but the arch remained unsupported and the heels still made hard contact with the ground on each step.

The pain had intensified to the extent that she started using a cane on her way from aisle to aisle to reduce the load on her heel. Yet the cane merely transferred weight to her knees in an awkward way, worsening her osteo‑arthritic pain. Her foot swelling increased from time to time; she noticed small bruises in the heel area, possibly blood pooling under the skin due to inadequate arch support.

---

### **Month 6–7: A Radiographic Misnomer**

Anna’s PCP ordered foot X‑rays after a week of unrelenting pain in the heel. The radiologist flagged a “calcaneal spur” as a potential cause. The office physician, following the report, explained that the spur was “not likely to be the real problem.” A foot specialist, Dr. Ramirez, was referred. He performed a simple physical exam, pressed on the heel, and, noting tenderness, concluded that “there was no fracture.” He recommended rest and light stretching. He did not address the arch issue or ask her about the specific pain pattern during walking.

Within a month, the pain had spread to the metatarsals, pressing against Anna’s forefoot. The pain when she stood was no longer just “the heel”; it was “the entire foot” that felt like her shoes were glued to her skin. Despite the foot specialist's reassurance of no fractures, Anna’s life was spiraling: she was missing shifts, her diabetes test results spiked due to poor nutritional intake, and her knee pain worsened from overcompensation.

---

### **Month 8–9: The Diabetes & Neuropathy Angle**

When her feet began to feel numb in between the balls of her feet—a new symptom—Anna was pushed toward a neurologist. She was told her diabetes could cause peripheral neuropathy, and that “the pain might be neuropathic.” A test panel for blood sugar control confirmed modestly elevated glucose levels (HbA1c 7.8%). She was given gabapentin at a low dose for neuropathic pain. The medication gave her a faint, diffuse numbness that alleviated some discomfort but was entirely unrelated to her heel pain. She kept missing appointments because her foot pain remained constant, and she had not yet connected the dots about chronic plantar fasciitis.

---

### **Month 10: A Frustrated, Yet Unidentified Patient**

At her next annual check‑up with the PCP, Anna was still in sharp, constant pain. Her doctor, perhaps exhausted by the cycle of complaints, summarized the diagnosis as “foot/ankle pain secondary to diabetes and poor footwear.” He wrote a letter to her podiatrist: “Patient is dealing with chronic plantar pain, consider advanced imaging if pain persists.” However, after the visit, the podiatrist’s office staff forwarded the letter to a different department, and Anna never followed up.

During this same month, Anna's glucose level flared further (HbA1c 8.4%), her weight increased by 3 pounds, and her knee pain returned with an intensity that forced her to change her usual route to the office’s supply room—slowly, with deliberate footfalls. Each time she touched the kitchen, she felt a dull, pulsing ache in the heel that persisted for minutes, fading only when she rested for a long pause.

---

### **Month 11–12: The Chronic Pain Stage**

A year after the first painful step, Anna’s condition had reached a “maintenance” phase. She used over-the-counter NSAIDs daily, though occasionally she became wary of liver side effects. She had a new, cheaper “comfort” shoe line, still lacking arch support. Physical therapy appointments were sporadic owing to scheduling conflicts. She avoided the gym altogether because the routine runs felt cruel.

Her foot pain never resolved—she still woke up each morning with that same throbbing in the heel, the pain radiating along the arch with each step. She had not heard the term “plantar fasciitis” yet; her only references were to "heel spurs", "ankle pain", "diabetic neuropathy", and "painful feet" spoken by various providers. A diagnostic label had eluded her—and it seemed this would remain the case.

---

### **Looking Back: Where Did the System Fail?**

Throughout this narrative, Anna’s journey reflects a tapestry of risk factors: obesity increasing mechanical load; diabetes potentially altering tissue healing; osteoarthritis making her cautious and limiting exercise; a high‑impact exercise routine that pushed her feet into repetitive stress; overpronation that stretched the plantar fascia excessively; poorly supportive footwear that offered no mitigation. Each healthcare encounter focused on a single facet—knees, spurs, diabetes—while neglecting the cumulative effect on her feet. The result: chronic plantar fasciitis, persistent, unmanaged, and clinically undiagnosed as such, persisted for a year and continues to haunt Anna in the silence of a misnamed disease.
