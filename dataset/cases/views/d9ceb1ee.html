**Prostate Adenocarcinoma With Extraprostatic Extension (T3a): A Clinical Narrative**

Mr. James Carter, a 68‑year‑old retired construction supervisor, had long regarded his health as “good enough.” He was obese (BMI 30.2 kg/m²), a lifelong smoker who had cut down to a pack a day just two years earlier, and he liked “grilled meats on the weekends” more often than he did fresh produce. While he exercised at a local gym two times a week, his diet remained high‑fat and low‑in‑fruit.

---

### **1. Initial Symptoms (Month 0)** - **Symptoms:** Over the preceding three months, James noticed an increasingly weak urinary stream, a frequent “urge” to urinate, and night‑time trips to the bathroom for a total of 4–5 nights per week. He also complained of a dull sensation in the lower abdomen that often resolved after urination but began to feel increasingly persistent. - **Self‑management:** He took over‑the‑counter alpha‑blockers (tamsulosin 0.4 mg nightly) with mild improvement but still experienced nocturia and a weak stream. - **Lifestyle context:** He attributed the symptoms to “the old age and the big weight,” so he delayed visiting a clinician.

---

### **2. Primary Care Visit (Month 1)** - **History & Exam:** James scheduled a visit with Dr. Patel at his family practice. The exam revealed an enlarged, non‑tender prostate on digital rectal exam, a common finding in benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH). - **Laboratory work:** PSAVs were ordered due to the urinary symptoms. Blood tests showed HbA1c 7.6 % (reflecting his type 2 diabetes), serum creatinine 1.1 mg/dL, and a total cholesterol of 210 mg/dL. A PSA of 16 ng/mL raised suspicion for prostate pathology. - **Decision Point:** Dr. Patel referred James for a transrectal ultrasound‑guided prostate biopsy.

---

### **3. Biopsy and Pathology (Month 2)** - **Procedure:** A 12‑core transrectal ultrasound guided biopsy was completed in the office. James tolerated the procedure well, and the pathology report identified adenocarcinoma involving 3 cores, Gleason score 3+4 = 7. - **PSA‑Nadir:** Serial PSA values rose from 16 to 20 ng/mL over the next two months. - **Referral:** Dr. Patel instructed James to see urology for staging. Urologist Dr. Lopez visited him in a two‑day window in March after the biopsy.

---

### **4. Urologic Staging (Month 3)** - **Imaging:** A multiparametric MRI of the pelvis showed a 2.2 cm tumor on the left side extending beyond the prostatic capsule into the neurovascular bundle, consistent with T3a disease (extraprostatic extension). There was no evidence of seminal vesicle invasion or distant metastasis. Whole‑body bone scan remained negative. - **Evaluation of Comorbidities:** Dr. Lopez noted James’s uncontrolled hypertension (average BP 158/92 mmHg) and a diabetic foot ulcer on the left foot. He also recognized the smoking history as a risk for cancer progression and cardiovascular disease. - **Treatment Counseling:** James’s options were discussed: radical prostatectomy, external beam radiation therapy (EBRT) with short‑course ADT, or radical prostatectomy with adjuvant ADT. James’s age, obesity, BPH-related discomfort, and comorbidities favored a non‑surgical approach for the short term. The patient expressed a strong desire to avoid a prolonged hospitalization due to his diabetes management concerns.

---

### **5. Initiation of Androgen Deprivation Therapy (Month 4)** - **ADT Plan:** James was started on a 4‑month course of leuprolide acetate 11.25 mg intramuscular injection (Long‑Acting GnRH agonist) with an anticipated PSA decline. - **Side Effects:** Within the first month, James experienced hot flashes, mild erectile dysfunction, and a drop in libido. He also reported an increased appetite and marginal weight gain of ~4 kg. - **Blood Glucose Fluctuations:** His HbA1c rose to 8.1 % by the end of month 5, requiring a temporary uptick in metformin dosage and a consultation with his endocrinologist. - **Hypertension Management:** He was started on a low‑dose ARB (losartan 25 mg daily) to assist with blood pressure control, which improved systolic numbers to 140/85 mmHg by month 6.

---

### **6. First Radiation Therapy Planning (Month 6–7)** - **Plan:** With the ADT response deemed adequate, James proceeded to radical external beam radiation therapy—70 Gy in 35 fractions over seven weeks—delivered at a community oncology center. - **Complications Post‑Radiation:** After the third week of EBRT, James developed a mild urinary frequency and a new pain at the bladder base. The oncologist suspected early post‑radiation cystitis. Low‑dose bladder irrigation and a temporary increase in tamsulosin helped, but the urinary symptoms persisted. - **Bone Scan:** At the end of month 7, a bone scan revealed a focal area of increased uptake in the left sacral ala, suggesting early bone involvement.

---

### **7. Symptom Progression and Intervention (Month 9)** - **Pain and Mobility:** James began reporting a dull ache in his left hip and occasional nocturnal pain that interfered with sleep. His weight had increased to 215 lb, and he noted fatigue that limited his gym attendance. - **Bone‑Targeted Therapy:** The oncologist initiated zoledronic acid 4 mg IV infusion to address bone lesions and reduce skeletal‑related events. - **Evaluation of BPH:** Because urinary obstruction worsened, Dr. Lopez recommended a transurethral resection of the prostate (TURP). James opted for the procedure, hoping symptomatic relief would allow better quality of life.

---

### **8. TURP and Post‑Op Course (Month 10)** - **Procedure:** TURP went smoothly. Post‑op, James experienced transient hematuria but returned to baseline urination strength. - **Complications:** Six weeks after TURP, he developed a urinary tract infection with *E. coli* and required a 7‑day course of ciprofloxacin. His PSA rose modestly to 18.3 ng/mL but remained stable relative to month 9.

---

### **9. Disease Progression (Month 12)** - **New Symptoms:** The pain intensified, now radiating to his left lower back and groin. He began experiencing occasional shortness of breath after walking a few steps without rest. In addition, James reported a new “dull” headache that he attributed to blood pressure fluctuations. - **Further Imaging:** Repeat MRI revealed a 3.1 cm intraprostatic tumor that now involved the seminal vesicle base, and CT of the chest/abdomen/pelvis showed small bilateral pulmonary nodules suspicious for metastatic disease. Bone scan now displayed multiple hot spots in the spine and pelvis, consistent with bone metastases. - **Biopsy of Pulmonary Nodules:** A CT‑guided thoracoscopic biopsy of a left lung nodule confirmed metastatic adenocarcinoma with the same Gleason pattern. - **Treatment Shift:** Dr. Lopez transitioned James to full‑dose androgen deprivation therapy plus a second‑line hormonal agent (enzalutamide 150 mg daily). The goal was to control systemic disease while stabilizing bone pain.

---

### **10. Deterioration and Palliative Care (Months 13–15)** - **Pain Management:** Despite zoledronic acid, James’s bone pain remained pronounced. Bone‑pain protocols were intensified: higher‑dose morphine PCA and intermittent ketamine infusions were started. - **Functional Decline:** James’s general fatigue worsened, impacting his ability to ambulate. The oncologist referred him for hospice at home after a multidisciplinary meeting. - **Outcome:** James’s PSA escalated to 45 ng/mL within two months, his creatinine rose to 1.5 mg/dL due to dehydration, and his metabolic state deteriorated. His respiratory symptoms progressed, culminating in brief episodes of hypoxia on exertion. He eventually was admitted to a hospice unit, where he passed away peacefully a few weeks later, surrounded by family.

---

## **Key Clinical Highlights**

- **Comorbidities Influence Management:** BPH necessitated TURP despite active cancer treatment; hypertension and diabetes dictated careful selection and adjustment of systemic therapies. - **Risk Factors Contribute to Aggressive Course:** Obesity, high‑fat diet, and smoking likely accelerated tumor aggressiveness, resulting in early extraprostatic extension (T3a) and metastasis. - **Treatment Sequence and Side‑Effect Burden:** ADT induced endocrine changes that exacerbated diabetes control; radiation induced early cystitis; zoledronic acid managed bone disease but failed to halt progression. - **Timeline of Clinical Decisions:** - **Months 0–1:** PSA elevation → biopsy referral. - **Months 2–3:** Biopsy confirms adenocarcinoma → MRI staging. - **Month 4:** Initiation of leuprolide ADT. - **Month 6–7:** EBRT (70 Gy). - **Month 9:** Bone scan showing mets → zoledronic acid. - **Month 10:** TURP for BPH. - **Month 12:** Pulmonary nodules → metastatic biopsy → change to enzalutamide. - **Month 13–15:** Palliative transition, hospice care.

This narrative encapsulates a typical yet aggressive course of advanced prostate cancer influenced by significant comorbidities and risk factors, culminating in a rapidly worsening disease trajectory and poor outcome.
