At the end of a quiet summer afternoon, 68‑year‑old Mr K. was left alone at home to recover from a sore throat that had been worsening for a week. He lived alone, was on a complex drug list that included levodopa/carbidopa (Sinemet™), pramipexole, donepezil, lisinopril, metformin, and daily multivitamins. He was mildly obese (BMI 30), had type 2 diabetes (HbA1c 7.3% a month ago), hypertension, and a three‑year history of Parkinson’s disease manifesting with resting tremor and rigidity that had been steadily escalating over the last two years.

### Day 0–Day 2 – Initial Onset **Day 0 (Monday)** – Mr K’s daughter, Mrs S., noticed a fever of 38.9 °C, a productive cough with yellow sputum, and that he seemed “dull and foggy.” She left him at home. He complained of difficulty swallowing and a vague “tightness” in his chest.

**Day 1 (Tuesday)** – At 8 p.m., his daughter heard a low‑volume cough and noticed that he was staring blankly; his pupils were mid‑dilated. He was still on his scheduled levodopa dose of 12 pm that day but had taken his usual morning dose of pramipexole 3 mg.

Mrs S. called the primary care office. The nurse practitioner (NP) asked about his home medications. The NP noted that Mr K had inadvertently dropped his usual dose of levodopa two days earlier because the bottle had been misplaced, and that he had not taken pramipexole for the last 48 hours because he forgot. She advised re‑establishing his dopaminergic regimen but urged the daughter to bring him to urgent care if his confusion worsened.

**Day 2 (Wednesday)** – At 11 a.m., Mr K was brought to the urgent‑care department. He was febrile (39.4 °C), tachycardic 110 bpm, and had a respiratory rate of 22/min. Oxygen saturation on room air was 92%. Neurologically, he was alert but clouded, unable to articulate a full sentence, and showed mild tremor. His gait was unstable.

The clinical team diagnosed community‑acquired pneumonia (CAP) – likely aspiration given the swallowing difficulty. Broad‑spectrum antibiotics (IV ceftriaxone 2 g daily + azithromycin 500 mg daily) were started after obtaining blood cultures and a chest X‑ray that showed left lower lobe infiltrates. Mr K was given a parenteral levodopa (Sinemet® IV 500 mg) to compensate for the missed oral doses. His labs revealed:

- Sodium 133 mmol/L (low) - Potassium 3.8 mmol/L - Creatinine 1.2 mg/dL (baseline 1.0) - Glucose 250 mg/dL - CRP 14 mg/dL - Hb 14.5 g/dL

The team reviewed his medication list. The NP noted that he had been taking over‑the‑counter diphenhydramine 25 mg nightly for insomnia, an anticholinergic that can exacerbate Parkinsonian symptoms. She discontinued it. The patient’s home levodopa was re‑initiated at 100 mg po TID starting the same afternoon.

### Day 3–Day 4 – Early Hospital Course **Day 3 (Thursday)** – The patient was admitted to the medical floor for pneumonia management. He was given IV fluids (0.9% saline 1 L IV push + maintenance 150 mL/hr) to treat the hyponatremia. His levodopa dose was increased to 150 mg po TID because his clinical status remained “sluggish” and tremor persisted.

At 2 p.m., he developed a brief episode of agitation, shouting incoherent words, and appeared “very anxious.” The team administered diazepam 10 mg IV for presumed delirium. His confusion resolved shortly after. The clinical decision point: the bedside nurse queried the cardiology team about adding an antipsychotic for possible hallucinations. The cardiologist recommended low‑dose quetiapine (5 mg at bedtime) because of his risk of orthostatic hypotension, but noted the anticholinergic burden.

**Day 4 (Friday)** – Mr K’s mental status deteriorated again. He was now increasingly rigid, with severe tremor in both hands and stiffness of the forearms to the point that he could barely lift his arms when his daughter came to the bedside. His temperature spiked to 40.5 °C. Pulse was 120, BP dropped to 98/54 (orthostatic). His pupils remained mid‑dilated. He was still febrile and had a persistent productive cough.

The floor team consulted Neurology. A neurologist evaluated him, noting the sudden worsening of rigidity, bradykinesia, hypophonia, and autonomic instability (tachycardia, hypotension, hyperthermia). In light of the recent antipsychotic exposure, anticholinergic use, and infection, the neurologist suspected a Parkinsonian crisis syndrome.

The neurology team decided to institute a continuous IV levodopa infusion (Sinemet® 200 mg/24 h via a central line), increase the oral levodopa to 150 mg po TID, and add a low‑dose clonazepam 0.25 mg PO TID to manage rigidity and agitation. They discontinued quetiapine. Metformin was held due to concern for lactic acidosis secondary to hypoperfusion.

The endocrinology team was called because glucose remained in the 220–260 mg/dL range. They started insulin sliding‑scale regimen. Electrolytes were monitored hourly; sodium gradually trended upward to 136 mmol/L.

### Day 5–Day 7 – Escalating Crisis **Day 5 (Saturday)** – Despite the infusion, Mr K remained rigid and unresponsive. He had been unable to speak beyond a few hoarse words. His vital signs fluctuated: temperature 39–40 °C, BP 90/50–95/55, heart rate 110–120. The ICU team was consulted. He was transferred to the intermediate‑care unit. Mechanical ventilation was not yet initiated; the staff attempted to sedate him with propofol 10 mg/h, but he still exhibited excessive rigidity (“lead‑pipe” rigidity) as if the infusion was insufficient.

The ICU team started a benzodiazepine infusion (midazolam 0.1 mg/kg/h) and an anticholinergic antagonist (physostigmine 2 mg IV push) to counteract dystonia. The neurologist noted that the patient’s autonomic instability persisted: sweating was profuse (diaphoresis), temperature remained high. Blood cultures came back positive for Streptococcus pneumoniae. Antibiotics were switched to IV vancomycin plus cefepime 2 g q8h.

Metabolic panel showed a rising creatinine to 1.7 mg/dL, hyperglycaemia (280–310 mg/dL), and a mild hyperkalemia of 5.2 mmol/L. They continued insulin regimen and adjusted fluids to maintain a negative fluid balance. They initiated empiric steroids (hydrocortisone 100 mg IV q8h) for possible adrenal insufficiency given the profound stress and autonomic symptoms.

**Day 6 (Sunday)** – Mr K’s level of consciousness remained at a Glasgow Coma Scale of 10 (E3 V4 M3). His temperature remained >40 °C. The ICU team considered neuromuscular blockade (pulses of pancuronium 0.1 mg/kg IV) due to severe generalized rigidity that was impairing ventilation mechanics. After consultation with anesthesia, they elected to proceed with a short‑term neuromuscular blockade to allow adequate ventilation and to facilitate further management.

Simultaneously, the nephrology team noted the patient’s rising creatinine and discussed initiating a renal replacement therapy due to fluid overload and electrolyte derangements. However, the neurologist cautioned that the patient’s renal dysfunction might be part of the crisis.

**Day 7 (Monday)** – Despite aggressive management—continuous levodopa infusion, escalating benzodiazepines, steroids, antibiotics, diuretics, and neuromuscular blockade—Mr K’s neurologic status remained refractory. Blood pressure fluctuations were worsening (BP 70/40 during wakeful periods, 112/70 during sedation). The ICU team added a dopamine‑based vasopressor (nor‑epinephrine) to maintain MAP ≥65 mmHg.

In a formal team meeting, the following decisions were made:

1. Increase the levodopa infusion to 300 mg/24 h, supplemented with levodopa/benserazide 100 mg po TID when he could tolerate oral intake. 2. Add a short‑acting anticholinergic antidote (physostigmine) as needed for dystonic fits. 3. Switch the sedative regimen to a propofol infusion to achieve sedation level RASS –4 with minimal hemodynamic impact. 4. Discontinue vancomycin and start meropenem 2 g q8h, considering MRSA coverage not yet needed. 5. Continue hydrocortisone at 80 mg q6h. 6. Arrange for continuous renal replacement therapy (CRRT) for hyperkalemia and fluid overload.

### Day 8–Day 10 – The Crisis Hits Full Force **Day 8 (Tuesday)** – Mr K’s temperature rose to 41.2 °C. His serum sodium plateaued at 138 mmol/L, potassium at 5.5 mmol/L. The neurologist noted that his rigidity had become so severe that he could not even turn his head to the side. The physical examination revealed a rigid trunk, tremor in both hands, and a “pseudohypoesthesia” (difficulty swallowing). The ICU team started an anti‑hyperthermia protocol (cooling blankets, water‑cooled ventilator). Despite aggressive cooling, temperature remained around 40.5 °C.

Blood lactate trended upward (2.8–3.5 mmol/L), but central venous oxygen saturation (ScvO2) remained within normal limits (68%). There was no evidence of secondary organ failure. He had an arterial line; the BP remained unstable.

The team considered the possibility of a neuroleptic malignant–like syndrome, so they initiated a sodium bicarbonate drip (1 g/kg) to elevate end‑organ perfusion. Meanwhile, the nephrology team began CRRT to correct mild hyperkalemia and address fluid overload.

**Day 9 (Wednesday)** – Mr K’s neurologic exam had not improved. He had a Glasgow Coma Scale of 6 (E2 V2 M2). He remained sedated but had occasional “myoclonic” jerks of his limbs. The neurology consult suggested adding amantadine 100 mg PO QAM to attempt to modulate dopaminergic activity. However, the team felt the patient was too unstable to add oral medications.

The ICU team elected to increase the nor‑epinephrine infusion to 0.2 mcg/kg/min to keep MAP around 75 mmHg, and added phenylephrine 0.5 mcg/kg/min for better arterial perfusion. The patient’s temperature remained high; the cooling blankets were ineffective, and no further anti‑hyperthermic measures were attempted.

**Day 10 (Thursday)** – The patient was still deeply rigid, febrile, and on a high‑dose sedative and vasopressor regimen. CRRT was providing mild improvement in fluid balance, but creatinine remained elevated at 1.9 mg/dL. The decision was made to switch from CRRT to continuous arteriovenous hemofiltration due to equipment constraints.

The team considered a tracheostomy for long‑term ventilation, but the family had not yet been fully engaged in the decision‑making process. The neurology team noted that the patient had entered a phase of “exacerbated rigidity” with neuroleptic‑like features, and that the prognosis was poor.

### Day 11–Day 13 – Decline Continues **Day 11 (Friday)** – The patient’s temperature remained at 40.3 °C. His blood pressure had fallen again to 72/48 despite vasopressors. The ICU team instituted a third‑party blood product transfusion for a drop in hemoglobin to 8.2 g/dL. The patient had developed a new onset of arrhythmia—a ventricular premature complex (VPC) pattern. The cardiology consult was called, and amiodarone 150 mg IV was initiated. He then developed a rash on his chest, suggesting possible drug‑induced photosensitivity.

Neuroimaging was considered; an emergent head CT revealed no mass effect or infarction—just a diffuse low‑density brain parenchyma, consistent with cerebral edema secondary to hyperthermia.

Given the escalating crisis, the ICU team elected to wean the ventilator to a high‑frequency oscillatory mode to facilitate gas exchange while maintaining low tidal volumes that might mitigate the hemodynamic strain of conventional ventilation.

**Day 12 (Saturday)** – After a prolonged day, the patient’s metabolic workup revealed a rising bicarbonate (from 20 to 24 mmol/L), a slightly improving lactate (3.2 mmol/L) but a persistently elevated glucose (>300 mg/dL). His creatinine plateaued at 2.0 mg/dL. The family was informed of the poor prognosis that continued despite aggressive care.

In an attempt to reset his dopaminergic tone, the neonatology team considered trialing a high‑dose levodopa infusion (600 mg/24 h) combined with a slow‑release dopamine agonist via continuous intravenous infusion; the neurologist declined as they feared additional adverse effects.

**Day 13 (Sunday)** – The patient’s condition continued to deteriorate. He had lost his ability to speak entirely. His rigidity had progressed to involve all four limbs with a “paralytic” rigidity. Blood pressure had dropped to 60/40; vasopressor requirements had increased again. The ICU team decided to limit further escalation of rescue therapies, focusing instead on comfort care. The family requested a brief family discussion about potential withdrawal from aggressive measures and considered palliative care consult.

### Day 14 – Outcome **Day 14 (Monday)** – With the family’s consent, the ICU team discontinued all non‑essential drug infusions (levodopa, vasopressors, benzodiazepines), transitioned to low‑dose morphine and a low‑dose midazolam infusion for comfort. They placed the patient on a humidified oxygen mask at 2 L/min. The patient’s temperature gradually came down to 38.6 °C over the next several hours. His pupils remained mid‑dilated; he was still unresponsive. The family decided to escort him home for hospice care.

Within 24 hours of hospice admission, Mr K’s heart rate dropped precipitously to 55 bpm, and then became variable. Family reported that he had had a brief episode of “seeing lights” before becoming unresponsive. An electrocardiogram showed a new onset atrial flutter with a rapid ventricular response (150 bpm). They could not resuscitate (DNR) due to his earlier decision.

Mr K passed away peacefully on Day 16 of hospitalization, a week after the onset of community‑acquired pneumonia. The sequence of events underscores how withdrawal of essential levodopa, anticholinergic exposure, infection, metabolic derangements, and comorbid hypertension and diabetes can culminate in a fulminant Parkinsonian crisis syndrome, with rapid progression from mild rigidity to catastrophic autonomic failure, refractory hyperthermia, and ultimately death.
