When 70‑year‑old Martin K. first told his daughter that he’d been “feeling a bit off” it sounded like a minor flare of his long‑standing ulcerative colitis. He’s had pancolitis since his early 40s and has worked as a book‑keeper, but for the past six months he had been noticing subtle changes: his stools were darker than usual, sometimes tinged with a faint reddish hue, and he lost about 10 pounds without trying. He also described a gnawing pressure in the lower right abdomen and a fatigue that made his usual brisk walks feel like running uphill. He didn’t view these complaints as anything beyond the “age‑related aches” that come with obesity and hypertension.

The first visit (Day 0) was with his primary care physician (PCP). Routine labs revealed a mild normocytic anemia (Hgb 11.4 g/dL), borderline hyperglycemia (HbA1c 7.2 %), and an elevated fecal calprotectin at 300 µg/g, a known marker of active colonic inflammation. His PCP suspected a UC flare and prescribed a short course of prednisone and a tapering plan for mesalamine. He was also advised to schedule a colonoscopy, citing the new-onset GI bleeding, which had never occurred before.

Colonoscopic evaluation (Day 28) was technically straightforward given his prior endoscopies. The scope revealed a 4 cm polypoid lesion in the medial aspect of the cecum, partially covered in inflammatory pseudopolyps. The surface eroded slightly, and it raised a suspicion of neoplasia. Five biopsies were obtained, and the pathologist later reported a moderate‑grade tubular adenocarcinoma, T2 (invading the muscularis propria) with negative margins in the superficial biopsies. At this point, Martin understood that he was dealing with a colorectal cancer, not just UC flare. The diagnosis spurred a rapid cascade of investigations.

An abdominal computed tomography (CT) scan with contrast (Day 35) confirmed the endoscopic findings: a localized mass in the cecum, with no regional lymphadenopathy and no distant metastatic lesions. The gastroenterologist determined that Martin’s tumor was appropriately a Stage II (pT2N0M0) cancer. He was referred to a surgical oncologist, and a multidisciplinary tumor board reviewed his case. Given his comorbidities—type 2 diabetes with peripheral neuropathy, well‑controlled hypertension (BP 135/80 under lisinopril), obesity (BMI 33.5), and a 30‑pack‑year smoking history—there was concern about operative risk, especially in the setting of uncontrolled inflammation from UC. The board decided on a planned elective right hemicolectomy, but only after a short period of preoperative optimization of his diabetes and a smoking cessation counseling session.

Preoperative optimization (Days 36–46) included adjustment of his metformin dose and initiation of a low‑dose basal insulin. He was prescribed a high‑protein, low‑fat diet to combat his weight loss. His family history—his father succumbed to colorectal cancer at 68—also made the team uneasy; they felt urgent intervention was warranted.

The right hemicolectomy (Day 48) was performed laparoscopically, with the surgical team employing a protective ileostomy due to Martin’s high intraoperative risk profile. Postoperatively, Martin’s recovery was protracted. On Post‑Op day 3, he developed a superficial surgical site infection (SSI) that required percutaneous drainage on day 5 and a brief course of vancomycin. The ileostomy output was large, and his metabolic demands increased, leading to hyperglycemia that peaked at 250 mg/dL on day 7. He also reported a worsening of abdominal pain, localized to the right lower quadrant, which the surgical team thought was typical ileitis; anti‑inflammatory therapy was increased. By day 10, Martin was still febrile (38.4°C) and had a leukocytosis of 12.5 × 10⁹/L. A repeat ultrasound ruled out abscess, and an infectious disease consult determined that the SSI had likely seeded into the peritoneal cavity—though the peritoneal cultures grew no organism. This warranted a longer course of intravenous antibiotics.

His ICU stay lasted 4 days, during which he was weaned off ventilatory support. By postoperative day 14, he was ambulating, but his appetite remained poor. The oncology team reviewed his pathology: 4 mm tumor budding, low differentiation, microsatellite-stable tumor, and a tumor lysis syndrome‑like picture on labs. They concluded that adjuvant chemotherapy was not indicated for Stage II if there were no adverse features. However, because of his high risk factors (age, obesity, UC), the team opted for a 4‑cycle adjuvant capecitabine to be initiated when he could tolerate oral intake.

Martin left the hospital on day 18 with wound care instructions and an appointment in 2 weeks with his surgeon and oncology nurse for follow‑up. The ileostomy was planned to be reversed within 8 weeks post‑surgery, contingent upon wound healing and metabolic stabilization.

The next clinical milestone (Day 35 after discharge, approx. Day 53 from initial symptoms) marked Martin’s first post‑discharge visit. His wound was healing, but his stoma output remained excessive. At the oncology visit, he reported increasing fatigue, new-onset diarrhea, and a sensation of “tightness” in his abdomen. On physical exam, there was a palpable firm, indurated mass in the right lower quadrant that moved with defecation. His labs were now deranged: hemoglobin had dropped to 9.7 g/dL, and his CRP was 18 mg/L. A CT scan showed a recurrence of the cecal lesion that had now invaded the adjacent terminal ileum, with an enlarged, suspicious para‑mesenteric node. There was no distant metastasis at that point.

On an urgent tumor board meeting (Day 64), the consensus was that Martin’s cancer had progressed to Stage III (pT3N1M0). Because of his frailty, comorbidities, and postoperative complications, a full adjuvant chemotherapy regimen (FOLFOX) was deemed intolerable. The surgical team recommended a palliative right hemicolectomy with resection of the involved terminal ileum and an end‑to‑end anastomosis, but this carried a high peritoneal seeding risk. Instead, they suggested a definitive chemoradiation, albeit with the knowledge that the benefit in colorectal cancer is limited without nodal involvement.

Martin and his daughter debated the options. He declined aggressive surgery, citing his recent complications and poor physical resilience. Instead, the oncology team initiated a modest palliative chemotherapy with capecitabine and bevacizumab, combined with supportive care for pain and nutrition. His appetite remained poor, and he experienced intermittent severe crampy abdominal pains. Over the next two months (Days 65–115), his condition steadily deteriorated. His hemoglobin fell to 8.3 g/dL, requiring two packed RBC transfusions. He developed worsening dyspnea and intermittent lower‑extremity edema, attributed to the progression of local disease and his chronic heart disease.

On the 120th day after the initial presentation, Martin’s condition had worsened to a point where the oncologist felt it appropriate to shift the focus from disease‑directed therapy to comfort measures. Hospice care was initiated. He returned to his home, with his daughter by his side, as the disease progressed. He passed away a few weeks later (Day 150 from initial symptoms), unable to cope with the cumulative burden of colorectal recurrence, uncontrolled diabetes, lingering hyperinflammatory UC flare, and the lingering scarring of his previous surgical sites.

Throughout this trajectory, Martin’s family history, lifestyle factors, and the dual pressures of chronic inflammatory bowel disease and metabolic derangements contributed to a course of colorectal cancer that went from a local Stage II tumor to an aggressive recurrence that overwhelmed his ability to tolerate aggressive therapy. The narrative underscores how a seemingly “curable” early colorectal cancer can still follow a grim arc when comorbidities, advanced age, and a pro‑inflammatory backdrop coexist.
